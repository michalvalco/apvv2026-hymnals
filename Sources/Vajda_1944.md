# Vajda, Jaroslav — A History of the Cithara Sanctorum (Tranoscius) (1944)

## Citation
VAJDA, Jaroslav: *A History of the Cithara Sanctorum (Tranoscius): The Slovak Lutheran Hymnal of George Tranovsky.* B.Div. Thesis, Concordia Seminary, St. Louis, May 1944. Available: https://scholar.csl.edu/bdiv/110

## Summary
A B.Div. thesis (100 pp., scanned OCR) providing the first comprehensive English-language historical study of the Cithara Sanctorum and its compiler, Jiří Třanovský/George Tranovsky (1591–1637). Written at Concordia Seminary from a confessional Lutheran perspective, drawing primarily on the Slovak scholarship of John Mocko (*Historia posviitnoj piesne slovenskej*, 1909; *Život Juru Tranovského*, n.d.), Ludevit Haan (*Cithara Sanctorum... jej historia*, 1875), John P. Ďurovič (in *Tranovského Sborník*, 1936), and Branislav Varsík.

The thesis is structured in six chapters: (I) Historical Background (the Reformation in Hungary/Slovakia, Counter-Reformation, and the Thirty Years' War); (II) Slovak Hymnody before Tranovsky (Hussite, Brethren, and Calixtine hymnals used in Slovakia; the Bystrická agenda; pre-Tranovsky Slovak hymn writers); (III) Biographical Sketch of Tranovsky (birth in Tešín 1591, education in Guben, Kolberg, Wittenberg; pastoral work in Holešov and Medziríčie; exile to Bielsko, Orava Castle, and finally Sv. Mikuláš in Liptov); (IV) The First Edition of the Cithara Sanctorum (1636) — division, contents, foreword, instructions for cantors, original hymns and translations; (V) Subsequent Editions; (VI) The Cithara Sanctorum Today and Tomorrow.

## Key Arguments

### On pre-Tranovsky hymnals in Slovakia (Chapter II):
- Scholarly disagreement between Haan (who asserts Slovaks used Hussite/Brethren hymnals) and Mocko (who says only Calixtine hymnals were used). Ďurovič settles the question: both Czech Brethren and Calixtine hymnals were in use, alongside Lutheran ones. (pp. 12–14)
- The Bystrická agenda (1585), a manuscript supplement to the Czech Agenda of 1581, is the "oldest known ms. of Slovak hymns before Tranovsky, many of which have found their way into the Tranoscius." Vajda provides a detailed list of 36 hymns from the BA, tracing each to its CS equivalent and earlier sources — with specific hymn numbers and page references. (pp. 16–21)
- Of these 36 hymns, "sixteen (if the antiphons are included) are of Slovak Lutheran origin. Most of the remainder are of Hussite or later Reformation origin." This is "the treasure from which Tranovsky gleaned." (p. 20)

### On the composition and sources of the first edition (Chapter IV):
- The first edition (1636, Levoča, Vavrinec Brewer) contained 402 hymns (Mocko notes 414, because Tranovsky counted some composite hymns as single units), plus 42 vesper hymns. Numbering: 700 numbered pages + XXXII unnumbered preliminary pages + 36 unnumbered final pages. (pp. 55–56)
- **Division of the hymnal:** Part One = hymns for the festival church year (Advent through Trinity); Part Two = *officia* and hymns for the non-festival Sundays, marriage hymns, and funeral hymns; Part Three = hymns of the Holy Christian Church, general hymns, hymns on the four last things. A supplement of Psalms follows. (pp. 59–68)
- **Tranovsky's editorial principles** (from his Foreword): (a) all hymns must "harmonise with the Word of God"; (b) he translated all of Luther's hymns into Czech, finding only four pre-existing Slovak translations; (c) longer hymns were retained "because they are cherished by many pious people for their antiquity"; (d) he corrected theological errors and ensured that words matched melodies syllabically; (e) alterations were necessitated by the chaotic diversity of existing manuscript editions — "such a chaos must exist until there is a single well-edited and ordered hymnal which is generally accepted and used"; (f) phrases that were "clear misstatements of scriptural expression" were corrected. (pp. 57–58)
- Tranovsky included "more than 150 hymns (and translations)" of his own, all anonymously. (p. 58)

### On the confessional character of the CS:
- Tranovsky's foreword makes the confessional stakes explicit: "the Church should be concerned that its singing be as pure and as pious as the Word and Sacraments from which it is derived." An out-of-tune hymnal "offends the theological conscience." (p. 54)
- Despite this scrupulousness, Mocko notes that "two Calvinistic errors taken over from Czech hymnals were overlooked" in the first edition — suggesting the Czech source material carried confessional content that Tranovsky's Lutheran filter did not fully catch. (p. 54)
- The *Literárny spolek* (literary society) constitution quoted by Vajda from Staroj stipulates that "hymns contrary to [the Augsburg Confession, Luther's Catechisms, and the Apology] are not to be sung at all, nor introduced into the church." (p. 29)
- The Cantor's instructions specify a liturgical order closely following the Lutheran Deutsche Messe pattern: Introit → Kyrie → Gloria → Epistle hymn → Gospel → Credo (or hymn) → Sermon → Benediction hymn. (pp. 69–72)

### On Tranovsky's relationship to Czech Reformation sources:
- Tranovsky's publication of the CS was prompted partly by the fact that "after the Synod of Zilina, Slovak Lutheranism was not satisfied with Czech hymnody growing out of the compromising theology of a Confession based on the Czech Confession of 1575." This is a direct statement that the CS represents a *confessional correction* of Czech Reformation hymnody — replacing theologically ambiguous material with material aligned to the Augsburg Confession. (p. 54)
- The detailed hymn list in pp. 74–84 attributes each hymn to its source: (T) = Tranovsky original; Luther, Ambrosius, Decius, Helmbold, and various Czech/Bohemian sources. This provides a starting point for identifying hymn pairs for the §5 transfer analysis.

### On subsequent editions (Chapter V):
- By 1944, the CS had gone through at least 65 (possibly 67) editions in three centuries. (p. 91)
- The 1653 edition (edited after Tranovsky's death) was the first major expansion, incorporating hymns from Pribis's Catechism (1634) and other sources.
- Jesuit censorship affected the 1768 edition: a section "Of the Opposition and Temptations to the Church" had to be removed, along with 12 hymns. Replacement hymns were inserted but not in alphabetical order. (p. 86)
- The 1896 Mocko edition is described as "the most critical ed. to date." (p. 90)
- American editions (Akron 1918, Pittsburgh 1928) omitted rarely-used hymns while maintaining numbering compatibility with European editions. (p. 91)

## Notable Quotes
> "...many pastors, together with me, believe that the Church should be concerned that its singing be as pure and as pious as the Word and Sacraments from which it is derived" (Tranovsky's Foreword, translated by Vajda, p. 54)
- Context: Tranovsky's own statement of the *lex orandi, lex credendi* principle — the theological content of hymns must align with doctrine. This is the founding statement of the editorial policy that drove the "Lutheranisation" process our chapter analyses.

> "...after the Synod of Zilina, Slovak Lutheranism was not satisfied with Czech hymnody growing out of the compromising theology of a Confession based on the Czech Confession of 1575" (Vajda summarising Mocko, p. 54)
- Context: Directly supports the thesis that CS represents a deliberate confessional correction — not merely a translation exercise but a theological filtering of Czech Reformation material through the lens of the Augsburg Confession.

## Relation to TEPALIT Chapter
- **Which locus/section:** §5 (Transfer and Reception) — primary; §2 (Confessional Context) — supporting; §4.1 (Eucharist) — indirectly, through the Cantor's instructions and liturgical order
- **How it supports our argument:** Vajda provides (a) the standard English-language historical narrative of the CS's creation and development; (b) Tranovsky's own stated editorial principles, which explicitly invoke the *lex orandi, lex credendi* connection; (c) evidence that the CS was conceived as a *confessional correction* of theologically ambiguous Czech hymnody; (d) a detailed hymn-by-hymn attribution list (pp. 74–84) identifying sources, which can serve as a starting point for identifying specific Czech → Lutheran textual modifications in §5; (e) the connection between the Bystrická agenda (1585) and the CS (36 hymns traced from BA → CS).
- **Points of agreement:** Strongly supports the thesis that the CS involved systematic theological filtering of Czech sources.
- **Points of disagreement or tension:** Vajda writes from a confessional Lutheran perspective (Concordia Seminary, 1944) and tends to frame the CS heroically as a bulwark against Calvinism, rationalism, and Catholicism. His treatment lacks critical distance from the Lutheran self-narrative. He does not engage with the Unity of Brethren's own theological self-understanding, treating their hymns primarily as raw material for Lutheran appropriation. Our chapter should provide a more balanced assessment that takes the Czech Reformation traditions seriously on their own terms.
- **Methodological limitation:** Vajda relies heavily on Mocko (1909), Haan (1875), and the *Tranovského Sborník* (1936). The OCR quality of the scanned thesis introduces reading difficulties. Primary source verification against the originals cited is still required.
