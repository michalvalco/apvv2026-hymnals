# Eucharistic Rite Analysis: Agenda Česká 1581

## Source
Benka 2024 (MLS X), ed. pp. 131–155. "Pořádek Msse weliké / kterýžby při posluhowánij Welebné Swátosti Wečeře Páně se zachowáwati měl." (Order of the Great Mass, to be observed when administering the Venerable Sacrament of the Lord's Supper.)

## Liturgical Structure

The rite follows this order (ed. page refs in brackets):

1. **Confiteor** — General Confession [pp. 131–132]
2. **Absolution** [p. 132]
3. **Introit / Kyrie** (sung by Žiáci/choir) [p. 132]
4. **Gloria** — "Sláwa buď na wýsostech Bohu" [pp. 132–133]
5. **Salutation** — "Hospodin buď s wámi" [p. 133]
6. **Collect** [p. 134]
7. **Epistle reading** [p. 134]
8. **Alleluia / hymn** [p. 134]
9. **Gospel reading** [p. 134]
10. **Credo** — "Wěřijm w gednoho Boha" / Patrem (Czech creed hymn) [pp. 134–135]
11. **Sermon** [pp. 136–137]
12. **General Prayer** (two forms provided: short and long) [pp. 137–139]
13. **Preface** — Sursum Corda / Vere dignum [pp. 140–141]
14. **Sanctus** (Czech paraphrase) [pp. 141–142]
15. **Admonition to communicants** (Napomenutí) [pp. 142–144]
16. **Lord's Prayer** (sung) [p. 144]
17. **Communion hymn** (Žiáci) [p. 145]
18. **Verba Institutionis** — Words of Institution (sung by priest) [pp. 145–147]
19. **Distribution** — sub utraque specie [p. 147]
20. **Communion hymns** (during distribution) [pp. 147–148]
21. **Thanksgiving** (two forms provided) [pp. 149–150]
22. **Aaronic Benediction** [p. 150]
23. **Closing hymn** [p. 150]

**Then separately:** Pořádek Msse kdyby Communicantůw nebylo (Order of Mass when there are no communicants) [pp. 150–153]

## Theological Analysis of the Eucharistic Formulations

### I. Terminology: "Msse" and "Swátost Wečeře Páně"

The rite's title is revealing: "Pořádek Msse weliké / kterýžby při posluhowánij Welebné Swátosti Wečeře Páně se zachowáwati měl" — "Order of the Great Mass, to be observed when administering the Venerable Sacrament of the Lord's Supper."

Two terminological registers coexist. The traditional Catholic term "Msse" (Mass) is retained for the liturgical framework, while the evangelical designation "Wečeře Páně" (Lord's Supper) names the sacramental action itself. This is conservative Lutheran practice — Luther's *Formula Missae* (1523) retained the term "Mass" while stripping the rite of sacrificial content. The Agenda Česká follows this pattern exactly. The retention of "Msse" signals liturgical continuity; the specification "Wečeře Páně" signals confessional reinterpretation.

Note further: the sacrament is called "Welebná Swátost" (Venerable Sacrament) throughout — language of high reverence, but without specifying what constitutes the sacrament's "venerability." Catholic and Lutheran piety use this language, but with different theological content.

### II. The Admonition (Napomenutí) — The Theological Heart of the Rite [pp. 142–144]

This is the most theologically revealing section. It is a substantial prose address to the communicants, placed BETWEEN the Sanctus and the Words of Institution — i.e., the congregation receives theological instruction about what the sacrament IS before the consecration is performed. This is a distinctly Protestant catechetical move: understanding precedes reception.

**Key formulations and their theological significance:**

**(a) "swé Tělo ku pokrmu / a swau Krew k nápogi dáti ráčil"**
"[Christ] deigned to give us his Body for food, and his Blood for drink."

This is simple identificatory language: Body = food, Blood = drink. It identifies what Christ gives, but does not specify HOW the body and blood relate to the bread and wine. Compatible with real presence, spiritual presence, or even a strong memorial view.

**(b) "abychom tijm wijry nassij posylňowali / a lásku vtwrzowali"**
"so that we might thereby strengthen our faith and fortify our love."

The PURPOSE of the sacrament is functional: strengthening faith and love. This is standard Reformation theology across all three traditions. But the absence of any further ontological specification is notable — there is no "and thereby receive the true body of Christ under the bread" or any such amplification.

**(c) The christological gloss on "This is my body":**
The admonition quotes the Words of Institution within a narrative framework and then GLOSSES them:

"Wezměte a gezte / to gest Tělo mé / kteréž se za wás dáwá / To gest / že gsem Cžlowěkem včiněn / a cožkoliw činijm a trpijm / to gest wssecko wasse dědičné / a pro wasse lepssij se děge. Toho k gistotnému dokázanij a swědomij dáwám wám Tělo mé za pokrm."

"Take and eat, this is my Body, which is given for you — That is: that I have been made man, and whatever I do and suffer, all of it is your inheritance, and is done for your benefit. For certain proof and testimony of this, I give you my Body for food."

**This gloss is theologically decisive.** The words "this is my body" are interpreted not in terms of the MODE of presence (transubstantiation, consubstantiation) but in terms of FUNCTION: the body is given "k gistotnému dokázanij a swědomij" — "for certain proof and testimony." The sacrament is understood as a SEAL or PLEDGE (an assurance-mechanism) of the salvific work accomplished in the incarnation and passion. The body given in the supper is the same body that was "made man" and that "suffered" — the identification is christological (body of the incarnate, crucified Christ) but the mode of giving is described functionally (as proof/testimony), not metaphysically.

The identical formula applies to the cup: "Toho na gisté dokázanij a swědomij / dáwám wám mau Krew k nápogi" — "For sure proof and testimony of this, I give you my Blood for drink."

**Comparative significance:** This "testimonial" or "assurance" language is closer to Calvin's understanding of the sacrament as a seal of the divine promise than to the strict Lutheran insistence on the real, substantial bodily presence. It is also remarkably compatible with the mature Unity of Brethren position (post-Lukáš Pražský), where the sacrament is a "spiritual" means of grace that strengthens faith through the Holy Spirit's work, not through a metaphysical transformation of the elements. HOWEVER — it does NOT contradict Lutheran real presence. A Lutheran could read this gloss as describing the PURPOSE of the real presence without denying the presence itself.

This is precisely the kind of irenic register that allows a liturgical text to function across confessional boundaries.


**(d) The definition of worthy reception [p. 143]:**
"Kdož giž tak z toho Cleba gij / a z Kalicha pige / a těm slowům / kteráž od Krysta slyssij / a tomu nařijzenij / kteréž od Krysta přigijmá / sylně wěřij / tent' zůstáwá w Pánu Krystu a Krystus w něm / a wěčně žiw bude."

"Whoever eats of this bread and drinks of this cup, and firmly believes the words which he hears from Christ and the institution which he receives from Christ — he remains in Christ the Lord and Christ in him, and shall live forever."

Worthy reception requires three things: (1) eating the bread and drinking the cup, (2) hearing the words of Christ, and (3) firmly believing (sylně wěřij) the words and institution. The criterion is FAITH IN THE WORDS — not faith in a specific doctrine about the mode of presence. A Lutheran who believes in real presence and a Calvinist who believes in spiritual presence could both "firmly believe the words" — they would simply understand them differently.

**This is the critical absence.** FC VII (Solida Declaratio) defines worthy reception in terms of believing specifically that Christ's body and blood are truly, substantially present in the elements — and defines UNWORTHY reception (manducatio impiorum) as receiving the real body and blood without faith, to one's condemnation. The Agenda Česká defines worthy reception in terms of believing Christ's words and institution GENERALLY, without specifying what content that belief must have regarding the elements.

**(e) The sacrament as directed to "poor and troubled consciences":**
"ta přeswatá Swátost gest k obzwlásstnijmu potěssenij a posylněnij dána / bijdným a zarmauceným swědomijm: kteřijž swé hřijchy wyznáwagij / Božijho hněwu a smrti se bogij / a po sprawedlnosti lačněgij a žijžněgij."

"This most holy Sacrament is given for special consolation and strengthening to poor and troubled consciences: to those who confess their sins, fear God's wrath and death, and hunger and thirst after righteousness."

This is pure Lutheran pastoral theology — the sacrament as *consolatio conscientiae*, comfort for troubled consciences. This formulation is directly Lutherian and could come straight from Luther's catechetical writings. It is NOT characteristic of Utraquist sacramental theology (which emphasised the sacrament's sanctifying/transformative power) or of the early Unity (which emphasised communal discipline and ethical commitment). The pastoral-consolatory accent is the most distinctively Lutheran feature of the entire rite.

### III. The Words of Institution — Formal Sung Form [pp. 145–147]

The Words of Institution appear TWICE in the rite:
- First, within the admonition, as narrative/catechetical exposition (pp. 142–143)
- Second, as the formal sung consecration (pp. 145–146)

The sung form follows the standard composite Pauline-Synoptic formula:

**Bread:** "Pán náss Gežijss Krystus w tu noc / w kterauž gest byl zrazowán / wzal Chléb / a Dijky činiw lámal / Včedlnijkům swým dal / a řekl: Wezměte a gezte / tot' Tělo mé / kteréž se za wás wydáwá. To čiňte na mé připomijnánij."

**Cup:** "Též wzal gest y Kalich / když gest odwečeřel / Dijky činil / a dal gim řka: Wezměte a pijte z toho wssyckni / Tento Kalich gest nowá vmluwa w mé Krwi / kteráž Za wás y za mnohé wylita bude / na odpusstěnij hřijchůw / To čiňte kolikrát koli pijti budete / na mau památku."

Key observations:
- "tot' Tělo mé" — "this is [= this-is] my Body." Simple identification. No qualifying adjectives ("true," "substantial").
- "Tento Kalich gest nowá vmluwa w mé Krwi" — "This Cup is the new covenant in my Blood." The cup is identified with the COVENANT, not directly with the blood. This is the Pauline formula (1 Cor 11:25), which is more covenantal-functional than the Synoptic "this is my blood of the covenant" which more directly identifies cup-contents with blood.
- "Za wás y za mnohé" — "for you and for many." Standard formula.
- "na mau památku" — "in my remembrance/memorial." The anamnesis is present — this is what gives the rite its memorial dimension.

### IV. The Distribution Formula [p. 147]

**Bread:** "Wezmi a gez / to gest Tělo Krystowo / kteréž za tebe wydané gest."
"Take and eat; this is the Body of Christ, which was given for you."

**Cup:** "Wezmi a pij / tata gest Krew nowé vmluwy / kteráž wylyta gest / na odpusstěnij hřijchůw twých."
"Take and drink; this is the Blood of the new covenant, which was shed for the forgiveness of your sins."

The distribution formula uses "Tělo Krystowo" (Body of Christ) and "Krew nowé vmluwy" (Blood of the new covenant). Again: no metaphysical qualifiers. The formula declares what is given but not the mechanism by which it is present.

**Rubrical note on remainders [p. 147]:** "co té prwnij částky Wečeře Páně po giných zbylo / to ať nikterakž neschowá k ginému času" — "whatever remains of the first part of the Lord's Supper after others [have received], he shall in no way save for another time."

This prohibition of reservation is significant: it implicitly denies that the elements retain sacramental status outside the liturgical action. This is consistent with Lutheran teaching (consecration depends on the use/action, not on an ontological change in the elements) and is INCOMPATIBLE with Catholic reservation-for-adoration practice. But the Agenda makes the prohibition as a practical rubric, not as a doctrinal statement.


### V. The Thanksgiving Prayers [pp. 149–150]

Two forms are provided. The difference between them is theologically consequential.

**Form 1 (shorter, p. 149):**
"Dijky wzdáwáme twé Swaté milosti wssemohúcý Pane Bože / že gsy nás skrze tyto spasytedlné dary posylniti ráčil"
"We give thanks to your Holy Grace, Almighty Lord God, that you have deigned to strengthen us through these salvific gifts."

Language: "tyto spasytedlné dary" — "these salvific gifts." Generic sacramental language. The gifts "strengthen" (posylniti). No identification of WHAT the gifts are or contain.

**Form 2 (longer, pp. 149–150):**
"že gsy skrze Syna swého milého / tu welebnau Swátost vstanowiti ráčil / w nijžto Tělo geho Swaté gijme / a Krew geho Swatau pigeme"
"that through your beloved Son you have deigned to establish this venerable Sacrament, in which we eat his Holy Body and drink his Holy Blood."

This is THE MOST EXPLICIT PRESENCE-LANGUAGE in the entire rite. But attend to the syntax: "w nijžto Tělo geho Swaté gijme" — "in which [= in the Sacrament] we eat his Holy Body." The body and blood are located IN THE SACRAMENTAL ACTION (w nijžto = in which), not identified WITH the material elements of bread and wine. This is real-presence-compatible language, but it is also spiritual-presence-compatible: the body is eaten "in" or "through" the sacramental event, not necessarily "in, with, and under" the bread (which is the distinctive Lutheran formulation from FC VII).

The two forms thus offer a spectrum: Form 1 is maximally generic ("salvific gifts"), Form 2 specifies that what is eaten and drunk is Christ's body and blood — but locates this eating/drinking within the sacramental action rather than in the elements themselves.

### VI. The Order of Mass without Communicants [pp. 150–153]

The existence of this order carries two theological implications:

**First,** it assumes that the Word-service (readings, creed, sermon, prayer) is complete and valid without communion distribution — the preaching of the Word is a means of grace in itself. This is standard Lutheran ecclesiology.

**Second,** the extended admonition to non-communicants (pp. 152–153) is a rebuke of eucharistic negligence. Key formulation:

"žádný se paknenassel / kterýž by toho pokladu přehogného / a naydražssijho hledal" — "no one came forward who would seek this most abundant and precious treasure."

The Eucharist is called "poklad přehogný a naydražssij" — "most abundant and precious treasure." Yet the rebuke frames the neglect not as a sin against the sacrament's ontological holiness but as a sign of spiritual torpor: "kde Srdce w poznánij hřijchůw / w rozgijmánij hněwu Božijho / a wzýwánij gména geho Swatého vstydlo" — "where the heart has grown cold in recognising sin, in contemplating God's wrath, and in calling upon his holy name."

In other words: neglecting communion is a symptom of faith gone cold — it's a pastoral and spiritual problem, not a ritual offense. This is distinctly Lutheran-pastoral framing.

## VII. Overall Theological Assessment: What the Lex Orandi Reveals

### The central finding

The Agenda Česká's communion rite operates in a **deliberately broad theological register** that is identifiably Lutheran in structure and pastoral accent, but avoids the polemically sharpened doctrinal formulations that would exclude non-Lutheran interpretations of the Eucharist. The rite says enough to be recognisably evangelical (no sacrificial Mass language, communion sub utraque, centrality of the Word, pastoral-consolatory purpose). But it systematically avoids saying the things that would enforce specifically *Lutheran* Eucharistic doctrine against either Reformed/spiritual-presence or moderate Catholic/Utraquist positions.

### What is present (Lutheran markers):

1. **Structure** follows the Deutsche Messe / Formula Missae pattern
2. **Vernacular throughout**, justified explicitly from 1 Cor 14 (p. 131)
3. **Communion sub utraque specie** — both bread and cup distributed to all communicants
4. **No sacrificial language** — the Mass is not presented as an offering to God; Christ's sacrifice is past-tense (on the cross), not present-tense (at the altar)
5. **No elevation** of the elements
6. **No reservation** of elements after the service (explicit prohibition, p. 147)
7. **Pastoral-consolatory purpose** — the sacrament directed to "poor and troubled consciences" (p. 142)
8. **Catechetical admonition** placed before consecration — understanding precedes reception
9. **Self-examination required** before reception, per 1 Cor 11 (p. 142)

### What is absent (Lutheran doctrinal specifics NOT stated):

1. **No "in, with, and under"** — the distinctive Lutheran formula for real presence (FC VII) is nowhere used
2. **No explicit real-presence teaching** — "Tělo" and "Krew" are named in the Words of Institution and distribution formula, but the MODE of their presence in/with/under the bread and wine is never specified
3. **No manducatio impiorum** — FC VII's teaching that unbelievers also receive Christ's true body and blood (but to their condemnation) is absent. The rite warns against unworthy reception but frames the danger in terms of "not believing" (absence of faith), not in terms of receiving a metaphysically real body/blood without faith
4. **No anti-Reformed polemic** — no rejection of "mere sign" or "spiritual presence only"
5. **No anti-Catholic polemic** on transubstantiation (the avoidance of sacrificial language is structural, not polemical)
6. **No explicit teaching on the relationship between bread/wine and body/blood** — the gloss in the admonition interprets "this is my body" in terms of function (proof/testimony), not in terms of ontological identity

### What this means for the three-tradition comparison:

**Against FC VII (normative Lutheran):** The rite is COMPATIBLE with FC VII but does not REQUIRE its distinctive commitments. A pastor holding FC VII's theology could celebrate this rite without conscience — but so could a pastor holding a more moderate, Melanchthonian, or even broadly Calvinist view. The rite does not police the boundary that FC VII establishes.

**Against old-Utraquist theology:** The Utraquists maintained real presence (some approaching transubstantiation) and insisted on sub utraque. The Agenda Česká shares the sub utraque practice but lacks the ontological presence-language that characterised Utraquist Eucharistic piety. However, the retention of "Msse" terminology and the overall liturgical conservatism (Preface, Sanctus, traditional structure) would have been familiar and acceptable to Utraquist sensibilities.

**Against the Unity of Brethren (mature, post-Lukáš Pražský):** The Unity's emphasis on the sacrament as a "spiritual" means of grace — operating through faith, strengthening the believer's relationship with Christ, without metaphysical transformation of the elements — is REMARKABLY COMPATIBLE with the Agenda Česká's "testimonial" language ("for certain proof and testimony"). The admonition's entire christological-functional framework (the body given as assurance of salvific work) reads almost as if it were designed to be acceptable to a mature Unity theologian.

### The Agenda's theological position in its historical moment

The Agenda Česká was compiled in the late 1570s by clergy ordained at Wittenberg, drawing on Wittenberg, Brandenburg, and Württemberg agenda sources (Foreword, pp. 108–112). This places it AFTER the Formula of Concord (1577) was formulated but during the period when its reception in the Bohemian/Hungarian space was still contested. The Confessio Bohemica (1575) — the ecumenical compromise between Utraquists, Lutherans, and Brethren — was only a few years old.

The Agenda's broad Eucharistic register makes strategic sense in this context: it provides a usable liturgical order for clergy who subscribed to the Augsburg Confession (as the Foreword explicitly states) but who ministered in a multi-confessional environment where sharp polemical formulations would have been divisive and pastorally counterproductive.

### The contrast with the Bystrická agenda (1585) — Benka's key observation

Benka (2024, pp. 78–79) notes that the Bystrická agenda manuscript — written only four years later, bound with the same Agenda Česká print, and presumably used in the same liturgical context — contains HARDER Eucharistic formulations, including language approaching manducatio impiorum: communicants receive "Tělo a Krew Syna Božijho" and unworthy recipients receive "ne k spasenij, ale k zatraczenij wěčnemu" (not for salvation but for eternal damnation).

This means that WITHIN A SINGLE LITURGICAL CODEX, we can observe a spectrum from irenic-broad (the printed Agenda Česká, 1581) to confessionally-sharpened (the manuscript Bystrická agenda, 1585). The handwritten supplement — produced locally, for local use — hardened what the printed template had left open. This is confessionalisation in action, visible at the level of liturgical practice rather than normative documents.

## VIII. Implications for §4.1 and Open Questions

### For the chapter argument:

This analysis provides the PRIMARY LITURGICAL EVIDENCE for the core thesis of §4.1. The argument can now be structured as follows:

1. **Lex credendi:** Present the normative Eucharistic positions from confessional documents (FC VII for Lutherans; Confessio Fidei 1535 for the Unity; Four Articles / Basel Compacts / Confessio Bohemica for Utraquists). These establish sharp doctrinal boundaries.

2. **Lex orandi:** Show that the ACTUAL LITURGICAL PRACTICE — as witnessed by the Agenda Česká 1581 — operated at a lower level of doctrinal specificity. The communion rite is identifiably Lutheran in structure but theologically open enough to accommodate non-Lutheran interpretations. The Eucharistic language is functional-testimonial rather than metaphysical-ontological.

3. **The gradient of confessionalisation:** The Agenda Česká (1581, printed, broader circulation) → Bystrická agenda (1585, manuscript, local use) → Cithara Sanctorum (1636, Tranovský's theologically filtered hymnal) represents a progressive TIGHTENING of confessional boundaries over roughly 55 years. Each step in the chain makes the Lutheran identity more explicit and the inter-confessional space narrower.

4. **What the lex orandi reveals that the lex credendi conceals:** The normative confessional documents present sharp either/or positions on the Eucharist. The liturgical texts reveal that ACTUAL WORSHIP was more accommodating, more pastorally pragmatic, and more open to shared practice than the polemical theology would suggest. The confessionalisation process was not instantaneous but gradual, and liturgical texts were among the last to be fully "captured" by strict confessional identity.

### Open questions requiring further sources:

1. **The Unity's Eucharistic liturgy:** We need a comparable liturgical text from the Unity of Brethren — ideally from their kancionáls or from the *Acta Unitatis Fratrum* — showing how THEIR communion rite expressed their "spiritual presence" theology. This would complete the three-way comparison.

2. **The Confessio Bohemica's Eucharistic articles:** How does the 1575 compromise document handle the Eucharist? Does it exhibit the same broad register as the Agenda Česká? If so, that would confirm that the Agenda's irenic language reflects the Confessio Bohemica moment rather than being a purely editorial choice.

3. **FC VII's specific formulations:** We need the FC VII text side-by-side to document precisely which Lutheran distinctive are absent from the Agenda Česká. The contrast between what FC VII DEMANDS and what the Agenda PROVIDES is the measure of the gap between lex credendi and lex orandi.

4. **The Eucharistic hymns in the CS:** Grešová-Sekelská (Príspevok 2021) identifies the "O Wečeří Páně" rubric as a confessionally sensitive zone where Tranovský composed or drew from Lutheran sources. We need to read those specific hymns and compare their Eucharistic language with the Agenda Česká's. Does Tranovský's hymnodic language (1636) show the confessional sharpening that the Bystrická agenda (1585) began?

5. **The communion hymn embedded in the rite:** The hymn "Gežijss Krystus náss Spasytel" (pp. 147–148), sung during distribution, is a Czech translation of Luther's "Jesus Christus, unser Heiland." Its 10 stanzas include both pastoral consolation and a warning against unworthy reception. A close reading of its theological content would show how HYMNODIC Eucharistic language compared with the LITURGICAL prose.
