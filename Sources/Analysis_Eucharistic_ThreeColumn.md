# Three-Column Eucharistic Comparison: FC SD VII — ConfFidei 1535 — ConfBohem 1575

**Date:** 2026-03-24 | **Phase:** 2 → 3 transition | **For §4.1**

> **Source status:**
> - FC SD VII (1577): Verified from thebookofconcord.org (Triglot/Mueller EN trans.)
> - ConfFidei 1535, Art. 11+13: Verified from Crews 2005 EN trans. (Moravian Archives PDF)
> - ConfBohem 1575, Art. XIII+XV: Czech text verified from PDF (Česká konfese, resources/). My translation below. **Cross-check against Just/Rothkegel 2012 when acquired.**

## I. Identification of Elements: What IS the bread and wine?

### FC SD VII (Lutheran)
"The body and blood of Christ are **truly and essentially present**, and are **truly distributed and received with the bread and wine**." (§6)
"The bread does not **signify** the absent body... but on account of the **sacramental union**, they are **truly the body and blood of Christ**." (§7)
"In, with, and under the bread" (*in pane, sub pane, cum pane*). (§35)
Words of institution read literally, not figuratively: "not as a figurative, allegorical expression... but as an unusual expression" (*inusitata propositio*). (§38)

### ConfFidei 1535 (Unity of Brethren)
"It is to be believed in the heart and confessed with the mouth that **the bread of the Lord's Supper is the true body of Christ**, which was given for us. And **the chalice is His true blood** which was poured out for us for the remission of sins." (Art. 13)
"No one should add to, mix in with, or take away anything of one's own from these words of Christ, by which He Himself proclaimed **the bread His body and the wine His blood**." (Art. 13)
Simple identification: bread IS body, wine IS blood. No elaboration of mode.

### ConfBohem 1575 (Trans-confessional)
"**Ten chleb v též večeři jest pravé tělo Krista pána** za nás dané a zrazené, **a víno v kalichu jest pravá krev** pána našeho Ježíše Krista za nás vylita." (Art. XV)
= "That bread in the same supper **IS the true body of Christ the Lord**, given and betrayed for us, and the wine in the chalice **IS the true blood** of our Lord Jesus Christ, poured out for us."
Same simple identificatory language as ConfFidei 1535. No "in/with/under."

### ⇒ COMPARISON
All three use identificatory language ("is the true body"). FC SD VII adds the *in/sub/cum* formula and the sacramental union concept. ConfFidei 1535 and CB 1575 deliberately stop at identification without specifying mode. **The gap is not in WHAT is affirmed but in HOW MUCH is specified.**

## II. Mode of Reception: Spiritual, oral, or both?

### FC SD VII
"The body and blood of Christ are received with the bread and wine, **not only spiritually by faith, but also orally** (*manducatio oralis*); yet not in a Capernaitic, but in a **supernatural, heavenly mode**, because of the sacramental union." (§15/Ep.6)
Explicitly rejects purely spiritual eating as the *only* mode.

### ConfFidei 1535
No explicit statement on oral vs. spiritual reception in Art. 13. However, Art. 11 (Sacraments general) says sacraments "do not bestow on those who are not first provided with a good intention... any grace or justifying faith... Indeed, **faith must come first**." Art. 6 (Christology) teaches Christ's body is in heaven and He is present on earth "through the Holy Spirit and His gifts" — an "invisible" mode of presence.
The emphasis is entirely on faith and spiritual reception. Oral reception is not denied but not affirmed.

### ConfBohem 1575 — THE KEY FINDING
"Abychom přijímajíce **duchovně i podstatně, věrou i také ústy** svátosti těla a krve pána našeho Ježíše Krista..." (Art. XV)
= "So that receiving **spiritually AND substantially, by faith AND ALSO BY MOUTH** the sacrament of the body and blood of our Lord Jesus Christ..."

**This is remarkable.** The CB 1575 explicitly affirms BOTH spiritual AND substantial reception, BOTH by faith AND by mouth. The word **"podstatně"** (substantially) is the Czech equivalent of Latin *substantialiter* — this is real-presence language. And **"ústy"** (by mouth) is explicit oral reception (*manducatio oralis*).

**Britannica's claim that the CB upheld "the Calvinist interpretation of the Eucharist" is flatly wrong on the evidence of the text.** The CB affirms exactly what the Calvinists denied: substantial presence and oral reception. The formula "duchovně i podstatně" (spiritually AND substantially) is in fact a carefully constructed **both/and** that bridges the Lutheran insistence on bodily presence with the Reformed emphasis on spiritual reception — it affirms both without choosing.

### ⇒ COMPARISON
FC SD VII: spiritual + oral reception, with oral as the distinctive marker. ConfFidei 1535: spiritual reception emphasised, oral not addressed. **CB 1575: explicitly "spiritually AND substantially, by faith AND ALSO by mouth" — a deliberate bridging formula that is closer to FC SD VII than to Calvin.** The CB's formula could be read as satisfying Lutheran requirements (substantial, oral) while simultaneously reassuring Reformed sensibilities (spiritual, by faith). This is irenic theology at its most sophisticated.

## III. Reception by the Unworthy (*Manducatio Impiorum*)

### FC SD VII
"Whether in the Holy Supper the true body and blood of our Lord Jesus Christ are truly and essentially present... and **received with the mouth by all those who use this Sacrament, whether they be worthy or unworthy, godly or ungodly, believing or unbelieving**; by the believing for consolation and life, **by the unbelieving for judgment**?" (§2)
"**Not only the true believers and the worthy, but also the unworthy and unbelievers, receive the true body and blood of Christ**; however, not for life and consolation, but for judgment and condemnation." (§16/Ep.7)
This is the hardest Lutheran distinctive — the *manducatio impiorum*. Unbelievers really receive Christ's body, to their judgment.

### ConfFidei 1535
Art. 11 (Sacraments general): "If anyone comes to the sacraments unworthily, this person is **not made worthy or clean by them**, but **calls down greater sin and damnation** on oneself." Cites 1 Cor. 11:27, 29.
Art. 13 (Lord's Supper): "This sacrament is to be received with purity of soul, reverence, and faith, and especially with **self-examination**." Cites 1 Cor. 11:28–29.
Uses Judas and Ananias/Sapphira as examples of unworthy reception (Art. 11). But the argument is that sacraments "did not remove this evil from them **nor give them the faith which makes alive**" — i.e., the sacrament was ineffective for them, not that they truly received Christ's body to their judgment. **The emphasis is on sacramental inefficacy for the unworthy, not on real bodily reception by the unworthy.**

### ConfBohem 1575
Art. XIII (Sacraments general): "Svátosti velebné nepřinášejí takovým odpuštění hříchův a spasení ale **hrozný soud a zatracení**."
= "The venerable sacraments do not bring such [people] forgiveness of sins and salvation but **terrible judgment and damnation**."
Art. XV (Lord's Supper) does not contain an explicit *manducatio impiorum* statement. The emphasis is entirely on worthy reception and its benefits.

### ⇒ COMPARISON
FC SD VII makes *manducatio impiorum* a confessional boundary marker — the test that separates true Lutherans from "subtle Sacramentarians." **Neither the ConfFidei 1535 nor the CB 1575 affirms it in FC SD VII's strong form.** Both affirm that unworthy reception brings judgment, but neither states that the unworthy *truly receive the body and blood*. The 1535 Confession's language ("the sacraments did not give them faith") implies the sacrament was ineffective, not that the body was truly present and received. The CB 1575 speaks of judgment for misuse but does not specify that the body itself is orally received by unbelievers.
**This is the sharpest divergence in the entire comparison.** FC SD VII would classify the CB 1575's silence on this point as suspicious — precisely the kind of ambiguity that "subtle Sacramentarians" exploit.

## IV. Purpose and Fruit of the Sacrament

### FC SD VII
Primarily ontological/christological: the sacrament IS the body and blood. Purpose is consolation, strengthening of faith, and assurance — but these flow from the real presence, not the other way around.

### ConfFidei 1535
"[Ministers] urge the people to **believe in the presence of the Body of Christ**. They then distribute [the elements] as the people fall on their knees... the people make expressions of gratitude recollecting in hymns and spiritual songs the blessings of God." (Art. 13)
The liturgical description is remarkably eucharistic — kneeling reception, exhortation to believe in Christ's presence. But the framing is functional: the sacrament testifies and confirms.

### ConfBohem 1575
Art. XIII (general): Sacraments are "viditelné pečeti a jistoty zaslíbení božských" = "visible seals and assurances of divine promises."
Art. XV: The supper is instituted "pro vzbuzení a potvrzení naší víry v účastenství Krista pána" = "for the awakening and confirmation of our faith in the participation of Christ the Lord and all his benefits."
Also: "abychom... vždy víc a víc učiněni býváme živí oudové Krista pána" = "so that we are ever more and more made living members of Christ the Lord."
And a strong ecclesiological dimension: communion makes us "jedno tělo" (one body) and obliges mutual love — "we cannot despise any of our neighbours without despising Christ the Lord in them."

### ⇒ COMPARISON
All three share the functional dimension (strengthening faith, assurance). The CB 1575 has the richest ecclesiological elaboration — communion as incorporation into Christ's body and mutual obligation of love. This echoes the Brethren's emphasis on community, not the Lutheran focus on individual consolation. FC SD VII's emphasis is on the ontological reality of the presence; the CB 1575's emphasis is on what the presence *does* in the community.

## V. *Sub Utraque Specie*

### FC SD VII
Not a central concern — communion in both kinds is assumed as standard Lutheran practice.

### ConfFidei 1535
"His Body and Blood should be received by all without distinction **in both kinds** (*sub utraque*) as was instituted and ordained by Christ Himself and as the primitive Church made use of this sacrament in its entirety." (Art. 13) Explicitly defended against those who "have the audacity to do something against this institution of Christ."

### ConfBohem 1575
The entire confession is framed as the confession "of all three estates... who receive the Body and Blood of Christ the Lord **under both kinds** (*pod obojí*)." Sub utraque is the defining marker of the confessing community — it appears in the title itself.

### ⇒ COMPARISON
Sub utraque is a shared practice but functions differently. For Lutherans, it's a return to apostolic practice. For the Brethren, it's a contested recovery requiring explicit defence. For the CB 1575 community, it's the **confessional identity marker** — the very name "pod obojí" defines who they are. This is the deepest shared ground — and the one area where all three traditions are fully aligned against Rome.

## VI. SYNTHESIS: What This Comparison Reveals

### The Spectrum of Eucharistic Theology

These three documents map a spectrum that is **not** a simple Lutheran → Reformed gradient:

| Dimension | FC SD VII (1577) | CB 1575 | ConfFidei 1535 | Agenda Česká 1581 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Identification (bread = body) | YES + mode specified | YES, simple | YES, simple | YES, functional gloss |
| Mode: in/with/under | YES, explicit | NO | NO | NO |
| Substantial presence | YES | **YES** ("podstatně") | Not specified | Not specified |
| Oral reception | YES, explicit | **YES** ("ústy") | Not addressed | Not addressed |
| Spiritual reception | YES (but not exclusively) | **YES** ("duchovně, věrou") | Emphasised | Emphasised |
| Manducatio impiorum | **YES — hard boundary** | **Silent** | Not in strong form | Not addressed |
| Sub utraque | Assumed | **Identity marker** | Defended | Practised |
| Primary register | Ontological/polemical | **Irenic bridging** | Functional/testimonial | Pastoral/catechetical |

### The Bombshell: CB 1575 Is NOT Calvinist

Britannica's standard characterisation — that the CB upheld "the Calvinist interpretation of the Eucharist" — is contradicted by the Czech text. The formula "duchovně i podstatně, věrou i také ústy" explicitly affirms substantial presence and oral reception, which are the very things Calvinists denied. The CB's Eucharistic article is better read as a **deliberately ambiguous bridging formula** that could satisfy Lutherans (substantial, oral) AND Reformed (spiritual, by faith) AND conservative Utraquists (simple identification, sub utraque) simultaneously.

This is precisely what Haberkern (2023) argues about the CB as a whole: it was an exercise in pragmatic toleration and theological common ground. But our analysis shows that this "common ground" was achieved not by choosing one side but by **affirming everything simultaneously without specifying which affirmation is primary**.

### The Absent Lutheran Distinctive: Manducatio Impiorum

The one thing NO Czech confession affirms is the reception of Christ's body by unbelievers. This is the line FC SD VII draws with maximum precision — the test that separates real Lutherans from crypto-Calvinists. The CB 1575's silence here is not accidental; it is a deliberate omission that keeps the tent wide enough for all three Czech traditions. **This silence is the confessional fault line our chapter can expose.**

### Implications for the Agenda Česká 1581 and for Tranovský's CS

The Agenda Česká's Eucharistic admonition (analysed in `Analysis_Eucharistic_Rite_AgCeska.md`) now falls precisely into place. Its "testimonial" gloss on "this is my body" — "for certain proof and testimony (*k gistotnému dokázanij a swědomij*) I give you my Body for food" — operates in the same irenic register as the CB 1575. It affirms identification (bread = body) without specifying mode, emphasises the functional/assurance purpose, and is silent on manducatio impiorum.

**The four-source sequence reveals a coherent pattern:**

1. **ConfFidei 1535:** Simple identification + faith emphasis. Open register.
2. **CB 1575:** Simple identification + explicit bridging formula ("spiritually AND substantially, by faith AND by mouth"). Wider tent, deliberately ambiguous on the hard questions.
3. **Agenda Česká 1581:** Simple identification + functional/testimonial gloss. Liturgical expression of the CB's irenic register.
4. **FC SD VII (1577):** Full ontological specification + explicit boundaries (manducatio oralis, manducatio impiorum, sacramental union). Polemical precision.

**The question for §5 (Transfer and Reception) now becomes precise:** When Tranovský compiled the CS (1636), which register did his Eucharistic hymns operate in? Did he select hymns that stayed in the irenic CB 1575 register? Or did he sharpen them toward FC SD VII's precision? If hymns from Brethren kancionáls entered CS with their open, functional Eucharistic language, did Tranovský alter the language to introduce manducatio impiorum markers, sacramental-union vocabulary, or anti-Calvinist polemical sharpening?

**This is now a testable hypothesis.** We can search CS Eucharistic hymns for the presence or absence of: (a) "in/with/under" language, (b) unworthy-reception language that specifies bodily presence to the unworthy, (c) functional/testimonial framing vs. ontological affirmation. If the hymns show confessional sharpening beyond the CB's irenic register, that IS the "Lutheranisation" our thesis posits — operating at the lex orandi level where the lex credendi documents remained diplomatically open.
