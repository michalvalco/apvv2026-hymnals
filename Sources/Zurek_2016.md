# Žurek, Jiří — The Utraquist Sanctoral in Sixteenth-Century Czech-Language Graduals (2016)

## Citation
ŽŮREK, Jiří: The Utraquist Sanctoral in Sixteenth-Century Czech-Language Graduals. In: *The Bohemian Reformation and Religious Practice* 10 (2016), pp. 279–308. Originally published in Czech in *Listy filologické* 136 (2013), pp. 315–341.

## Summary
A systematic comparative study of the sanctoral (calendar of saints' feasts) in 43 surviving Czech-language Utraquist graduals from the 16th century, compared against official/quasi-official documents of both the sub utraque and sub una (Roman) churches. The study identifies which saints' feasts were actually celebrated in Utraquist liturgical practice, their rank, and the degree to which Utraquist practice converged with or diverged from the Roman calendar. Special attention is given to Czech saints and to the feast of Jan Hus.

Published in the peer-reviewed *Bohemian Reformation and Religious Practice* series (BRRP 10), which is the leading English-language venue for Czech Reformation liturgical studies.

## Key Arguments

### Main finding — Utraquist sanctoral broadly coincided with Roman practice:
- "The selection of the holy days of obligation in the sixteenth century basically coincided in the sanctoral of the Utraquists and the sub una." (p. 298) Utraquists celebrated the same saints' feasts as Catholics, with the addition of Jan Hus and with vacillation on some minor feasts.
- Confirms Halama's conclusion: "The veneration of Czech saints among the adherents of the Church sub utraque in our lands did not differ much from the veneration which these saints enjoyed within the framework of the Roman Church." (Halama, cited p. 298)
- The feast of Jan Hus was present in almost all graduals, though many relevant folia were destroyed by Counter-Reformation censors. 33 documented occurrences identified. Hus's texts indicate he had "actually taken over the role of the principal patron of the Bohemian lands." (p. 298)
- St. Wenceslaus occupied the leading place among Czech saints, appearing in almost all sources. (p. 297)

### Critical finding for our chapter — the Agenda Česká as a break from Utraquist practice:
- "This contrasts in particular with the drastically reduced sanctoral of the Agenda česká of 1581, which took for its model German Lutheran agendas, in which the principle of Sola Scriptura unambiguously prevailed in the question of celebrating holy days — the compilers of the Agenda included exclusively the commemoration of persons or events found in Scripture." (pp. 298–299)
- "We do not find a single gradual which was composed on the basis of the requirements of the Agenda česká." (p. 299) The Agenda Česká's reduced sanctoral was NOT adopted in actual Utraquist liturgical practice. The graduals continued to follow the traditional Utraquist calendar.
- This means the Agenda Česká represented a theological PROGRAMME (Lutheran *sola Scriptura* applied to the calendar) that was NOT implemented in the broader Utraquist/neo-Utraquist worship landscape. The gap between the Agenda's prescriptions and actual practice is significant.

### On the graduals as evidence of actual practice vs. normative prescription:
- Holy days celebrated "per excessum" (beyond official requirements) are "a very important find, because they supplement and distinctly correct the idea of the holy days of obligation, which is derived from official documents." (p. 292) This is a direct *lex orandi* argument — actual liturgical practice exceeded what normative documents prescribed.
- Graduals "cannot be considered as calendaria that covered every individual saint and the pertinent rubricated directions" — they represent what was actually sung, not a comprehensive calendar. (p. 450 in numbering = p. ~290 in article)
- Methodological caution: "A complex view of the sixteenth-century Utraquist calendar would require further study of antiphonaries, kancionáls, calendars, missals, Latin graduals, lectionaries — indeed, also sermon collections." (p. 297)

### On confessional boundaries in liturgical sources:
- The Kolínský kancionál (1517) is excluded from the gradual comparison because it is a songbook with attached choral offices, not a gradual proper — but it is identified as a key source (p. 87 in numbering = p. ~281). This is the same Kolínský kancionál that Grešová-Sekelská identifies as a major source for the CS.
- The Českoskalický graduál has an "extremely abbreviated sanctoral" but even it does not follow the Agenda Česká's requirements — it includes the Assumption (non-scriptural) but lacks feasts the Agenda includes. (p. 299)

## Relation to TEPALIT Chapter
- **Which locus/section:** §4.4 (Scripture and Tradition — *sola Scriptura* applied to the liturgical calendar); §2 (Confessional Context — Utraquist liturgical practice); §4.2 (Ecclesiology — the saints' feasts reveal ecclesiological commitments about the communion of saints)
- **How it supports our argument:** Žurek's finding dramatically enriches the *lex orandi, lex credendi* analysis. The Agenda Česká's Eucharistic rite (as we showed in the Analysis document) operates in a broad, irenic register. But its sanctoral is sharply Lutheran — reduced to scriptural figures only. This creates an internal asymmetry within the Agenda itself: the Eucharistic theology is accommodating, but the calendar is confessionally strict. This asymmetry suggests that different aspects of the liturgy were confessionalised at different speeds and to different degrees.
- **The non-reception finding is crucial:** If no gradual followed the Agenda Česká's requirements, then the Agenda represents a PROGRAMME that outpaced its community. The Eucharistic rite's irenic register may have been deliberate pastoral accommodation — the editors knew they were pushing the confessional boundary with the calendar and compensated by keeping the sacramental theology broad. This is a sophisticated reading that the data supports.
- **For §5:** If the Agenda Česká's Lutheran calendar was not adopted in Utraquist practice, but the CS (1636) succeeded where the Agenda failed, then Tranovský's achievement was precisely to create a hymnological vehicle that ACTUALLY effected the confessional shift that the Agenda had only prescribed.
- **Methodological model:** Žurek's comparison of normative documents against actual liturgical sources (graduals) is exactly the *lex orandi* methodology our chapter employs for Eucharistic theology.
