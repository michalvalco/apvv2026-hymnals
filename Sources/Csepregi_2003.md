# Csepregi, Zoltán — Konfessionsbildung und Einheitsbestrebungen im Königreich Ungarn zur Regierungszeit Ferdinands I. (2003)

## Citation
CSEPREGI, Zoltán: Konfessionsbildung und Einheitsbestrebungen im Königreich Ungarn zur Regierungszeit Ferdinands I. In: *Archiv für Reformationsgeschichte* 94 (2003), pp. 243–275. Open-access mirror: [academia.edu/5472154](https://www.academia.edu/5472154).

## Summary

A source-critical reassessment of the *opinio communis* surrounding three foundational documents of central-European Protestant confession-formation under Ferdinand I (1527–1564): Johannes Honterus's *Reformatio ecclesiae Coronensis* (1543) for the Transylvanian Saxons, the Upper-Hungarian *Confessio Pentapolitana* (allegedly 1549), and the legal-ecclesial recognition process of the latter. Csepregi presents three theses which, taken together, redraw the confessional landscape of mid-16th-century Hungary: (1) the term *reformatio* in Honterus's title is rooted in Catholic-renewal vocabulary, not in any later Protestant self-designation; (2) Leonhard Stöckel cannot be the sole author of the 1549 *Pentapolitana* as submitted, and the document we possess may in fact be a conflation of two distinct texts under a single title; (3) neither the *Confessio Augustana* nor the *Pentapolitana* was ever officially recognized by Hungarian authorities during the period under study — at best they were tactically tolerated (*taktische Duldung*) (p. 245).

The article's method is a careful disentangling of two contradictory traditions about the *Pentapolitana*'s origin: the older one, traceable to the diary of the Leutschau senator Daniel Türk, which records a confessional document submitted in 1549 by the Preschau city council, names no author, and makes no reference to Anabaptists or Sacramentarians; and the younger one, traceable to the *Apologia examinis ecclesiae et scholae Bartphensis* (1590), authored by Stöckel's son-in-law and successor Thomas Faber, which names Stöckel as author and centres the document in the anti-Anabaptist and anti-Zwinglian polemics of the 1560s. Ribini's 18th-century *Memorabilia* harmonised these two traditions, and the harmonised version dominated subsequent scholarship until Suda's 2001 challenge.

Csepregi's broader achievement is to relocate the *Pentapolitana* away from the standard Lutheran-confessionalisation narrative (where it functions as a regional appropriation of CA) and to read it instead as a document of *taktisch-konziliante* Catholicity, deliberately less polemical than CA itself, designed to keep options open in a political environment where neither imperial recognition nor open Lutheran identification were yet possible. The conclusion summarises in six points the parallel confessional developments in Transylvania and Upper Hungary, and ends with the 1597 Leutschau synod's identification of the *Pentapolitana* as a mere "Extract" of CA — an identification which itself, Csepregi argues, marks the retrospective Lutheranisation of a document that had been something more theologically capacious in its origins.

## Key Arguments

### The two-document hypothesis for *Confessio Pentapolitana* (pp. 261–263)

Csepregi's central source-critical move is to show that the standard scholarly account of the *Pentapolitana* — composed by Stöckel in 1549, submitted to royal commissioners, approved from 1558 onward by king and bishops — is a harmonised fiction.

The earliest source, Türk's diary, records only "the submission of an unspecified confessional document in 1549 by the Preschau city councillors, names no author, and makes no reference to Anabaptists and Sacramentarians" (p. 261, my paraphrase from Csepregi's German). The 1590 *Apologia examinis* (by Faber, Stöckel's son-in-law) is the first source to name Stöckel as author, but it does so in the context of 1560s polemics against Anabaptists and Zwinglians — and even there, no specific date is given for Stöckel's authorial work. Stöckel's own correspondence — preserved and edited by Skoviera — contains nothing on the *Pentapolitana* (p. 261, fn. 83).

The original 1549 submission is lost; not even a contemporary copy survives. The *editio princeps* did not appear until 1613 in Kaschau, sixty-four years after the alleged submission and fifty-three years after Stöckel's death. By that point what was being printed was certainly a Stöckel-redacted text (Csepregi places the redaction "around 1559," p. 262), but the relationship between the printed 1613 text and the 1549 submission cannot be established with certainty. Suda 2001 had already challenged Stöckel's authorship on stylistic, theological, and biblical-citation grounds.

Csepregi's preferred reconstruction: there were probably two distinct texts subsumed under the *Pentapolitana* name — a 1549 submission of unknown authorship, and a later (1550s) Stöckel redaction that more closely follows CA. The fact that Stöckel's name appears in connection with the document only in 1590 supports this reading (p. 262).

### *Pentapolitana* vs. *Confessio Augustana*: Suda's five differences (pp. 263–264)

Following Suda, Csepregi enumerates five sharp differences between the *Pentapolitana* and CA, all of which point in the same direction: the *Pentapolitana* is more conciliatory than CA, deliberately. (1) The *Pentapolitana* does not emphasise the priority of faith over works. (2) It places the church under temporal authority — fitting the political situation of the five free royal cities. (3) It has no separate article on priestly marriage. (4) Its critique of the existing church is only indirect; the CA *abusus mutati* articles (CA XXII–XXVIII) have no parallel. (5) It condemns no heresies, ancient or contemporary — not even the Anabaptists, whose expulsion was being legislated by the very Diet to which the document was submitted.

Csepregi's reading of this evidence: the *Pentapolitana* "in its conciliatoriness followed the model of the *Confessio Augustana*, but went considerably further than it. The caution in what the confession says, and in what it does not say, must be recognised as a meaningful and at the same time necessary adaptation to the ecclesiastical and political circumstances of this time" (p. 264, my translation). The *Pentapolitana*'s reticence is calculated.

### Pentapolitana's 20-article structure (p. 263)

The 1613 printed text divides into 20 articles: (1) De Deo, (2) De creatione, (3) De peccato originali, (4) De incarnatione Christi, (5) De iustificatione, (6) De fide, (7) De bonis operibus, (8) De ecclesia, (9) De baptismo, (10) De coena Domini, (11) De confessione, (12) De poenitentia, (13) De usu sacramentorum, (14) De ministris ecclesiae, (15) De ceremoniis, (16) De magistratu civili, (17) De matrimonio, (18) De resurrectione mortuorum, (19) De libero arbitrio, (20) De invocatione sanctorum. Article X on the Lord's Supper is structurally where CA's Article X sits. Csepregi gives the Latin and German texts of Article XVII (marriage) as his sample; the Eucharistic article itself is referenced but not quoted in extenso.

### *Pentapolitana* never officially recognised — only *taktisch geduldet* (pp. 264–273)

The fourth substantive section of the article systematically dismantles the long-standing claims of imperial or archiepiscopal approbation of the *Pentapolitana*.

The 1548 Pressburg Diet's Article 11 ordered the expulsion of Anabaptists and Sacramentarians; later diets (Article 26 of 1556, Article 10§4 of 1557) retained only the anti-Anabaptist clause. Csepregi reads this as a *dissimulating compromise formula* (p. 265, citing Kohnle's habilitation) — Hungarian authorities in 1548 also wanted to suppress Lutherans, but tactical considerations forced them to drop the explicit anti-Sacramentarian language. The 1548 Article 5 nonetheless committed Hungary to a *reformatio catholica*: religion and divine worship were to be brought back to the old norm.

The Xylander/Holtzmann claim (in the *Matricula Molleriana*) that Ferdinand approved the *Pentapolitana* at the 1558 Frankfurt Reichstag is a fiction: no Hungarian city sent envoys to that diet, and no other source records such an approbation. The motivation, Csepregi suggests, was retrospective securing of confessional and legal status by appeal to the oldest possible imperial recognition (p. 267).

What *did* happen — and what the documentary record secures — is the 1559 Augsburg encounter, where envoys of the seven Lower-Hungarian mining towns sought to justify their cult before Ferdinand and were dressed down. The Kremnitz council reported back to Schemnitz that the king cited the Esztergom archbishop's accusations against them, mocked their Schuhmacher-und-Schneider clergy, and — when the envoys appealed to the *Confessio Augustana* — replied with the line that has become emblematic of the Habsburg position on Hungarian Lutheranism. The full passage, in the original Kremnitz reportage, is on pp. 267–268; the most quotable sentence is below in the Notable Quotes section.

The 1573 Verantius visit to Preschau, sometimes treated in older scholarship as the moment of archiepiscopal approbation of the *Pentapolitana*, is shown by Platner's *Matricula Molleriana* entry to have been something quite different: a discussion of priestly marriage, an exchange of gifts, and an episcopal blessing — not an approbation of the confession (p. 270, fn. 114). Verantius died one month later. Later Lutheran tradition then projected approbation onto the friendly visit.

By the time of the 1594 *Protocollum Rimanovianum* and Alvinczi's 1634 last will, Lutheran tradition had become so confused that the *Pentapolitana* was being attributed to the year 1530 (!) and to Charles V (!), and was claimed as approved at Augsburg in 1549 (no Augsburg recognition occurred) (pp. 270–271). Csepregi's verdict is restrained: an approbation may have happened, but only late and only as tactical toleration — "such a 'permission' — or better: toleration — of the *Confessio Pentapolitana* could only have occurred relatively late and only on tactical grounds" (p. 271, my translation).

### The 1597 Leutschau "Extract" identification (p. 275)

The conclusion ends with a striking passage from the 1597 Leutschau synod, where the *Pentapolitana* and CA were read in parallel and the *Pentapolitana* was declared to be "a true Extract or Excerpt of the Augsburg Confession" (Sculteti, *Hypomnema* 1599, fol. B3b, cited in Csepregi p. 275). This identification, Csepregi notes, "soon led — as has been shown above by many examples — to the simple confusion of the two writings" (p. 275, my translation). The *Pentapolitana* lost its distinctive theological character precisely at the moment it was assimilated to CA.

### Six summary points of confessional development (pp. 273–275)

The *Schluss* section offers six parallel observations on Transylvania and Upper Hungary under Ferdinand I: (1) Reich-level religious politics directly shaped Hungarian confessional documents. (2) A pre-Tridentine concept of "Catholicity" was claimed by both *Reformatio Coronensis* and *Pentapolitana*. (3) Both confessions were conceived as consensus documents — hence the anonymity of the *Pentapolitana* and the suppression of Honterus's personal authorial role. (4) Honterus's possession of a printing press preserved his text; the absence of printing capacity in Upper Hungary meant the five cities did not even possess copies of their own confession in the years following its submission, leaving its text little-noticed even after its proclamation. The *Rezeption seiner Entstehungsgeschichte* (reception of its origin story) thus far outstripped the *Rezeption des Textes* (reception of the text itself). (5) The functions of these documents multiplied over time: from accountability before authority, to ecclesial-legal foundation for unification, to doctrinal consensus among clergy, to grounds for claiming "old privileges." (6) CA acquired ecclesial-legal weight in the region only after the period studied (Transylvania 1571; Upper Hungary tentatively 1546, then more securely 1582). Until then, the local confessions held primacy because they were better adapted to local political conditions.

## Notable Quotes

> "1. Der Reformationsbegriff von Honterus ist auf dem Hintergrund der katholischen Erneuerung und Integrationsversuche zu verstehen. 2. Leonhard Stöckel kann nicht als alleiniger Verfasser der 1549 eingereichten 'Confessio Pentapolitana' gelten. Es ist nicht auszuschließen, daß es sich hier um zwei verschiedene Schriften handelt, die unter dem Titel der 'Confessio Pentapolitana' zusammengefaßt wurden: ein 1549 vorgelegtes Bekenntnis, das wahrscheinlich nicht mit der in den fünf Städten 1546 rezipierten 'Confessio Augustana' identisch ist, und eine spätere, nicht genau datierbare Ausarbeitung von Stöckel, die inhaltlich der 'Confessio Augustana' folgt. 3. Das lutherische Bekenntnis wie auch die 'Confessio Pentapolitana' wurden im Untersuchungszeitraum durch die ungarischen Behörden nicht offiziell anerkannt, sondern bestenfalls aus taktischen Gründen geduldet oder nur zur Kenntnis genommen." (p. 245)
- Context: The three theses of the article. Each one materially affects the chapter's framing of Slovak Lutheran confessional identity in §3.

> "die Augspurgische confession, die gehört in das Reich und nicht in Hungeren." (p. 268, citing the 1559 Kremnitz council report on Ferdinand I's Augsburg reception)
- Context: Ferdinand's blunt rebuke of the Lower-Hungarian mining towns when their envoys appealed to CA. The line marks the formal-legal boundary: CA had standing in the Reich after the 1555 Augsburg Religious Peace, but Ferdinand explicitly refused to extend that standing into Hungary. For Slovak Lutheran identity in the chapter's period, this means the appeal-to-CA strategy that worked in the Empire did not work for the Hungarian Crown's territories. The five free royal cities had to find a different rhetorical register — and the *Pentapolitana*'s deliberate Konzilianz is the result.

> "Die 'Confessio Pentapolitana' ist in ihrer Konzilianz dem Vorbild der 'Confessio Augustana' gefolgt, ging darin jedoch noch entschieden weiter." (p. 264)
- Context: The crystallisation of Csepregi's reading. The *Pentapolitana* is *more* irenic than CA, deliberately. This pairs naturally with the chapter's similar reading of the Agenda Česká 1581 as deliberately accommodating in its Eucharistic rite.

> "In welcher verlesung beider Confession [Confessio Augustana und Confessio Pentapolitana] / eine nutzliche Collation / oder zusammenhaltung / vergleichung / vnd erklerung geschehen ist eines ieglichen Artickels / vnd befunden / das diser fünff stedt Bekentnus / ein warhafftiger Extract / oder Außzug sey der Augspurgischen Confession / vnd mit derselbigen aller ding / in allen Artickeln vnd puncten einhellig vberein stimme." (Sculteti, *Hypomnema* 1599, fol. B3b, cited in Csepregi p. 275)
- Context: The 1597 Leutschau synod's identification of the *Pentapolitana* as a "true Extract" of CA. This is the moment of retrospective Lutheranisation — the *Pentapolitana*'s distinctive Konzilianz dissolves into CA-conformity. The 1597 framing then governs all later reception, which is why Slovak Lutheran historiography has tended to read the *Pentapolitana* as straightforwardly Lutheran when its 1549/1550s text was something more theologically capacious.

## Relation to TEPALIT Chapter

- **Which locus/section:** §3 (Konfesionálny kontext — Slovak Lutheran confessionalisation in the period leading up to the chapter's source base); §4.1 (Eucharistia — *Pentapolitana* Article X as part of the Slovak Lutheran sacramental-doctrinal background to AC 1581 and CS 1636); §4.4 (Písmo a tradícia — the late and contested establishment of CA's ecclesial-legal weight in Upper Hungary, 1582); §2 (general confessional context for the chapter's period).

- **How it supports our argument:** Csepregi's three theses re-frame the Slovak Lutheran confessional landscape in ways the chapter must take seriously. The traditional narrative — Stöckel as 1549 author, multiple imperial and archiepiscopal approbations, Pentapolitana as straightforwardly Lutheran daughter-document of CA — is shown to be a layered fiction built up between 1590 and the 18th century. What we actually have in the *Pentapolitana* is a deliberately reticent document that pre-dates clear confessional consolidation in the region. This matters for the chapter because the same period that produced the *Pentapolitana* (1546–1559) is the period in which the Bohemian *Confessio Bohemica* 1575 was being prepared in Prague, and the tendency in older scholarship has been to read these documents through retrospectively-confessionalised lenses. Csepregi gives us license to read both sets of documents as more conciliatory and less rigidly confessional than later Lutheran or Calvinist self-narration claimed.

- **The "Konzilianz" reading is methodologically powerful for the chapter.** If the *Pentapolitana* is deliberately less polemical than CA — going beyond CA in its irenic register precisely because of political-tactical pressure — then the parallel suggestion that AC 1581's Eucharistic rite operates in a similarly capacious register becomes more plausible. Both documents emerge from the same broad pressure: a Reformed-leaning urban elite operating under Habsburg authority that has not formally tolerated their cult, working with rhetorical resources designed to maximise tactical room. The Theologos paper's reading of AC 1581 / BA 1585 is strengthened by Csepregi's parallel reading of the *Pentapolitana*.

- **The "Augspurgische confession gehört in das Reich, nicht in Hungeren" line is foundational for §3.** It establishes the formal-legal asymmetry between Reich-Lutheranism (CA-recognised after 1555) and Hungarian-Crown Lutheranism (no CA recognition until much later, and even then only for restricted territories). Slovak Lutheran identity in the chapter's period developed in this asymmetry. The chapter should make this asymmetry explicit, since it shapes how we read the Slovak Lutheran reception of Czech Reformation hymnology — not as Reich-confident Lutherans absorbing weaker Czech material, but as Hungarian-Crown Lutherans operating in a tactically constrained confessional environment, drawing on Czech-Reformation resources that were similarly tactically constrained.

- **Methodological model.** Csepregi's source-critical disentangling of the Türk vs. Faber traditions, his exposure of Ribini's harmonisation, his recovery of the original archival voices — this is precisely the method the chapter applies to the AC-1581-Calvinist-Eucharistic-mis-reading. Both cases are examples of *reception-history fictions hardening into scholarly orthodoxy*, and both can be undone by the same archival method.

## Notes

- The article is dedicated *dem Andenken Prof. Jenő Sólyoms (1904–1976)* — a member of the *Theologischer Arbeitskreis für reformationsgeschichtliche Forschung* (TARF) who first identified Ribini's selective harmonisation. The dedication is a methodological signal: Csepregi 2003 stands in a Hungarian-Lutheran source-critical tradition that has been challenging the older confessional historiography for decades.
- Csepregi cross-references his own related work in the same article: *Die Anfänge der Reformation im Königreich Ungarn bis 1548* (Ljubljana/Vienna 2011, but the underlying paper is from earlier), open-access at academia.edu/5333056. This is an additional Csepregi item worth tracking for §3 background.
- The Ferdinand-Augsburg 1559 episode (pp. 267–268) is one of the historically vivid moments in the article: the Bishop of Esztergom accuses the mining-town clergy of being shoemakers and tailors, says they are "as little priests as his hounds" and can administer the sacrament "as little as his hounds." The envoys retort that their *Kirchenordnung* is set up *nach der Augspurgischen Confession* — at which point Ferdinand delivers his memorable rebuke. The full passage repays close reading; for §3 it could even be quoted at length in the chapter as a window onto the formal-legal asymmetry between Reich-Lutheranism and Hungarian-Crown Lutheranism.
- Csepregi makes the *Pentapolitana*'s lack of printing capacity a substantive theological point: because the five cities did not possess printed copies of their own confession in the years after its 1549 submission, the document's *text* was poorly received even as its *origin story* circulated and grew. This is a useful framework for the chapter — it parallels what happens with the Brethren *Šamotulský* kancionál (1561), whose manuscript transmission also outstripped its textual reception in the post-1620 period.
- Worth noting for the chapter's overall *lex orandi, lex credendi* framing: Csepregi's article shows that even the formally-confessional-document layer of central-European Protestantism is more porous and tactical than retrospective confessionalisation imagined. The argument *a fortiori*: if the formal confession layer is this fluid, the lived-worship layer (hymnody, liturgy) is fluid too. Csepregi's revision strengthens the chapter's case that *lex orandi* analysis recovers theological nuance that normative-document analysis loses.
