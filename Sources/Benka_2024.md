## Summary
A critical edition (388 pages) of two interconnected liturgical sources from the evangelical milieu of the Upper Hungarian mining towns: (1) the printed *Agenda Česká* of 1581 (pp. 107–264) and (2) its manuscript supplement, the *Bystrická agenda* of 1585 (pp. 265–368). The edition is based on the exemplar held in the Lyceal Library in Bratislava (administered by the Central Library of the Slovak Academy of Sciences), which preserves the manuscript bound together with the print. Where the Bratislava exemplar is defective (missing leaves of the Czech and Latin prefaces), the text is reconstructed from other surviving copies and flagged in the apparatus.

The volume includes two substantial scholarly studies: Peter Benka's 80-page historical introduction (pp. 10–90) contextualising the sources within the Reformation history of the mining towns (especially Banská Bystrica), and Pavol Žigo's linguistic analysis (pp. 91–103). The editorial methodology is described on pp. 104–106. Manuscript ink variations are marked ([n] = black, [f] = brown); abbreviations are expanded in brackets; musical notation is reproduced as facsimile. An English and German summary (pp. 369–374) and a comprehensive bibliography follow.

The Agenda Česká itself is a composite liturgical order compiled from several German agendas (primarily Wittenberg, Brandenburg, and Württemberg) and translated into Czech for use by neo-Utraquist/Lutheran clergy in Bohemia who had been ordained in Wittenberg. Its preface explicitly declares alignment with the Augsburg Confession and frames the work as an instrument of ceremonial unity among clergy of diverse origins.

## Key Arguments

### From Benka's Historical Introduction (pp. 10–90):

**Confessional context of the mining towns:**
- The Reformation in Banská Bystrica and surrounding mining towns followed a gradual, pragmatic path. The 1558 synod and subsequent agreements sought ceremonial uniformity while preserving "Christian freedom" in adiaphora. (pp. 50–60, passim)
- By the 1570s–80s, anti-crypto-Calvinist policing was intensifying. Georg Creutzer's handbook for identifying hidden Calvinists listed suspicious theological positions including denial of *manducatio impiorum* (unworthy reception of Christ's body and blood). (pp. 77–78)

**Eucharistic theology in the Agenda Česká — key finding for §4.1:**
- Benka observes that the Agenda Česká's Eucharistic language is **more moderate** than the strict Lutheran confessional polemic of the period. The communion rite mentions "body and blood" (*tělo a krev*) without the qualifying adjectives "true" (*pravý*) or "substantial" (*substanciálny*) that characterised hardcore Lutheran anti-Reformed polemic. (pp. 78–79)
- The *Bystrická agenda*, by contrast, contains formulations that more explicitly point toward Lutheran *manducatio impiorum* — the teaching that the real presence is independent of the communicant's worthiness, and unworthy reception brings condemnation. Benka notes repeated mentions that communicants receive "Tělo a Krew Syna Božijho" and that unworthy recipients receive "ne k spasenij, ale k zatraczenij wěčnemu." (pp. 78–79)
- This difference between the printed Agenda Česká (more moderate, closer to a Czech irenic register) and the manuscript Bystrická agenda (more explicitly Lutheran-confessional) is significant: **it suggests a spectrum of Eucharistic expression even within a single liturgical codex**, with the handwritten local supplement being theologically "harder" than the printed Czech template.

**Confession and Eucharist as linked practices:**
- Private confession (*súkromná spoveď*) was foregrounded as preparation for communion. The 1558 synod, the 1580 regulations, and the Bystrická agenda all link confession to worthy reception of the Lord's Supper. Confession was to take place the evening before or early morning of communion. (pp. 73–77)
- The linking of confession and Eucharist served a double confessional function: distinguishing Lutheran practice from both Catholic obligatory confession and Reformed elimination of private confession.

**The Agenda Česká's preface — confessional self-identification:**
- The preface (pp. 108–112 in the edition) explicitly states alignment with the Augsburg Confession and the three ecumenical creeds (Apostolicum, Nicenum, Athanasianum). It claims full doctrinal unity ("ani naymenssijho Artykule" — "not even the smallest article") among the clergy while acknowledging ceremonial diversity as the problem the Agenda seeks to solve.
- The compilers identify their ordinations as Wittenberg-derived and name their sources: Wittenberg, Brandenburg, and Württemberg agendas. This firmly situates the document in the Lutheran confessional orbit even though the language is Czech.

**Structure of the Mass rite (Pořádek Msse weliké, pp. 131–150):**
- The communion service is designated by the traditional term "Msse" (Mass), not "Večeře Páně" (Lord's Supper), suggesting conservative liturgical terminology.
- The rite includes: Introit, Kyrie, Gloria, Collect, Epistle, Gospel, Creed, Sermon, General Prayer, Words of Institution, Distribution (sub utraque), Thanksgiving, Benediction.
- A separate order is provided for "Mass without communicants" (p. 151) — significant because it shows continued celebration of the liturgical framework even without communion distribution, a point of tension with Reformed practice.

## Notable Quotes
> "Príslušné pasáže v nej [Agende českej] obsahujú napríklad zmienky o tele a krvi, bez prívlastkov typu 'pravý' či 'substanciálny'." (p. 79)
- Context: Benka's observation that the Agenda Česká's Eucharistic language lacks the polemically sharpened qualifiers of strict Lutheran confessional documents. This is a key datum for §4.1 — the liturgical text (*lex orandi*) is theologically softer than what the normative confessional documents (*lex credendi*) would require.

> "Kdo giž z toho Chléba gij a z Kalicha pige a těm slowům, kteráž od Krysta slyssij a tomu nařízenij, kteréž od Krysta přigijmá, sylně wěřij, tenť gest hodný Host k stolu Páně." (cited by Benka, p. 79, from the Agenda Česká)
- Context: The Agenda's own definition of worthy communion — whoever eats the bread, drinks the cup, and firmly believes the words of Christ's institution. Notable for its emphasis on faith in the words of institution (Lutheran) without specifying the *mode* of presence.
