# Grešová-Sekelská, Adriana — Georgius Tranoscius Teschiniensis / Jiří Třanovský (1592–1637): Duchovné piesne zo spevníka Cithara Sanctorum (1636) – výber (2023)

## Citation
GREŠOVÁ-SEKELSKÁ, Adriana: *Georgius Tranoscius Teschiniensis / Jiří Třanovský (1592–1637): Duchovné piesne zo spevníka Cithara Sanctorum (1636) – výber / Hymns from the Cithara Sanctorum (1636) – Selection.* (= Musicalia Istropolitana 4/2). Bratislava: Stimul, 2023. ISBN 978-80-8127-395-7.

## Summary
A bilingual (Slovak/English) critical edition of 8 hymns from the Cithara Sanctorum (1636) whose melodies are attributed to Třanovský himself, accompanied by a substantial introductory study. The publication is part of the Musicalia Istropolitana series (vol. 4/2) and was supported by VEGA 1/0331/20–23. The editor is from the Department of Musicology, Faculty of Arts, Comenius University, Bratislava.

The introductory study (pp. IX–XVIII) provides: (1) a contextualisation of the CS within the broader history of Christian hymnody from Ambrose through Luther to early Baroque composers; (2) a biographical sketch of Třanovský (born 1592 in Tešín, educated in Guben, Kołobrzeg, and Wittenberg; active as teacher/pastor in Prague, Třeboň, Holešov, Valašské Meziříčí; exiled to Bielsko-Biała and then to Upper Hungary — Orava Castle and Liptov; died 29 May 1637); (3) a quantitative inventory of the CS (471 textual units: 403 hymns, 34 antiphons, 34 biblical texts); (4) identification of melodic source traditions; and (5) a musical analysis of the 8 selected hymns examining modes, ambitus, rhythm, and form.

The critical edition itself (pp. 1–17) presents the 8 hymns in diplomatic transcription with modern notation, followed by editorial notes (pp. 18–19) and a critical apparatus (pp. 20–22).

## Key Arguments
- Třanovský is identified as "probably the most significant Central European hymnist" — author of approximately 180 hymn texts. (p. XII)
- **Source composition of CS:** Melodically, Czech and Moravian sources dominate — nearly 30 different Bohemian hymnological sources have been identified as melodic antecedents. German repertoire comes primarily from the first half of the 16th century, dominated by the earliest Lutheran sources (*Eyn Enchiridion*, Erfurt 1524; *Eyn geystlich Gesangk Buchleyn*, Erfurt 1524). (pp. XIII–XIV)
- **Třanovský's editorial method:** He drew melodies from older Czech tradition and "was fond of using Calvinist metric melodies" — specifically, French psalm tunes, which are "more frequent in the hymnal than Třanovský's own new melodies." He set his new texts to these existing melodies rather than composing new ones. (p. XV)
- **Connection to Bystrická agenda:** The hymn *Otče Nebeský Bože* contains a solo liturgical intonation whose melody appears in four-part setting in the Banská Bystrica Agenda of 1585. The CS introit *Neyswětegsij* also has textual precedent in the 1585 agenda. This documents a direct liturgical continuity between the mining-town agendas and the CS. (pp. XV–XVI)
- Approximately one quarter of the CS repertoire consists of hymns not found in earlier sources — these are attributed to Třanovský. (p. XIII)
- 8 melodies specifically attributed to Třanovský: *Slysste pobožnj nowinu*; *Genž sy trpěl za nás*; *Poděkůgmež Krystu Pánu*; *Otče Nebeský Bože*; *Ay diwná Maudrost, dobrota*; *Otče swětel, děkugemeť*; *Rozhněwal se můg milý Pán*; *GEžjssy dárce milosti*. (p. XVI)

## Notable Quotes
> "Hymns about the Passion, some of the prayer hymns, and hymns about the Lord's Supper" received "a key of minor character" while "Christmas or prayer hymns have a major character." (p. XVII, paraphrased from bilingual text)
- Context: Suggests a correlation between theological theme and tonal affect in Třanovský's original compositions — relevant for §5 analysis of how theological content shaped musical setting.

> "[Třanovský] drew the melodies to the texts of the hymns in the hymnal from earlier Bohemian tradition. He was fond of using Calvinist metric melodies. It was his new texts that he would mostly furnish with melodies of French psalms." (p. XV, English column)
- Context: Demonstrates Třanovský's eclectic method — drawing on Calvinist melodic material even within a firmly Lutheran confessional framework. This is directly relevant to the "Lutheranisation" thesis: the melodic layer could carry confessionally "foreign" material while the text was theologically controlled.

## Relation to TEPALIT Chapter
- **Which locus/section:** Primarily §5 (Transfer and Reception); secondarily §4.1 (Eucharist — through the Lord's Supper hymn among the 8 selected)
- **How it supports our argument:** Provides hard data on the source composition of CS — the dominance of Czech/Moravian melodic sources confirms that Třanovský was systematically drawing from Czech Reformation tradition. The use of Calvinist metric melodies alongside Lutheran textual content supports the thesis of selective theological filtering: Třanovský was willing to borrow musical material across confessional lines while maintaining doctrinal control through the text. The connection to the Bystrická agenda (1585) demonstrates a liturgical continuity chain from neo-Utraquist practice through mining-town Lutheran adaptation to the CS.
- **Points of agreement:** Supports the core thesis that CS represents a theological mediation between Czech and Lutheran traditions.
- **Points of disagreement or tension:** The study is *musicological* in orientation — it does not analyse the theological content of the hymns. Grešová-Sekelská's focus is on melodic attribution and formal analysis, not on doctrinal comparison. For §4.1 and §5, we need to go beyond her work to examine the *textual* theology of the hymns, which she does not address.
- **Limitation for our purposes:** The critical edition includes only 8 hymns attributed to Třanovský's own composition. For the transfer/reception analysis in §5, we need the hymns Třanovský *adapted* from Czech sources — i.e., the ones where he changed someone else's text. These are not covered here. Grešová-Sekelská's dissertation (2020) may contain more on this; the *Príspevok* article should also be checked.
