## Summary

A two-lecture biographical, historiographical, and theological study of Lukáš Pražský (Luke of Prague, c. 1458–1528), the architect of mature Unity-of-Brethren theology. Crews — long-time Archivist of the Moravian Church Southern Province, translator of Říčan's *History of the Unity of Brethren* (1992), and the principal English-language interpreter of Brethren theology — wrote this text as the Walter Vivian Moses Lecture in 1997, published it in *The Hinge* in 2005, and republished it in *JMH* in 2018. The article remains the single most accessible English-language overview of Luke's life, leadership, and theological method.

The historiographical section (pp. 2–5) is itself substantively important: Crews documents how thoroughly the Bohemian Reformation, and Luke specifically, have been neglected in mainstream Reformation scholarship. He cites Dickens & Tonkin's *Reformation in Historical Thought* (1985) confessing — at p. 331 in a section called "Sins of Omission" — that they had not gotten around to the Bohemian Brethren. Crews's verdict: "the Czech Reformation, and the Unitas Fratrum in particular, have much to contribute, not only for the historical record . . . but also for what they have to say to the churches today" (p. 2). The body of the article makes this case through Luke's biography (pp. 5–18), his theological method and standing (pp. 18–25), the essential / ministrative / incidental architecture (pp. 25–28), Luke on Scripture (pp. 28–31), Luke on the Church (pp. 31–35), Luke and the catechism (pp. 35–40), and Luke's hymns (pp. 40–43).

For the TEPALIT chapter, the article is *foundational across all four loci*. It supplies the structural framework (essentials / ministratives / incidentals) without which Brethren theology cannot be read coherently; it documents the substantive disagreements with Luther in the 1520s on Eucharistic presence, the number of sacraments, *sola scriptura*, and the relationship of faith to works; it shows how Luke's hymns enter the German-language Brethren tradition through Michael Weisse's 1531 *Gesengbuchlein*, providing a documented Czech → German hymnological transfer route that pre-figures the Czech → Slovak transfers analysed in §5; and it provides the methodological vocabulary (koinonia, *semper reformanda*, theology as ministrative-of-the-church-for-the-church) that the chapter's own *lex orandi* lens can engage productively.

## Key Arguments

### Essential, ministrative, and incidental things — the architecture (pp. 25–28)

Luke's structural backbone is the threefold distinction between essential, ministrative, and incidental things, and Crews quotes Luke's own claim that "all errors flow from misunderstanding of the difference between essential and ministrative things or from erroneous definition of their content or reciprocal action" (p. 25, citing Molnár 1992 in Říčan p. 406).

The **essentials** are twofold (p. 26):
- **Divine** essentials: (1) the grace of God [the Father] who wills our salvation; (2) the saving work of God the Son in Christ; (3) the gifts of the Holy Spirit.
- **Human** essentials: (1) faith; (2) love; (3) hope.

Crews flags an interpretive caution that the chapter must take seriously: "these essentials were never a list of specific doctrines, whether five, seven, eight, or a thousand, to which the Christian was expected to swear allegiance. Rather, they were objective attitudes and actions on the part of God to which humans by grace respond in a living relationship" (p. 26). The "human essentials" are response; therefore the "divine essentials" are operative cause.

The **ministratives** are Scripture, the Church and its servants, and the sacraments. They are not unimportant — they are the divinely-appointed means of salvation — but they are *tools*, not ends. The **incidentals** are specifics of church order, type of bread used at communion, and so on. Confusing ministratives with essentials is a "grave mistake"; confusing incidentals with essentials is "a tragedy of the highest order, as portions of the Christian Church over the centuries have proved time and time again" (p. 26).

This three-tier architecture is the *single most consequential framework* the chapter inherits from Crews. Brethren positions on the Eucharist, the church, Scripture, and the sacraments are unintelligible — and easily caricatured — without it. The chapter's §4 cannot speak about Brethren confessional identity in any of the four loci without invoking it.

### Luke on the Church — koinonia, multiple *jednoty*, *semper reformanda* (pp. 31–35)

Crews's account of Luke's ecclesiology is the densest single locus in the article and the largest single deposit for §4.2 of the chapter. Five interlocking claims:

1. **Both essential and ministrative church.** In the strict ministrative sense, the church on earth is a tool. But Luke also speaks (drawing on Wyclif and Hus) of an *essential* church as "the assembly of all the elect" — past, present, future — discerned by faith and hope, not by sight. The two senses are kept analytically distinct: the church as Bride of Christ (essential) vs. the church as net cast in the sea (ministrative, mixed-character — citing Chelčický via Luke's *O původu církve*, p. 32).

2. **Communal/koinonia nature of the church is fundamental.** Crews quotes Molnár's interpretive summary of Luke's view: "the life of faith cannot be lived in solitude. The communion of saints shakes a person loose from personal isolationism" (p. 32, Crews's English of Molnár, *Bratr Lukáš*, 75; the Czech in Crews fn. 131 is Molnár's own formulation, not a verbatim Luke quote). And: "Luke, then, reduces the communal nature of the Christian profession to joint common listening to the tidings of Scripture, joined with common binding of selves to obedience" (p. 33, Crews's English of Molnár). The point is theological-principial, not merely a personal preference of Luke's: it was theological *necessity* that brought him to the Unity in 1481.

3. **Multiple *jednoty* (denominations) within one true church.** "The church is one, but it is composed of many 'unities' (jednoty) — what we might call denominations today. Thus the Brethren, the Utraquists, the Romans, etc. are all part of the one true church of Christ, so long as they have their foundation in the truths proclaimed in the Apostles Creed and the essential and ministrative truths of the faith" (p. 33). Crews paraphrases Molnár: "no theologian could find a better explanation of Christian ecumenicity" (p. 33). And Luke against Roman exclusivism (in *Česká Apologia* 302): "The Roman church is not universal, so that outside it God would not have any membership or salvation" (p. 33, citing Molnár's Czech of Luke).

4. ***Semper reformanda***. From *O obnovení církve svaté*: "The renewing of the church not only ought to be, but must be" (p. 33, Crews's English of Luke preserved in Molnár, *Bratr Lukáš*, 78). And: "According to Brother Luke the church is always to be reformed (semper reformanda). It is always necessary anew to examine whether it is in accord with the measuring stick of the essential and ministrative Christian truths, found in holy Scripture and the apostolic confession of faith" (p. 34, citing Molnár).

5. **Theology is ministrative.** Theology is "a human attempt at interpreting revelation" and "cannot contain [that revelation] in all its fullness" (p. 35, citing Molnár). Luke submitted his theological work to the Unity, "not as to a Catholic imprimatur, but rather this was an expression of the fact that the theologian is of the church and for the church" (p. 35).

### Luke on Scripture — ministrative, mediated, communal (pp. 28–31)

Scripture is a ministrative thing — "and at first glance, it may seem amazing to some that the 'formal principle' of the Reformation [*sola scriptura*] appears to occupy a secondary place in the theology of Luke" (p. 28). But ministrative ≠ unimportant. Among the ministratives, Scripture is the *only* one that is absolutely necessary (the others can be provisional in cases of persecution, for example). From *O gruntu viery* (cited Crews p. 28, citing Molnár p. 82f.):

> "Without the preaching of the holy reading no one recognizes the establishment of salvation nor comes into it from faith. [Therefore] we preach Scripture before all the sacraments, and with the sacraments, and after the sacraments so that first of all we may instruct people in the essence of faith and penance by the reading [i.e. Scripture]." (Luke, *O gruntu viery*; cited Crews p. 28)

But Luke draws a sharp Word/word distinction. Crews quotes Luke (via Molnár): "the biblical word is not the immediate Word of God" (p. 28, Crews fn. 118 citing Molnár, *Bratr Lukáš*, 82f.). Crews himself glosses immediately: *Jesus alone is the Word.* The biblical word "is indeed only for testimony to the revelation of the Word of God, which in Christ — and only in Him — became flesh" (p. 29, Crews's English of Molnár's Czech, fn. 119). For Luke, Christ is present in Scripture, but he is present *as proclamation*. Crews's gloss: "So much for those who would exalt Scripture almost to the point of idolatry. Here again Luke worried about Luther's exaltation of Scripture, fearing that it might indeed lead some to just this sort of idolatry" (p. 29).

Two further moves matter for the chapter. First, Luke distinguishes (writing to Vavřinec, 1519) "between the external writing of the law, with ink on paper or parchment, and the external reading of it, and . . . the internal truths contained in it" (p. 29, citing Molnár). The book can burn; the truth witnessed cannot. Second, on biblical languages: Luke is content with the Vulgate, has "a rancorous distaste for education in the biblical tongues" (p. 30, citing Molnár), is suspicious of "newcomers" (*nováků*) who recently learned Greek and Hebrew. He pays attention to "the time, the person, the places, and the motives of the biblical narratives and history" (p. 30, citing Molnár of Luke's *Zprávy kněžké* p. 9) — i.e., not a literalist, but a contextual reader within tradition.

### Luke and Luther — substantive 1520s correspondence and its limits (pp. 16–18, 22–24)

The Luke–Luther exchange is the single most important moment of inter-confessional contact in the article and the one most directly relevant to the chapter's §4.1 framing of Brethren-vs.-Lutheran Eucharistic theology. Crews documents it carefully (pp. 16–18, 22–24).

**Initial contact, early 1520s.** Through Jan Roh and Michael Weisse as messengers, Luke and Luther exchanged ideas. Luther had differences with the Brethren on:
- **rebaptism** (Brethren practice; Luther opposed);
- **the nature of Christ's presence in the Eucharist** (Crews p. 17, no further detail given here but the disagreement is named);
- **emphasis on works** (Luther concerned about Brethren's discipline-emphasis);
- **continuing celibacy of the clergy** (Brethren retained, Luther opposed).

**Luke's calm response.** Crews, citing Říčan: Luke's "response and critique was carried on with the calm self-assurance of the old teacher who was able to deliver superior teachings and church orders, which had been tested in adversity, to a young comrade who had only recently begun to make his way out of the Roman Babylon to a better knowledge of the truth" (p. 18, citing Říčan). Crews flags: "I repeat that Luther took this with surprising good grace" (p. 18). The contact was discontinued after 1524.

**Theological substance — the "modes of being" of Christ (pp. 22–23).** This is the most substantively important fragment for the chapter's §4.1. In a 1507 document Luke schematised Christ's modes of being as: (1) Personal (in heaven); (2) Spiritual (in the believer); (3) Powerful (in the church); (4) Sacramental (in the sacraments) (p. 22). By 1520 (in *O Pokoloně*) and especially in his 1523 reply to Luther, Luke had refined this into a more elaborate scheme that distinguishes "essential" from "ministrative" being, with the ministrative including "the word of the reading [Scripture]" and the sacraments (baptism, laying on of hands, Lord's Supper) (p. 22). Crews flags Molnár's count of "at least eight variations" of this scheme across Luke's various works (p. 22, fn. 93, citing Molnár, *Bratr Lukáš*, 50–54). Crucially, "such mathematical and geometrical schemes of organization were dear to Luke, but he did not see any such scheme as a rigid fixed formula: 'It was rather a tool, whose imperfection attempted to elucidate what he well-understood in his heart: namely, that Christ is present in the church and in believers'" (p. 22, fn. 94, Crews's English of Molnár, *Bratr Lukáš*, 54).

**The seven-vs.-two-sacraments exchange (p. 24).** Luther urged the Brethren to abandon the "papist" seven sacraments and return to the "biblical" pattern of baptism and Lord's Supper. Luke's reply is theologically deft: in the Vulgate (Eph 5:32 — *Sacramentum hoc magnum est*), the *only* rite explicitly named *sacramentum* is **marriage**. Neither baptism nor Holy Communion is termed *sacramentum* in the Vulgate. Crews: "I am not aware of Luther's reply, if any, to this" (p. 24).

For the chapter's §4.1: this is direct evidence that the Brethren-Lutheran Eucharistic disagreement is not merely about real presence vs. spiritual presence, but operates within a different *theological-grammatical* framework altogether. Luke's scholastic-derived essential/ministrative schema treats the Lord's Supper as one ministrative locus among several where Christ is present, and the chapter cannot reduce this to a real-presence-vs.-symbolism axis without misrepresenting Brethren self-understanding.

### Luke's hymns — German transmission and the Czech → DE → SK pre-figuration (pp. 40–43)

The hymnological section is direct preparation for §5. Three findings:

1. **Luke wrote many hymns and they enter the Unity's hymnals immediately.** Eleven of the pieces in the 1501 hymnal are by Luke (p. 40). The 1505 hymnal "has a better claim to the title of 'first hymnal of the Unity'" with Luke definitely involved as editor (p. 14, citing Říčan p. 103f.). Crews flags Molnár's observation that Luke's hymns appear in Václav Miřínský's 1492 collection too — meaning Luke began writing hymns soon after joining the Unity in 1481 (p. 40).

2. **Michael Weisse 1531 as the German conduit.** Crews identifies Weisse 1531 as the route by which Luke's hymns enter the German Brethren tradition (pp. 41–43). Specifically: "Now lay we calmly in the grave" (Catharine Winkworth's 1858 English translation) is based on Weisse's 1531 German version of a Luke original — the only Luke hymn in the 1923 American Moravian and 1912 British Moravian hymnals. And "Christus, der uns selig macht" (Weisse 1531 German) is itself a translation of Luke's "Moudrost boha otce prawda," which is *itself* Luke's Czech adaptation of the 14th-century Latin "Patris sapientia veritas divina" (p. 43). The full chain — *Patris sapientia* (Latin, 14th c.) → *Moudrost boha otce prawda* (Luke's Czech) → *Christus der uns selig macht* (Weisse 1531 German) → 18th-c. and 19th-c. English translations — is a fully-documented transfer-and-adaptation case. For the chapter's §5: the Tranovský / *Cithara Sanctorum* transfer to Slovak Lutheran worship in 1636 sits within an older pattern of *Brethren* hymnological traffic between Czech and German contexts, in which the same hymn could be Latin, Czech, and German simultaneously, with different theological communities owning different versions.

3. **The English-language Moravian inheritance is sparse.** This is a meta-observation but methodologically useful for the chapter: even within the Moravian tradition that descends from Luke, only one or two Luke hymns appear in 20th-century English-language Moravian hymnals, and only the 1995 *Moravian Book of Worship* uses a translation directly from the Czech rather than via a German intermediary (p. 43, on Jaroslav Vajda's translation of "Kristus přiklad pokory" → "Christ, the model of the meek"). The point: hymnological reception across language and confessional boundaries is filtered, attenuated, and at times nearly lost. The Slovak Lutheran reception of Czech-Reformation hymnody analysed in §5 is one specific instance of a much wider pattern of attenuated transfer.

### Christ-centered ecclesiology of the cross — Luke on Revelation 5 and the *agnus dei* (pp. 26–27)

A locus that easily goes missed in summary-level engagement with Crews and that the chapter should not lose. In his commentary on Revelation 5, discussed by Crews (pp. 26–27, citing Molnár, *Bratr Lukáš*, 57), Luke insists the church is "mistaken if it wishes to travel the way of glory on this earth." Instead, "the pattern or paradigm for the church on earth can be nothing other than the 'lamb once slain' (*beránek zabitý*)." Christ does live in glory to intercede for us, but Christ is present in the church *as the lamb whose death was the cause of our salvation*. It is not Christ's glory but his "shameful death" which has brought us forgiveness. Crews quotes Luke: "God, however, by the foolishness of the cross established the way for Christians and the church to follow" (p. 27, Crews's English of Molnár's Czech of Luke).

Two consequences for the chapter. (i) **Methodologically:** Luke's cross-ecclesiology is not an incidental flourish; it is the *Christological key* to his church-doctrine, and the chapter's §4.2 should read his ministrative-church / multiple-jednoty model through this Christological lens rather than as ecumenical pragmatism. The church is plural-in-jednoty *because* it is the church of the Lamb-once-slain, not because the Brethren were tactically irenic. (ii) **Iconographically:** Crews speculates (cautiously, with three "or"-questions) that this passage of Luke may be the origin of the Moravian seal's "Our Lamb has conquered; let us follow Him" (Luke's *agnus dei* + admonition to follow being directly continuous with the seal's lamb-with-banner-of-the-cross plus motto). The matter is not settled, but Crews's flag is worth noting if the chapter ever needs to gesture at the iconographic afterlife of Brethren theology in the Moravian tradition. The chapter's §5 argument about hymnological reception across confessional and linguistic boundaries has a parallel in the visual-iconographic register, and Crews's speculation supplies a useful instance.

### Soteriology — grace as basis, response in obedience (pp. 8–9, 17, 38–39, 44–45)

Crews keeps coming back to the soteriological balance Luke struck. The locus classicus is *Bárka* (1493), Luke's first major work, cited via Říčan p. 64:

> "Without his death and the merit of his grace and sharing in his righteousness, no suffering, nor self-denial, nor merit makes for the gaining of salvation." (Luke, *Bárka* 1493; cited Crews p. 9)

This is the early-Luke restatement of grace-priority against the perfectionist impulse of the founding generation. By the early 1520s, Luke is responding to the *opposite* concern. When some Bohemian students returned from Wittenberg spouting slogans that the Brethren's emphasis on discipline was "contrary to the Gospel" (Crews's narration, p. 17, with only the bracketed phrase in his quotation marks), Luke's reaction was caution: a "loud proclaiming of 'salvation by faith alone apart from works of the law' would seem dangerous to them as possibly promoting the idea that faith did not have to produce works as its fruits" (p. 17). Crews's editorial verdict: "Luke had led the rejection of works righteousness in the 1490s, but in discussions with Luther he raised the dangers of faith without works. His concern, as always, was that the flock committed to his care receive proper guidance and help in living the lives to which they were called in faith, love, and hope" (p. 45).

Crews's culminating soteriological statement, in the conclusion: "Indeed, for Luke, grace is always paramount in this. Doing good works depends on grace and, unlike Roman theology, he did not see virtue as a power of our human will. Refusing to do good works is, however, our choice, and the sort of faith that is associated with this refusal is trying to build salvation on false hope. Neither course was acceptable. Similarly, the sacraments are not magic charms for salvation, nor are they merely empty symbols. They are rather manifestations of grace which we, again by grace, may receive" (p. 45). This is the theological position the chapter's §4.3 needs to engage as the Brethren formulation against which both the Lutheran *sola fide* and the Utraquist works-tradition can be calibrated.

## Notable Quotes

> "all errors flow from misunderstanding of the difference between essential and ministrative things or from erroneous definition of their content or reciprocal action." (p. 25, Molnár's English in Říčan p. 406, citing Luke)
- Context: The single sentence that captures Luke's whole structural approach. The chapter's §4 must invoke this principle whenever it juxtaposes Brethren positions on Eucharist, Church, Scripture, or sacraments to Lutheran or Utraquist counterparts.

> "these essentials were never a list of specific doctrines, whether five, seven, eight, or a thousand, to which the Christian was expected to swear allegiance. Rather, they were objective attitudes and actions on the part of God to which humans by grace respond in a living relationship." (p. 26)
- Context: Crews's interpretive framing of the essentials, against the temptation to read Luke's three-divine-and-three-human scheme as a doctrinal checklist. The chapter's §4 should preserve this relational-not-propositional reading of the essentials.

> "Without his death and the merit of his grace and sharing in his righteousness, no suffering, nor self-denial, nor merit makes for the gaining of salvation." (Luke, *Bárka* 1493; cited Crews p. 9 via Říčan)
- Context: Luke's grace-first soteriological statement from his first major work, written precisely against the perfectionist impulse of the founding Brethren generation. The chapter's §4.3 should treat this as the canonical Brethren grace-statement, since it pre-dates the Luther correspondence by nearly three decades and cannot be assimilated to a Lutheran *sola fide* derivation.

> "It is characteristic of the theological thinking of Lukáš that he did not limit himself to the resolution of these individual questions but went beyond them to their underlying principles." (Říčan, cited Crews p. 9)
- Context: Crews's framing of Luke's theological method via Říčan. Luke's habit is to resolve a particular controversy by re-articulating the underlying theological architecture. The chapter's §4 method (taking specific hymn-pairs, liturgical units, or confessional articles and asking what theological architecture they imply) is methodologically continuous with Luke's own.

> "His response and critique was carried on with the calm self-assurance of the old teacher who was able to deliver superior teachings and church orders, which had been tested in adversity, to a young comrade who had only recently begun to make his way out of the Roman Babylon to a better knowledge of the truth." (Říčan, cited Crews p. 18)
- Context: The Brethren self-image vis-à-vis Luther in the early 1520s — the older, more battle-tested tradition addressing the newcomer. The chapter cannot frame Lutheran-Brethren contact as if Luther were the dominant partner; from the Brethren side it was the reverse. This dovetails with Csepregi 2003 on the formal-legal asymmetry between Reich-Lutheranism and Hungarian-Crown Lutheranism: in both cases, the conventional Lutheran-as-protagonist framing of Reformation history flattens the actual power-dynamic of the encounter.

> "the life of faith cannot be lived in solitude. The communion of saints shakes a person loose from personal isolationism." (p. 32, Crews's English of Molnár, *Bratr Lukáš*, 75 — Molnár's interpretive formulation, not a verbatim Luke quote)
- Context: Luke's koinonia-foundational ecclesiology, as Molnár glosses it. Methodologically rich for the chapter's *lex orandi* lens: hymn-singing in particular is a koinonia-act, and the Brethren ecclesial-theological framework gives the chapter a principled reason to read sung-worship as a constitutive theological practice rather than as expressive overflow of doctrines held privately.

> "The renewing of the church not only ought to be, but must be." (Luke, *O obnovení církve svaté*; cited Crews p. 33 via Molnár p. 78)
- Context: Luke's *semper reformanda* — pre-Lutheran, Brethren-formulated, Wycliffite-Hussite-rooted. The chapter should NOT cede *semper reformanda* to a Reformed (Calvin → Karl Barth) genealogy; the Brethren formulation is older and structurally different (church reform as ongoing examination against the essentials/ministratives standard, not as Reformed antithesis to Roman corruption).

> "The Roman church is not universal, so that outside it God would not have any membership or salvation." (Luke, *Česká Apologia* 302; cited Crews p. 33 via Molnár p. 76)
- Context: Luke's denial of Roman exclusive catholicity — but in the form of "the Roman church is one *jednota* among others," not "Rome is unsalvific." This is a more nuanced anti-Roman position than the Lutheran or Reformed equivalents, and aligns surprisingly well with the irenic register that Csepregi 2003 documents for the *Confessio Pentapolitana*.

> "Sacramentum hoc magnum est." (Vulgate, Eph 5:32, on marriage; cited Crews p. 24, citing Luke against Luther)
- Context: Luke's reductio against Luther's call to abandon "papist" seven-sacraments for "biblical" two-sacraments. The chapter's §4.1 should cite this as evidence that the Brethren-Lutheran disagreement on sacrament-count is not a simple Lutheran-vs.-Roman binary but turns on a different theological-grammatical reading of the Vulgate itself.

## Relation to TEPALIT Chapter

- **Which locus/section:** §4.1 (Sacramental doctrine — Brethren spiritual-presence Eucharist; the modes-of-being scheme; the seven-vs.-two-sacraments exchange with Luther); §4.2 (Ecclesiology — the koinonia ecclesiology, the multiple-jednoty model, *semper reformanda*, the apostolic-succession-via-Tůma-and-Eliáš ordination of 1500); §4.3 (Soteriology / Ethics — *Bárka* 1493 grace-statement; the Wittenberg-students episode; works as fruit of grace, not as cause); §4.4 (Authority of Scripture and Tradition — Scripture as ministrative; Word/word distinction; Vulgate-conservatism; the Apostles-Creed-and-early-church-interpretation rule); §3 (Konfesionálny kontext — for the historiographical case Crews makes about the Bohemian Reformation's neglect in mainstream Reformation scholarship); §5 (Transfer a recepcia — Luke's hymns and their German transmission via Weisse 1531 as a documented Czech → German transfer that pre-figures the chapter's central Czech → Slovak case).

- **How it supports our argument.** Crews delivers the *theological architecture* without which the chapter's Brethren positions cannot be coherently presented. The essential / ministrative / incidental distinction is the indispensable framework, and the chapter's §4 must invoke it explicitly the first time each Brethren position enters the comparative grid. Crews's documentation of the 1520s Luke–Luther correspondence gives the chapter a precise, dated, theologically-substantive moment of Brethren-Lutheran contact, which can be set alongside the Czech-confessional documents (CB 1575, Just-Rothkegel 2012 critical ed.) and the Slovak Lutheran reception (CS 1636 + later Levoča editions analysed by Ruščin 2019 and Grešová-Sekelská) to map the *changing* shape of the Brethren-Lutheran encounter across 1520s → 1575 → 1636 → 1674/1684. The hymnological section makes Crews directly load-bearing for §5: the chain *Patris sapientia* → Luke's Czech → Weisse's 1531 German → 18th- and 19th-c. English translations is the cleanest documented case the chapter has of multi-language hymnological transfer with theological adaptation.

- **How it disciplines our argument.** Three disciplines.
  - (1) **The "Lutheranisation" thesis cannot mean assimilation to a normative Lutheran theology**, because Luke's mature Brethren theology is structurally *different* (essentials/ministratives/incidentals architecture; modes-of-being scheme; theology-as-ministrative) rather than *deficiently Lutheran* or *not-yet-Lutheran*. The chapter's working thesis should specify: "Lutheranisation" in the *Cithara Sanctorum* transfer means selective filtering of Brethren content through Lutheran doctrinal commitments (especially on Eucharist, sacraments-count, faith-and-works), not wholesale absorption of Brethren material into a Lutheran framework. Where Brethren material persists in CS, it persists with its own theological-grammatical fingerprint, and the chapter's *lex orandi* analysis should be calibrated to detect this.
  - (2) **The Brethren are not a pre-Lutheran or proto-Lutheran tradition**; they are an older, theologically-self-consistent tradition that Crews documents as *correcting* Luther on works, on Eucharistic presence, on Scripture-exaltation, and on the sacrament-count. The chapter's §4 should not narrate the Brethren in proto-Reformation terms.
  - (3) **The Brethren ecclesiology of multiple *jednoty* is structurally compatible with the Confessio Bohemica 1575's irenic ecumenism**, and the chapter's §4.2 should explore this convergence rather than reducing CB 1575 to a Lutheran-Reformed compromise. Luke's "no theologian could find a better explanation of Christian ecumenicity" reading (Crews p. 33, citing Molnár) is a Brethren resource the CB 1575 architects could plausibly have known and drawn on, given the Brethren's leading role in the CB 1575 negotiations.

- **Methodological resonance.** Three nodes of methodological resonance with the chapter's *lex orandi, lex credendi* lens.
  - **Theology-as-ministrative.** Luke explicitly classifies theology itself among the ministratives, "of the church and for the church" (Crews p. 35, citing Molnár). This is precisely the disposition the chapter's §4 wants to bring to the comparison — confessional dogmatics not as truth-itself but as church-mediated witness to truth, properly examined via lived practice.
  - **Communal listening as the locus of Scripture-reception.** Luke "reduces the communal nature of the Christian profession to joint common listening to the tidings of Scripture, joined with common binding of selves to obedience" (p. 33, Crews's English of Molnár). Hymn-singing is one specific case of this communal-listening-and-binding; the chapter's §5 case study is a study of exactly this phenomenon under conditions of inter-confessional transfer.
  - **Time, person, place, motive.** Luke's hermeneutical rule for Scripture (Crews p. 30, citing Molnár of Luke's *Zprávy kněžké* p. 9) is "He always paid attention to the time, the person, the places, and the motives of the biblical narratives and history." This is structurally identical to the chapter's own commitment to historical-critical contextualisation in Methodology #2.

## Notes

- The article is structurally a 1997 Walter Vivian Moses Lecture, twice-reprinted with editorial light-touch (*The Hinge* 2005, *JMH* 2018). The 2018 JMH version is the *citable* one (peer-reviewed journal, DOI, scholarly-standard pagination), but the *substance* dates to 1997 and is not refreshed against the 1997–2018 wave of Brethren scholarship (Atwood 2009, Haberkern 2016/2023, Crews's own *Faith, Love, Hope* of 2008). The chapter should treat Crews 2018 as a 1997-vintage document with light editorial polish — substantively rich, but not a synthesis of post-2000 Brethren scholarship.

- Crews's historiographical note about the neglect of the Bohemian Reformation in mainstream Reformation scholarship (pp. 2–5, citing Dickens & Tonkin 1985 admitting the omission at p. 331) is itself worth quoting in the chapter's *Úvod* / introduction. The TEPALIT chapter is, in part, a contribution against precisely this neglect, and the Crews / Dickens & Tonkin observation gives the chapter an authoritative third-party warrant for its own framing.

- Several Crews observations are quotable on the Brethren methodological-theological disposition itself, which the chapter can productively engage rather than merely cite. The phrase "the theological process of the Unity was collegial in nature" (p. 44) and the conclusion that "one surprising strength of the Unity was that it had no Luther or Calvin which it canonized as the interpreter of Scriptural truth" (p. 44) together capture the Brethren's distinctive theological mode — collective, dynamic, *semper reformanda*, principled-but-not-dogmatic. This is methodologically important: the chapter cannot read Brethren confessional documents as one reads Lutheran confessional documents (which carry the weight of an authoritative canonical theologian-figure), because the Brethren documents are products of a different theological-procedural culture.

- Several specific data-points from Crews are useful for the chapter and are flagged here for downstream use:
  - **Luke's birth year:** "around 1460" per Müller; "perhaps 1458" per Říčan (p. 5).
  - **Luke's BA at Charles University:** 2 October 1481 (p. 5); graduated 8th in a class of 11 per Molnár.
  - **Luke joins the Unity:** soon after his 1481 graduation (p. 6).
  - **First major work:** *Bárka* (The Ship), 1493 (pp. 8–9).
  - **First *Brethren* hymnal Luke was definitely involved in:** 1505 (p. 14).
  - **Synod of Rychnov:** 1494 — the moderation of the founding generation's restrictions; Luke elected to Inner Council (pp. 9–10).
  - **Italian trip:** 1498 — disappointment with Rome and Waldensians; respect for Mary; introduction of Roman liturgical elements into Brethren practice on return (pp. 12–13).
  - **Consecrated bishop:** 1500 (with Ambrož of Skuteč, by Bishops Tůma and Eliáš) — apostolic-succession evidence for §4.2 (p. 13).
  - **Catechism (*Dětinské otazky*):** 1501 or 1502, 75 questions; preserved in *Otazky trojí* 1523; German translation 1521, printed 1523 (pp. 35–36).
  - ***Apologia sacrae scripturae* (Apology of Sacred Scripture):** printed 1511 (p. 20). Crews summarises Müller's view (no verbatim quote in the article) that this document is the source from which Erasmus and Luther learned the Brethren's theology. This is methodologically important: it is the *primary access route* by which mature Brethren systematic theology entered the Reformation-era European theological conversation; the chapter's §4 should treat *Apologia sacrae scripturae* 1511 as a privileged primary source for what Wittenberg actually saw of Brethren doctrine in the early 1520s. Acquisition: needs a separate verification trail; Crews does not give a critical-edition reference. The 1511 Czech-language print may survive only in research-library holdings.
  - **Persecution:** Mandate of St. James 1508; Luke imprisoned by Petr Suda 1515; relief after 1516 (pp. 15–16).
  - **Tůma dies, Luke becomes head of Inner Council:** 1518 (p. 16).
  - **Erasmus contact:** 1520; non-committal response (p. 16).
  - **Luther correspondence:** early 1520s, ended 1524 (pp. 17–18).
  - **Brethren New Testament translation, 1525:** Luke "expresses great concern" for translation accuracy (p. 30); works from the Vulgate, not Erasmus's Greek. Methodologically significant for §4.4 — the Brethren NT 1525 is a Vulgate-based vernacular translation produced *during* the period of Luke's most intense engagement with Lutheran exegetical reform, which means Brethren scriptural practice in 1525 deliberately diverges from the Wittenberg *ad fontes* programme. The chapter's §4.4 should not assimilate Brethren scriptural practice to a Lutheran-Erasmian Greek-NT framework. Acquisition: standard Czech reference works on Bible translation; Kyas 1997 likely covers it.
  - **Death:** 11 December 1528, "from a condition called 'stone'" (p. 18).
  - **1531 Synod (3 years after Luke's death):** explicitly freed Brethren clergy from being bound to Luke's writings (pp. 20–21). Lutheran-influence period follows.
  - **1546 Synod:** Brethren return to Luke's writings, having been disillusioned with Lutheranism's "eloquence of words" and "fair sweetness of speaking" (p. 22).

- Crews discusses Luke's "modes of being" of Christ (pp. 22–23) at a level of detail that is directly load-bearing for §4.1 of the chapter. The 1507 four-fold scheme (Personal in heaven, Spiritual in believer, Powerful in church, Sacramental in sacraments) and its 1520/1523 revision into an essential-vs.-ministrative scheme are the chapter's *direct* evidence that Brethren Eucharistic theology operates within a different theological-grammatical framework from both Lutheran and Reformed Eucharistic theologies. The chapter's §4.1 should reproduce the 1523 scheme verbatim (Crews p. 22) when introducing the Brethren Eucharistic position, since no shorter summary captures the structural distinctiveness.

- Crews's own institutional position (Moravian Archivist, Southern Province; translator of Říčan; author of *Faith, Love, Hope* 2008) means the article carries a confessional-Moravian framing alongside its scholarly framing. The chapter should be aware of this: Crews is not a neutral historian but a Moravian historian writing for Moravian readers, and a few passages (e.g., the closing pages on contemporary Moravian church reform; the comparison of Luke's lamb-symbol passages to the modern Moravian seal at p. 27) are essentially homiletic. None of this damages the article's substantive scholarly value, but the chapter should distinguish Crews's *historiographical* and *theological-confessional* modes, citing him for the former without subscribing to the latter.

- The Lissa Folios (Crews cites them via Andrew Slaby's translation, held at Moravian Archives Bethlehem) are a substantive primary-source resource for Luke's pastoral letters that Crews uses extensively (Lissa Folio 5 Book 11, Lissa Folio 6 Books 12–14). For PAIR-level verification work and for any chapter section that wants to cite Luke's pastoral letters directly, Lissa Folios access via the Moravian Archives Bethlehem is the canonical path. This is *not* the same as the *Acta Unitatis Fratrum* digitisation at badatelna.eu/fond/1014 already on the project Source Index — the Lissa Folios are the late-medieval and early-modern Brethren manuscript collection now held at Národní archiv Praha (the *Acta UF* per the existing Source_Index entry), but the *Slaby translation* is a separate English-language working translation held in Bethlehem PA. The chapter should flag this for downstream acquisition logistics if any Lissa-Folio-sourced Luke material enters the draft.
