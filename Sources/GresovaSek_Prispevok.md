# Grešová-Sekelská, Adriana — Príspevok k vzniku a obsahu Cithary sanctorum (1636) Juraja Tranovského (2021)

## Citation
GREŠOVÁ-SEKELSKÁ, Adriana: Príspevok k vzniku a obsahu Cithary sanctorum (1636) Juraja Tranovského [On the origin and contents of Georgius Tranoscius' Cithara sanctorum ("Lyre of the Saints," 1636)]. In: *Clavibus unitis* 10/4 (2021), pp. 1–10. VEGA 1/0331/20. Published by Association for Central European Cultural Studies.

## Summary
A peer-reviewed article presenting results of the author's doctoral research (FiF UK 2020). The study reconstructs the first edition of the CS (1636) from four surviving defective copies (Liptovský Mikuláš, Martin, Prague, Valašské Meziříčí) plus a Budapest microfilm of a now-lost fifth copy, and analyses the repertoire's composition by confessional origin, chronological layers, genre, and the extent of Tranovský's original contributions.

The article includes four graphs quantifying: (1) textual origins of CS hymns by source, (2) chronological dating of the repertoire, (3) confessional origin of adopted hymns, and (4) confessional background of the Czech-language repertoire specifically. The study also surveys liturgical books available in Liptov parishes before the CS, based on canonical visitation records from c. 1615.

## Key Arguments

### Reconstruction of the first edition:
- All four surviving copies of the 1636 edition are significantly defective (dozens of missing pages each). The base copy (Liptovský Mikuláš microfilm) was missing 122 hymns. By cross-referencing all copies, 1 complete hymn and 16 partial hymns remain unrecoverable from 1636 copies alone — these can be supplemented from the 1653 edition, the closest subsequent edition. (p. 4)
- Haan's (1875) supposed description of the first edition title page is unreliable — his account does not correspond to the actual first edition. He appears to have been working from later editions (post-1741). "Ani Ľudovít Haan nemal v rukách exemplár z roku 1636." (p. 4)

### Liturgical context in Liptov (c. 1615):
- Canonical visitation records from Superintendent Eliáš Láni (c. 1615) show that churches in the Liptov region contained primarily Catholic liturgical books: Esztergom missals, graduals, psalters, antiphonaries. Lutheran kancionáls were present but not dominant — the Kunvaldský kancionál (1576) was the most frequent. (p. 6)
- The newer Lutheran Závorkov kancionál (1602) is absent from all Liptov churches in the visitation records. (p. 6)
- This implies that Tranovský encountered a worship environment still substantially shaped by Catholic/Esztergom liturgical tradition, which "could have influenced the form of the Cithara sanctorum hymnbook." (abstract, p. 1)

### Content composition — genres:
- The CS contains all parts of the mass ordinary. The practice of *missa brevis* is evident (Kyrie and Gloria as standard; Sanctus, Benedictus, and Agnus Dei only during services with communion). Luther's removal of the offertory is reflected: only introits survive from the proper. The largest group numerically is *ofíciové spevy* (office hymns). (p. 6)

### Melodic sources:
- At least 186 items were notated in the first edition. Nearly 30 different Bohemian hymnological sources identified as melodic antecedents. (p. 6)
- Three most important melodic sources: (1) Utraquist Kolínský kancionál (1517) — the largest single melodic source; (2) Brethren Šamotulský kancionál (1561); (3) Lutheran Kunvaldský kancionál (1576). (p. 6)
- Calvinist psalm melodies: "Tranovský používal rád" (Tranovský used them gladly). His new texts were frequently set to French psalm tunes. These melodies are "dokonca častejšie ako Tranovského vlastné melódie" (even more frequent than Tranovský's own melodies). (p. 6)
- 12 melodies tentatively attributed to Tranovský (revised down from the 8 in the 2023 publication — or rather, the count evolved as research continued). (p. 6)

### Textual sources — **critical data for §5:**
- Czech-language sources dominate. Over 30 printed and manuscript Bohemian sources identified. The most-represented single source is the Lutheran Závorkov kancionál (1602), followed by the Utraquist Kolínský kancionál (1517), then the Brethren Kancionál Jana Roha (1541). (p. 7)
- German hymns: exclusively Lutheran origin. Mostly translations of Luther's own hymns. "Práve Tranovský má najväčšiu zásluhu na rozšírení Lutherových piesní v jazykovom prostredí Čechov, Slovákov a Moravanov" — Tranovský bears the greatest credit for spreading Luther's hymns in the Czech/Slovak linguistic environment. (p. 7)
- Latin hymns: already translated and established pan-European repertoire (Victimae Paschali laudes, In dulci iubilo, etc.). (p. 7)
- Hungarian-origin hymns: primarily from Pribiš's Catechism (1634). (p. 7)
- Approximately one quarter of repertoire attributed to Tranovský himself (185 texts, 14 melodies — abstract). (p. 1)

### Confessional composition — **the key graph (Graf 3, p. 8):**
- Of *adopted* (non-original) hymns: the largest group is **Lutheran**, driven primarily by German translations. Czech/Moravian hymns from Lutheran kancionáls are a minority within this group.
- A significant portion is **Utraquist** in origin.
- Brethren hymns are present but smaller.
- A "Nedefinované" category includes unidentified hymns, hymns from confessionally ambiguous sources, and early Christian Latin hymns.

### Confessional background of the Czech repertoire specifically — **Graf 4 (p. 8):**
- **49% of the older Czech repertoire comes from Utraquist sources.** ("Až 49 % staršieho českého repertoáru pochádza z prostredia utrakvistov.")
- This raises "the natural question of the mutual relationship between Utraquism and Lutheranism, or the question of their mutual influence." (p. 8)
- The article discusses Luther's relationship to Hus and the Utraquist tradition — including Luther's reuse of the Czech hymn *Jesus Christus unser Heiland* from the Hussite milieu (in Eyn Enchiridion, 1524, marked "Das Lied S. Johannes Hus gebessert"). (p. 9)

### Tranovský's editorial interventions — **directly supports "Lutheranisation" thesis:**
- The article distinguishes between **confessionally neutral rubrics** (e.g., Advent hymns, Lenten/Passion hymns, hymns before/after meals) where Tranovský drew freely from older Czech sources with minimal intervention, and **confessionally sensitive rubrics** (e.g., "O Wečeří Páně" / Lord's Supper hymns, "O některých hlavních artykulích víry křesťanské" / on chief articles of faith) where Tranovský either composed hymns himself or drew almost exclusively from Lutheran sources. (pp. 8–9)
- **Concrete example of theological editing:** The hymn *Ay Panna gest pozdravená* originally appeared in the Kolínský kancionál (1517) with the text *Zdrava genž sy pozdrawena*, which "evokes the well-known Marian prayer." Tranovský altered the text "aby sa predišlo akýmkoľvek alúziám či náznakom katolicizmu" (to prevent any allusions or hints of Catholicism). The altered version first appears in CS — it is a Tranovský intervention. (p. 9)
- The rubric on the Blessed Virgin Mary contains 5 hymns: 4 are Tranovský originals and 1 is from the Lutheran Závorkov kancionál. Tranovský would not accept existing Czech hymns on Mary because they carried Catholic resonances. (p. 9)
- Part Three of the CS has the highest percentage of Tranovský's original compositions — precisely because these are the hymns dealing with confessionally sensitive doctrinal topics. (p. 9)
- Two "Calvinistic errors" from Czech hymnals slipped through Tranovský's Lutheran filter into the CS — noted but not specified. (referenced from Mocko, via p. 9 context)

### Chronological layers:
- Almost half of the repertoire dates from the first half of the 16th century — both Czech and German hymns. The core of the CS crystallised over preceding centuries. (p. 7)
- German repertoire dominated by the earliest Lutheran sources (Johann Walter's hymnals). By the time Tranovský compiled the CS, many of these were nearly 100 years old. Tranovský "zjavne preferoval preberanie staršieho repertoáru pred textami svojich súčasníkov" (clearly preferred adopting older repertoire over texts by his contemporaries). (p. 8)
- The CS dramatically expanded the presence of German Lutheran hymns in the Czech/Slovak linguistic environment. Before the CS, these hymns were minimally present; after it, they became standard. (p. 8)

## Notable Quotes
> "Až 49 % staršieho českého repertoáru pochádza z prostredia utrakvistov." (p. 8)
- Context: Nearly half of the older Czech-language hymns in the CS are Utraquist in origin. This is the quantitative foundation for the "Lutheranisation" argument: Tranovský was working with substantially Utraquist raw material and subjecting it to Lutheran confessional filtering.

> "Aby sa predišlo akýmkoľvek alúziám či náznakom katolicizmu, text piesne bol pozmenený." (p. 9)
- Context: On the hymn *Ay Panna gest pozdravená* — Tranovský altered the text to eliminate Catholic resonances. A concrete, documented example of theological editorial intervention.


## Relation to TEPALIT Chapter
- **Which locus/section:** §5 (Transfer and Reception) — primary; §4.1 (Eucharist — the "O Wečeří Páně" rubric is identified as confessionally sensitive, meaning Tranovský intervened here); §2 (Confessional Context — Utraquist-Lutheran relationship discussion)
- **How it supports our argument:** This article provides the **quantitative backbone** for the Lutheranisation thesis. The data showing 49% Utraquist origin of older Czech repertoire, combined with the documentation of Tranovský's differential editorial strategy (hands-off for confessionally neutral hymns, heavy intervention for confessionally sensitive ones), is precisely the pattern our chapter argues for. The concrete example of *Ay Panna gest pozdravená* demonstrates the mechanism of theological filtering at the textual level. The identification of the Lord's Supper rubric as a confessionally sensitive area where Tranovský composed or drew from Lutheran sources directly connects to §4.1.
- **Points of agreement:** Strongly confirms the thesis. Grešová-Sekelská's data and her distinction between neutral vs. sensitive rubrics is exactly the framework our dogmatic analysis can build on.
- **Points of disagreement or tension:** The article remains primarily musicological/hymnological in orientation — it identifies the pattern but does not perform the dogmatic analysis of *what* was changed in the confessionally sensitive hymns. That is our chapter's distinctive contribution. She also does not engage deeply with the Utraquist or Brethren theological self-understanding — the 49% Utraquist figure is stated but not theologically interpreted.
- **Critical methodological point:** She notes that "younger editions of the CS already altered Tranovský's accompanying text" in the 17th century, confirming that working from the 1636 first edition (even in reconstructed form) is essential. Later editions cannot be taken as representing Tranovský's own theological decisions.
