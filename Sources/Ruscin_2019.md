# Ruščin, Peter — Nové nápevy levočských vydaní Cithary Sanctorum z rokov 1674 a 1684 (2019)

## Summary

A specialised musicological study of the third and fourth notated Levoča editions of *Cithara Sanctorum* (CS 1674, edited by Jeremiáš Lednický; CS 1684, edited by Daniel Sinapius-Horčička), produced at the Ústav hudobnej vedy SAV — the home institution of the principal Slovak hymnological journals and a major venue for the secondary literature on which the chapter's §5 will rely. Ruščin's central thesis: the two editions reveal *contrasting editorial logics* in Slovak Lutheran hymnology under the post-1671 persecution. CS 1674 is stylistically progressive and forward-looking, integrating both newer German hymn melodies and a substantial body of new domestic-origin tunes (some composed in the high-baroque idiom). CS 1684 is conservative and retrospective, confining its 21 genuinely-new domestic-origin tunes to the older early-baroque layer that had been transmitted in regional manuscript culture for decades.

The article's evidentiary base is comparative: 43 newly-notated entries in CS 1674 (40 truly new vs. CS 1653) and 30 newly-notated entries in CS 1684 (21 truly new vs. all earlier Slovak sources). Ruščin tracks each through MHB (*Melodiarium hymnologicum Bohemiae*, online clavmon.cz), Zahn (German hymnody concordance), older Czech kancionály (Mirinský 1522, Roh 1541, Závorka 1606, Karolides 1620, Rozenplut 1601), the Catholic *Cantus Catholici* (1655), the Genevan Psalter, and — most consequentially for the §5 argument — Spiš/Upper-Hungarian manuscript hymnals (Ľubický spevník; Spevník Mikuláša Simonidesa z Veľkej, 1685; Spevník zo Smolníckej Huty; Kruczayho spevník; Banskobystrický kancionál 1623; Vietoris tabulatúra; Prešovský graduál 1635). Both editions are placed within Kačic's periodisation of Slovak baroque (early baroque c. 1600/1620–1670; high baroque c. 1670–1720/30) and Sehnal's four-stratum classification of Czech baroque kancionál song.

The article confirms — and significantly complicates — the picture of Slovak Lutheran kancionál culture as a recipient tradition. Across two decades of religious persecution, two editors made fundamentally different curatorial choices about the same hymn-book; both choices reveal a *productive* relationship to a domestic, manuscript-borne hymnological substrate that has no direct equivalent in Bohemia proper. The transfer of melodic material into CS is multi-directional: Czech print → Slovak print is only one of at least four operative routes, alongside German-multi-voice manuscript → Slovak monophony, Catholic kancionál (CC 1655) → Lutheran CS, and Hungarian-Reformed Prešov gradual → Slovak CS.

## Key Arguments

### Two editions, two editorial strategies (pp. 49–79, esp. pp. 67, 79)

Ruščin's framing argument runs through the entire article: CS 1674 and CS 1684 look from outside like sequential moments in the same hymn-book history, but on close musical analysis they embody divergent editorial priorities under the same political pressure.

**Lednický's 1674 edition** (pp. 49–67) absorbs progressive high-baroque elements — duple metre, mannerist dotted rhythms, hints of musical rhetoric (anabasis, katabasis), three-voice trio settings, dramatic recitative-style writing as in Lednický's own dialogue song *Ó bolest! Ó žalost!* (CS 1674, dodatok). About two-thirds of the 40 genuinely-new melodies are presumed domestic in origin (attested only in Hungarian/Upper-Hungarian sources, with no parallel in foreign printed kancionály). Seven derive from German hymnody, four from older Czech sources, and two from *Cantus Catholici* (1655). Ruščin's verdict on the edition: it "demonstrated the vitality of our musical tradition by emphasising its progressive and dynamising features (and perhaps also the compositional ambitions of the editor)" (p. 79, my translation).

**Horčička's 1684 edition** (pp. 68–79) reverses this stylistic vector. Of 30 newly-notated melodies, only 21 are genuinely new (i.e., not previously notated in any Slovak source); seven of those 21 derive directly from CC 1655, leaving only ~10 that can be regarded as fresh domestic-tradition contributions. And those 10 are stylistically conservative — early-baroque, often pre-baroque-derived, characteristically transmitted in 17th-century regional manuscripts long before they entered print. Horčička's edition, Ruščin writes, "looks back into [the tradition's] more glorious past" (p. 79, my translation).

The contrast is not merely a quirk of editorial taste; Ruščin gives it a sociological reading. Horčička's generation, having lived through the 1671–1681 illegality of Lutheranism in Upper Hungary, faced "the existential problems and the effort to renew the tradition of Lutheran ecclesial song, the continuity of which had been seriously disrupted" (p. 79, my translation). The 1684 edition is restoration-by-tradition-mining, not innovation. The same persecution-pressure that produced Lednický's stylistic forward-leaning produced, ten years later, Horčička's stylistic look-backward.

### The Spiš manuscript-print loop (pp. 60–61, 73–77)

The most consequential evidence for the chapter's §5 framing is Ruščin's documentation that several CS 1674 melodies *originated as descant lines of multi-voice German chorales* preserved in Spiš manuscript kancionály — Ľubický spevník, Spevník Mikuláša Simonidesa z Veľkej (1685), Spevník zo Smolníckej Huty, Kruczayho spevník — and that the Lednický edition *lifted those descants* and applied them to Czech-language texts. Three named cases:

- **Důvod svůj má z Boha** — descant of the four-voice setting of *Lob sei Gott und Ehre* in three Spiš manuscripts (Ľubický spevník fol. 135; Spevník Mikuláša Simonidesa z Veľkej fol. 207–208; Kruczayho spevník s. 194–195). The German source is *not* attested outside Hungary; it is a Spiš regional production (p. 60).
- **Ó Bože náš, slyš nás hříšných** — descant of the four-voice setting of *O Herre Gott straf mich nicht in deinem Zorn* (Ľubický spevník fol. 198–199; Simonides fol. 227–228; Smolnícka Huta s. 285–286; Kruczayho s. 266–267). Attested in Transylvania earlier; absent from non-Hungarian sources (p. 60).
- **Jezu Kriste jenž studnice** (Czech translation of Bartolomeus Ringwaldt's *Herr Jesu Christ, du höchstes Gut*) — the CS 1674 melody differs from contemporary German printed versions but matches the descant of the four-voice setting in three Spiš manuscripts (p. 60).

Ruščin draws the methodological inference (pp. 60–61): when faced with the question of whether a single-voice melody preceded a choral setting or vice versa, "we are inclined rather toward the second possibility." That is — the CS 1674 editor was reading off descant lines from a four- or five-voice German manuscript culture and reframing them as congregational unison melodies for Slovak Lutheran use. This is hymnological transfer at the level of *interleaved manuscript and print, German and Slovak, four-voice and one-voice* — none of which is captured by a simple "Czech source → Slovak reception" model. The chapter's §5 transfer-and-reception narrative has to accommodate this fourth route.

### Editorial intervention is the rule, not the exception (pp. 56–58, 73–77)

Ruščin documents specific editorial interventions in *melody* — not just text — across both editions. Lednický transposed *Anjelové, ti poslové* to G (versus the standard A in Czech sources and CC 1655) and shortened the opening melisma; only one parallel transposition is attested in MHB — the Ivančický kancionál 1564 (Brethren). The case of *Radostná novina, poslyštež jí* is more telling: the CS 1674 melody is the lower line of the CC 1655 melody, suggesting that the CS editor was working from a multi-voice setting rather than a printed monophonic source. Ruščin's verdict: "in editing some melodies, Lednický did not start from printed exemplars but rather from the local tradition, or from manuscript records" (p. 57, my translation).

For Horčička 1684, the analogous evidence is the addition of melodies to two CS 1636 texts that had previously circulated *without* notation — *Slyšte pobožní novinu* and *V den soužení* (p. 76). The melodies attached are conservative (early-baroque); their origin (oral tradition or Horčička's own composition) cannot be settled. But the very act of attaching new melodies to long-established Tranovský texts is, as Ruščin observes (p. 76), "a striking, even if rare, intervention into the basic, hitherto unchanged stock of notated melodies of the kancionál" (my translation).

A further case worth flagging: *Veselým hlasem zpívejme* (pp. 76–77). Tranovský in 1636 noted that the song existed but had its own well-known melody and so did not bother printing one; by Horčička's time the melody apparently had to be re-supplied to the kancionál, suggesting a *generational loss of oral knowledge*. Horčička's notated version corresponds to CC 1655 in transposition (which itself draws from Rozenplut 1601 and the Hlohovský print), but the cadence follows the older Závorka kancionál (1606). The case crystallises the layered manuscript-print-oral transmission model the chapter's §5 needs.

### Cross-confessional permeability with *Cantus Catholici* (pp. 56, 58, 71)

The catechetical assumption that *Cithara Sanctorum* and the Catholic *Cantus Catholici* (1655) operated as confessionally separated hymn-corpora is materially false at the level of melodic transmission. Ruščin documents:

- Two CS 1674 melodies attested only in CC 1655 (in its Slovak or already-Hungarian editions) — i.e., melodies that the Lutheran editor took from the Catholic kancionál.
- Seven of the 21 truly-new CS 1684 melodies are also in CC 1655 — most of them with deeper roots in older Czech kancionály.
- The *Veselým hlasem zpívejme* case above, where Horčička's transposition follows CC 1655 but his cadence follows the older Brethren-Utraquist tradition (Závorka 1606).

The boundary work in CS is not against Catholic *material per se* (some of which is absorbed); it is against specific theological loci — a point made unforgettably by the Horčička 1684 apologetic gloss documented next.

### The 1684 apologetic footnote on a Catholic-derived text (p. 69, fn. 39)

A small but theologically resonant find. Horčička appended an apologetic gloss to the second strophe of *Takť jest tento den slavný* — a song that had circulated in CC 1655, in earlier CS editions, and in pre-Cithara Czech material (Pribiš 1634, Rozenplut 1601). The strophe in question speaks of Christ "leading out" Adam, Eve, and the holy fathers from hell into eternal joy. Horčička's footnote (cited by Ruščin from the 1684 edition) reads:

> "Tohoto mluvení žáden se neboj, neb vzato je z Ozeáše/ Kap. 13. v. 14 1. Kor: 15 v. 54 Žid. 2 v. 15. Nebo Adam/ neb Eva etc. z pekla vyvédeni jsou, ne jakoby v něm skutečne byli, ale že svou vinou sobě ho zaslúžili." (CS 1684; cited Ruščin p. 69 fn. 39)

Ruščin's reading: the apologetic gloss does not respond to Catholic critique of CS but to *internal Lutheran suspicion* that the harrowing-of-hell strophe smuggled in Catholic purgatory doctrine. The need for the gloss arose, he suggests, "from the editor's positive approach to the older song repertoire and reacted to criticism from the ranks of evangelicals themselves" (p. 69 fn. 39, my translation). For the chapter, this is a precise locus where an editorial decision in the kancionál *visibly registers* the confessional pressure that §5 wants to track. The "Lutheranisation" thesis, as the chapter currently formulates it, has to make room for cases where editors *retain* problematic older material *plus* a marginal gloss — i.e., a layered confessional-formation strategy that neither suppresses the older song nor leaves it unmarked.

## Notable Quotes

> "V ich dobe sa Cithara Sanctorum Juraja Tranovského stala symbolom identity slovenských evanjelikov, ktorú politický útlak a prenasledovanie ešte viac posilnili." (p. 47)
- Context: The article's framing claim about CS as confessional-identity object under persecution. The *lex orandi* function the chapter's §5 wants to track — a hymn-book operating as identity-marker in conditions where the formal institutional life of the Lutheran Church was suppressed.

> "Lednického edícia sa javí ako prvý odvážnejší pokus aktualizovať Citharu piesňami domácej, slovenskej tradície, pričom zohľadňuje aj prvé vydanie katolíckeho kancionála." (p. 50)
- Context: CS 1674 as the first ambitious updating of CS — *crucially* by means of domestic Slovak material *and* including melodies from the Catholic CC 1655. Cross-confessional permeability operates within Slovak Lutheran kancionál tradition itself, not just at its margins.

> "Vyzerá to tak, že pri editovaní niektorých melódií Lednický nevychádzal z tlačených predlôh, ale skôr z lokálnej tradície, prípadne rukopisných záznamov." (p. 57)
- Context: The methodological key to understanding hymnological transfer in CS. The editor is working not from printed sources alone but from a manuscript-and-oral substrate of regional tradition. This shifts the §5 transfer-and-reception narrative away from a "print-to-print" model and toward a layered "manuscript / oral / print" model.

> "V otázke, ktorý zdroj bol prvotný, či jednohlasná melódia, ku ktorej vznikla zborová úprava, alebo naopak, viachlasná sadzba, z ktorej bola aplikovaná diskantová melódia, sa prikláňame skôr k druhej možnosti." (pp. 60–61)
- Context: The reverse-flow finding: descant lines of German four-voice settings becoming Slovak kancionál monophonic melodies. For §5 this is decisive — the transfer is German-multivoice → Slovak-monophonic, not just Czech-monophonic → Slovak-monophonic.

> "Otázka, prečo Horčička rozšíril okruh notovaných melódií domáceho pôvodu práve o staršie nápevy, nás vedie k úvahám o vedomom tradicionalizme oslabenej evanjelickej cirkvi v Uhorsku po ťažkom desaťročí ilegality." (p. 79)
- Context: The sociological reading of editorial conservatism as response to persecution. The 1684 edition's archaising style is not editorial taste but ecclesial restoration. For the chapter, this reframes "Lutheranisation through hymnody" as a multi-modal phenomenon: progressive in 1674, restorative in 1684 — both confessional, but with opposite stylistic vectors.

> "Zatiaľ čo Lednického edícia demonštrovala vitalitu našej hudobnej tradície akcentujúc jej progresívne a dynamizujúce črty (a možno aj kompozičné ambície editora), vydanie z roku 1684 sa obzerá späť do jej slávnejšej minulosti." (p. 79)
- Context: The single-sentence formulation of the 1674 vs. 1684 contrast. Quotable for §5.

> "Among the new melodies from the editions of the Slovak hymnbook *Cithara Sanctorum* published in 1674 and 1684, apart from melodies carried over (principally German and Czech) there are also 35 anonymous tunes of domestic origin, attested only in Hungarian sources." (p. 46, English Abstract)
- Context: The aggregate count from the article's English abstract. The 35-tune domestic-origin finding is the single most important quantifiable result and should anchor any §5 statement on the magnitude of the domestic Slovak Lutheran contribution to CS.

## Relation to TEPALIT Chapter

- **Which locus/section:** §5 (Transfer a recepcia — primary location, since Ruščin is musicological-philological work directly on CS reception); §3 (Konfesionálny kontext — for the post-1671 persecution dynamics that condition both editions); §4.4 (Písmo a tradícia — for the Horčička 1684 apologetic gloss as a case where confessional self-positioning operates inside the kancionál's editorial apparatus and engages a Scripture-citation register).

- **How it supports our argument.** Ruščin's two-edition contrast is exactly the kind of fine-grained editorial evidence that the chapter's *lex orandi, lex credendi* lens is designed to surface. The chapter's working thesis — that hymn transfer from Czech-Reformation sources into Slovak Lutheran worship involved *systematic theological adaptation* — gets reinforced and *complicated* by Ruščin in three ways:
    - (1) The Lednický 1674 evidence shows that "Lutheranisation" in the kancionál is not just a filtering of Czech material — it is also a *generative* process: new domestic-origin melodies, drawn partly from regional German Lutheran multi-voice manuscript culture, populate the hymn-book.
    - (2) The Horčička 1684 evidence shows that the *same* confessional-formation pressure can produce an *opposite* stylistic vector — restorative-archaising rather than progressive-current. Methodologically rich for the chapter: editorial conservatism and editorial innovation can both be theological choices, and *lex orandi* analysis must be sensitive to both registers.
    - (3) Cross-confessional borrowing from the Catholic *Cantus Catholici* (1655) is *normal* in CS editorial practice, not exceptional. This raises the temperature on the chapter's question of what "Lutheranisation" actually is. The boundary work in CS is not against Catholic material *per se* (some of which is absorbed); it is against specific theological loci (e.g., purgatory-coded glosses, as Horčička's 1684 footnote shows).

- **How it disciplines our argument.** Ruščin's article disciplines the chapter against any over-simplified "Czech source → Slovak Lutheran reception" model. The transfer is multi-directional: Czech kancionály, German multi-voice manuscript chorales, Catholic kancionál (CC 1655), Hungarian-Reformed Prešov gradual, and a regional manuscript-oral substrate all feed into CS. The chapter cannot speak of "Lutheranisation of Czech material" as if Czech-Reformation sources were the dominant input to all CS editorial practice. By the 1670s and 1680s — i.e., precisely the editions where CS becomes *symbolic* of Slovak Lutheran identity — the most active inputs are German and domestic-Hungarian, not Czech. The chapter's §5 should therefore distinguish between (a) the Tranovský 1636 first-edition layer (where Czech and German Reformation sources dominate) and (b) the post-Tranovský Levoča editions (where domestic Slovak and German-Spiš sources dominate). Sekelská's musicological work on the 1636 layer and Ruščin's work on the 1674/1684 layer are both essential for keeping these chronological strata analytically separate.

- **Methodological resonance.** Ruščin works the same source-critical disentangling that Csepregi 2003 works for the *Pentapolitana* — taking apart a layered reception history (here: 19th-c. Mocko, mid-20th-c. Vajdička, 21st-c. Grešová-Sekelská) and reading individual editorial decisions against contemporaneous regional manuscript culture. This is the exact methodological register the chapter's §5 wants to inhabit. Ruščin's article is the kind of close hymnological work that makes the chapter's broader theological argument empirically responsible.

- **Points of disagreement or tension.** None substantive. Ruščin operates at a level of detail well below the chapter's theological-comparative register; his findings are observation-rich and interpretation-light, which makes them ideal raw material for the chapter to interpret theologically. One small caution: Ruščin uses "evanjelický" throughout to mean "Lutheran" (standard Slovak usage), and the chapter must be careful not to import the 19th-century Slovak nationalist framing of "evanjelický = identitne slovenský" without qualification. The 17th-century Slovak Lutheran kancionál was confessionally Lutheran first; its identity-function for "slovenskí evanjelici" is, in part, a retrospective construction.

- Ruščin's musical examples (Notové príklady 1–14) and image reproductions (Obr. 1–18) are specialist musicological evidence and are not directly transferable to a dogmatic-comparative chapter. But the underlying findings about transposition, ornament, and rhythmic adaptation are theologically resonant: each editorial decision about a melody is also a decision about how the congregation will *sing* the theological content of the text. The chapter's *lex orandi* lens can fold these decisions into the four-loci grid wherever they intersect with confessionally-marked content (Eucharistic hymns, ecclesiology hymns, hymns on Word and Tradition, soteriology/ethics hymns).

- A more adventurous use of Ruščin's findings for the chapter: the 1674 vs. 1684 stylistic contrast suggests that Slovak Lutheran *lex orandi* during 1674–1684 was not theologically static but oscillated between progressive (high-baroque, expressively exposed) and restorative (early-baroque, archaising) registers, both deployed in the service of confessional formation. This is potentially a model for how the chapter might treat *lex orandi* dynamics more generally — not as a single confessional voice but as a tradition with internal stylistic-theological tensions that confessional pressure renders visible rather than resolves.
