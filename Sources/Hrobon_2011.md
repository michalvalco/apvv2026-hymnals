# Hroboň, Bohdan — The Kralice Bible: Czech-mate to the KJV (2011)

## Citation
HROBOŇ, Bohdan: The Kralice Bible: Czech-mate to the KJV. In: *Word & World* 31/3 (Summer 2011), pp. 279–286. Open access at [wordandworld.luthersem.edu](https://wordandworld.luthersem.edu/wp-content/uploads/pdfs/31-3_Bible_Translation/The%20Kralice%20Bible;%20Czech-mate%20to%20the%20KJV.pdf). Author affiliation at time of publication: founder and dean of the Lutheran Bible School in Martin, Slovakia (now the Center for Christian Education).

## Summary

An essay-format piece for *Word & World* — Luther Seminary's pastoral and theological journal — that traces the history of Czech-language Bible translation from Cyril and Methodius (863) through the Leskovec Bible (1360), Jan Hus (1369–1415), and the Reformation context, culminating in the *Bible kralická* (KB) of 1579–1594. The article frames the KB as the Czech-language analogue to Luther's German Bible and the King James Version, and argues that all three translations represent a *kairos* — a "rare and unique historical opportunity" — produced by the convergence of social, ecclesiastical, and economic factors.

The piece is not peer-reviewed historical-critical scholarship but a popular-academic synthesis aimed at a Lutheran pastoral readership. It draws heavily on Kyas's *Česká Bible v dějinách národního písemnictví* (Praha: Vyšehrad, 1997) for the historical narrative and on the standard secondary literature (Cambridge History of the Bible; Deanesly's *The Lollard Bible*; Britannica entries) for context. Its scholarly weight for the chapter lies less in original research than in two specific factual anchors: Souček 1933's analysis of the KB's theological notes (cited in fn. 18 — "the Brethren were clearly Protestants, more Calvinist than Lutheran in their theology"), and the article's framing of Slovak Lutheran liturgical use of the KB Czech text "until the second half of the nineteenth century" (fn. 20).

The author's biography is itself useful context. Hroboň is a Slovak Lutheran scholar working at the Center for Christian Education in Martin, the same town that houses the SNK collections relevant to *Cithara Sanctorum* and *Augustínová 2011*. He writes from inside the Slovak Lutheran tradition, which means his framing of the KB as the trans-confessional Slovak-Czech Protestant Scripture both reflects and reinforces the Slovak Lutheran self-narration the chapter has to engage. The article should be read as a primary witness to that self-narration as well as a secondary source for the historical narrative.

## Key Arguments

### The pre-Reformation arc as preparation for KB (pp. 279–283)

Hroboň's narrative arc places the KB at the end of a six-century Czech-language Scripture tradition. He begins with the Salonika brothers' 863 mission and Methodius's translation of "the rest of the Bible into Old Church Slavonic" (p. 280) — preserved through Bulgarian, Russian, Romanian, Serbian, and Croatian liturgies after Methodius's students were expelled from Moravia in 885. The 1054 schism imposed Latin dominance, but oral Old Slavonic tradition survived and revived in the thirteenth century as a tool against "popular misconceptions and heresies" (p. 280, citing Kyas 1997, p. 31).

The thirteenth- and fourteenth-century cloister-women translations led to the Leskovec Bible (1360) — the first complete Czech Vulgate translation, dramatic in its preservation history (the manuscript burned by German troops in Leuven, 1914, with only one third copied just hours before the fire; p. 281, fn. 4 citing Vraštil 1933). Charles IV's 1369 prohibition of vernacular Bible reading by laypeople was motivated, Hroboň argues, "not [by] the translation itself (the Leskovec Bible contained no heterodox readings), but [by] its possible misinterpretation by theologically untrained people" (p. 282, citing the English translation in Deanesly's *The Lollard Bible* p. 84).

The early-fifteenth-century "Bible fashion" produced beautifully decorated luxury Bibles "very rarely read" (p. 282) — at which point Hus enters as the rupture: "He vigorously opposed multiple papal bans to read Scripture in the vernacular and promoted Czech translations in his popular sermons at the reformist Bethlehem Chapel from 1402" (p. 282). Over fifty complete or fragmentary fifteenth-century Czech Bible manuscripts have been preserved (p. 282, fn. 9, citing Cambridge History of the Bible). Hroboň's striking summary: "Thus the Czechs, Moravians, and Slovaks were ready for *sola scriptura* a century before the Reformation" (p. 283).

### The KB as Brethren scholarly achievement (pp. 283–285)

Hroboň characterises the early Unity of Brethren (1457–1557) as "a typical pietistic movement" that "shy[ied] away from human-composed creeds or doctrines, and initially [rejected] theological education and training to interpret the Bible" (p. 283, quoting J. R. Rice 2006). The shift comes with Jan Blahoslav, who became Brethren bishop in 1557. Blahoslav's insistence that "only a thorough education for the leaders [and] for the simple Brethren can ensure a peaceful development of the *Unitas*" (Strupl 1968, p. 10, cited p. 284) drove the Brethren to send students to Wittenberg, Heidelberg, Geneva, and Oxford.

The KB was translated 1577–1594 by a team of seven translators and three assistants (p. 284). The first six-volume edition contained "much extrabiblical material, such as introductions, text-critical notes, word studies, commentaries, summaries, cross-references, and beautiful artistic decoration" (p. 285) — the apparatus, Hroboň notes, was meant "to substitute for a whole theological library of the Brethren pastors and congregations" and "contained the summary of the Brethren's religious views and church apologetic as well as their essential common dogmatic (*loci communes*)" (p. 285, citing Kyas 1997, p. 188). A 1596 single-volume edition followed without the notes, and the third edition (1613) became the standard text for subsequent revisions.

The KB's publication "was illegal because the Council of Trent decided on the Vulgate's magisterial authority in 1546" (p. 284, fn. 17). Blahoslav's earlier 1564 New Testament from Greek had set the translational norm: "the reverence for Scripture should be reflected also in a translation with a rich and refined vocabulary, cultured syntax, and graceful literary style" (p. 284) — pushing toward "traditional, even archaic Czech, especially that of the old sacred writings."

### The KB / KJV parallel (pp. 285–286)

Hroboň's central comparative argument is that despite radically different starting points — KJV with crown and church support, KB as the illegal product of a persecuted minority — both translations achieved similar outcomes through similar combinations of factors: the *prehistory* (Salonika brothers and Hus for Czech; Wycliffe and Tyndale for English); the *milieu* (relative peace, humanism, religious-political striving); the *Reformation emphases* (Scripture, original-language rendering, comprehensibility); the *intellectual credentials* of translators; and *economic factors* (printing press, well-to-do patronage). Both Bibles were authorised by their churches and "some adherents even regarded them as inspired texts" (p. 285).

The closing *kairos* argument — that "such a *kairos* for Bible translation must be of God — the divine gift of a rare and unique historical opportunity" (p. 286) — moves the article from historical narrative into pastoral theology. This is the section most clearly addressed to Hroboň's *Word & World* readership rather than to scholarly historiography.

### Two crucial footnotes for the TEPALIT chapter

Two footnotes carry disproportionate weight for the chapter's argument and require independent verification:

**Footnote 18 (p. 285)** cites J. B. Souček, *Theologie výkladů kralické šestidílky* (Praha: Královská česká společnost nauk, 1933), and reports the conclusion that the KB notes show the Brethren were "clearly Protestants, more Calvinist than Lutheran in their theology." This is exactly the kind of reception-history claim the chapter is committed to scrutinising in the CB-1575 case. Souček's 1933 framing may be accurate, but it may also be an early-twentieth-century reading that needs revisiting against the original notes. The chapter cannot simply absorb the claim from Hroboň. **Action item:** acquire Souček 1933 (Czech, possibly digitised at Kramerius / NDK ČR) before §4.4 drafting begins.

**Footnote 20 (p. 285)** states that "Slovak Lutheran worship was almost exclusively in the Czech language of KB until the second half of the nineteenth century." Hroboň gives no source for this; he states it as known fact. The claim is broadly correct (matching Vajda 1944's narrative and standard Slovak Lutheran historiography), but the precision of "almost exclusively" and "until the second half of the nineteenth century" needs to be triangulated against Slovak Lutheran liturgical sources for the chapter's period (16th–18th century). For §5 (transfer and reception), this is a substantive anchor: the *Cithara Sanctorum*'s success rests on a liturgical-linguistic environment where Slovak Lutheran congregations were already worshiping in *bibličtina* — the literary Czech of the KB — well before Tranovský's 1636 work.

## Notable Quotes

> "Thus the Czechs, Moravians, and Slovaks were ready for *sola scriptura* a century before the Reformation." (p. 283)
- Context: Hroboň's encapsulation of the Hus-Hussite-Brethren preparation for the KB. The line is rhetorically striking and historically defensible — the Hussite reverence for vernacular Scripture preceded Luther by a century. For §4.4 (Písmo a tradícia), this is a useful starting framing, though it requires nuance: Hus's *sola scriptura* sat in a different theological matrix than Luther's, and the chapter should resist any simple genealogical reading.

> "A detailed study of these notes and comments by J. B. Souček showed that the Brethren were clearly Protestants, more Calvinist than Lutheran in their theology." (p. 285, fn. 18, paraphrasing Souček 1933)
- Context: The most consequential single sentence in the article for the chapter's argument. If Souček's 1933 reading holds, then the KB itself is a primary witness to Brethren theological identification with Reformed positions. If Souček's reading is itself a reception-history artefact (analogous to the CB-1575 Calvinist mis-reading the chapter is committed to refuting), then the situation is more complicated. The chapter cannot proceed without checking Souček 1933 directly.

> "Slovak Lutheran worship was almost exclusively in the Czech language of KB until the second half of the nineteenth century." (p. 285, fn. 20)
- Context: Direct support for §5's transfer-and-reception narrative. The KB's Czech (the *bibličtina*) was not just the Czech-confessional Scripture but also the liturgical-linguistic medium of Slovak Lutheran worship — which is precisely the linguistic-theological substrate the *Cithara Sanctorum* enters in 1636. For §2 (confessional context) and §5 (CS reception), this fact-anchor is foundational.

> "[The KB's] publication was illegal because the Council of Trent decided on the Vulgate's magisterial authority in 1546." (p. 284, fn. 17)
- Context: A reminder of the legal-confessional environment the KB navigated. The Trent decision rendered all non-Vulgate translations canonically illegitimate from a Roman perspective, which means the KB's circulation through Bohemian-Moravian-Slovak Protestant communities was already a confessionally significant act apart from any specific theological choices in its text or apparatus.

## Relation to TEPALIT Chapter

- **Which locus/section:** §4.4 (Písmo a tradícia — Scripture's authority across the three confessional traditions; KB as Brethren-produced Scripture used by Slovak Lutherans); §5 (Transfer a recepcia — the KB's *bibličtina* as the linguistic substrate for the *Cithara Sanctorum*'s reception); §2 (Konfesionálny kontext — the trans-confessional Czech-Slovak Protestant Scripture).

- **How it supports our argument:** Hroboň's strongest contribution to the chapter is the documentation of trans-confessional Scripture-use across the Brethren-Lutheran boundary. The KB was a Brethren product (translated by Brethren, printed at Brethren Kralice, accompanied by Brethren *loci communes*-shaped notes), and yet it became "the standard Protestant Bible for Czechs, Moravians, and Slovaks for three and a half centuries" (p. 284) — including, decisively for the chapter, for Slovak Lutherans whose worship through the period of the *Cithara Sanctorum* used KB Czech as its liturgical language. This is concrete evidence for the kind of inter-confessional permeability the chapter argues for: even on the Scripture-and-tradition *locus*, where confessional rigidity might be expected to be highest, Slovak Lutherans and Czech Brethren shared a single textual base. The chapter's *lex orandi* analysis can extend this point: the very Czech in which Slovak Lutherans prayed and sang was Brethren Czech.

- **The Souček-1933 footnote is both a resource and a methodological warning.** If Souček's analysis of the KB notes shows Reformed-leaning theology, the chapter has direct textual evidence for Brethren-Reformed doctrinal proximity in the late-sixteenth-century period. But the chapter also cannot accept that conclusion uncritically — Souček 1933 is exactly the kind of older anglophone-or-Czech secondary source that may carry forward the same "Calvinist Brethren" reading framework the chapter is committed to scrutinising. The *correct* methodological move is to use Hroboň as the entry point to Souček 1933, then read Souček against the KB notes themselves, then judge.

- **Caution about the article's genre.** *Word & World* is a Luther Seminary pastoral journal, not a peer-reviewed historical-critical venue. Hroboň's article serves the chapter best as: (a) an accessible English-language anchor for the KB narrative; (b) a primary witness to current Slovak Lutheran self-narration about the KB; (c) a footnote-trail leading to the older Czech-language scholarship (Kyas 1997, Souček 1933, Vraštil 1933). The chapter should cite Hroboň for orientation and pastoral framing but should anchor its substantive claims in Kyas, Souček, and the primary KB material directly.

- **For §5 specifically.** Hroboň's fn. 20 anchors the chapter's case that the *Cithara Sanctorum*'s success rests on a pre-existing trans-confessional Slovak-Czech Protestant linguistic substrate. Tranovský is not creating that substrate — he is operating within it. The CS's borrowing of Czech-Reformation hymn material thus sits inside a broader pattern of Slovak Lutheran reception of Czech Protestant textual resources, of which the KB is the largest single example. This framing makes CS hymnological transfer more legible as a *natural* extension of an already-functioning trans-confessional textual ecology, rather than as Tranovský's idiosyncratic editorial choice.

## Notes

- The article appears in *Word & World*'s Summer 2011 issue on Bible Translation (Volume 31, Number 3), an issue that pairs Hroboň's piece with comparable essays on other vernacular Bible projects. The companion-essay framing means Hroboň was writing for an audience already primed to think comparatively about the KJV — useful to know when reading his rhetorical choices.
- Hroboň cites Wikipedia at fn. 13 for the standard claim about the third (1613) edition. While the underlying claim is correct, the citation choice signals the article's genre — popular-academic, not historical-critical. The chapter should not echo this citational casualness.
- The article never engages the Brethren / Lutheran / Reformed boundary question with the precision the chapter requires. The phrase "more Calvinist than Lutheran" (fn. 18) is reported flatly without scrutiny. For the chapter's §4.4, this means Hroboň cannot be the final authority on KB theology — only an entry point.
- Worth flagging: the article's geography (Center for Christian Education, Martin) places Hroboň at the institutional centre of contemporary Slovak Lutheran biblical and missiological work. This is the same institutional landscape that produced Augustínová's 2011 *Cithara sanctorum bibliografia* (SNK Martin) and that supports ongoing Slovak Lutheran historical theology. Reading Hroboň as a witness to that institutional matrix — alongside reading him as a secondary historical source — is a defensible interpretive move.
- For the chapter's §4.4, an additional methodological discipline emerges from this note. Hroboň's piece illustrates how Slovak Lutheran self-narration about the KB has crystallised into a kind of confessional commonplace — one that the chapter can engage sympathetically but must not absorb uncritically. The same discipline that the chapter applies to the CB-1575 mis-reading (refute via primary sources, not via secondary echo) applies equally here.
