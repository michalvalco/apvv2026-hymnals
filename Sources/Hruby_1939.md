## Author and historical context

František Hrubý (21 August 1887, Strukov near Olomouc – 10 February 1943, Brno) was a major Czech historian and archivist. Born to a family of modest origin (one of eleven children), he studied three semesters of theology in Olomouc before transferring to Charles University in Prague, where he became Josef Pekař's seminar assistant 1912–1915. Career: 1920–1937 archivist at the Moravian Land Archive in Brno, with the directorship 1927–1937 (succeeding Bertold Bretholz); 1937–1943 University Professor at Masaryk University in Brno. Honors: 1927 corresponding member of the Royal Bohemian Society of Sciences (KČSN), 1933 corresponding member of the Czech Academy of Sciences and Arts. **Editor of *Český časopis historický* from 1936** (succeeding Pekař). Specialty: economic history of Moravia in the 16th–17th centuries, the Czech uprising in Moravia, and the prosopography of Czech and Moravian Protestant nobility.

The 1939 article appeared in the journal Hrubý himself was editing, in the year that began with the German occupation of Bohemia and Moravia (15 March 1939) and the establishment of the Protectorate. Hrubý survived four more years before dying in occupied Brno in February 1943; he is buried at the Central Cemetery in Brno (group 102, grave 117–118). His major posthumous work — the multilingual edition of correspondence between Czech and Moravian Protestant students and Western European Reformed academies (*Étudiants tchèques aux écoles protestantes de l'Europe occidentale à la fin du 16e et au début du 17e siècle*) — was completed by his daughter Libuše Urbánková-Hrubá and published in 1970. *In memoriam František Hrubý 1887–1943* (Brno 1988) collects testimonials and the bibliography of his works.

Disambiguation: this is **not** the Catholic priest-poet "František Hrubý-Bukovec" (Silesian regional writer, distinct entry on databazeknih.cz), and **not** Karel Hrubý, the postwar historian who later wrote on ČČH's first decade.

## Summary

The 1939 article is the foundational Czech-language attack on the historiographical category of "Neo-Utraquism." Reconstructed from the title and from David's three substantive citations in `David_Integrity_2005`, the article's central argument is that the so-called "Neo-Utraquists" of the late 16th and 17th centuries — i.e., the third and fourth generations of *sub utraque* clergy and laity in Bohemia and Moravia, often presumed to have synthesised Lutheran and Hussite-Taborite elements into a distinctive new theological formation — left no body of writings that warrants the "Neo-Utraquist" label as a *theologically distinct* category. What survives instead is straightforwardly Lutheran in doctrinal substance, with no specifically Taborite or distinctively Hussite-derived theological content beyond the institutional fact of the lay chalice and a few liturgical traditions. The ostensibly "Neo-Utraquist" texts read essentially as standard Lutheran writings produced by Czech-speaking authors.

The article is short (14 pp.), which is itself analytically significant: Hrubý argues from absence, not abundance. The thesis stands or falls on what can NOT be found in the documentary record. If a distinctive Neo-Utraquist theological synthesis existed, its texts should be locatable, identifiable, and characterisable. They are not. The label "Neo-Utraquism" therefore functions historiographically as a placeholder for the demographic fact that some 16th–17th-century Bohemian Lutherans were institutionally connected to the Utraquist Consistory — but it does NOT name a theological position.

This argumentative structure — denial-via-absence — is precisely what David inherits and amplifies in his BRRP "Integrity" article (2005), where Hrubý 1939 sits as one of two foundational interlocutors (alongside Hrejsa 1912's *Česká konfesse* and Hrejsa's later 1934 *ČČH* article on Lutheranism and Calvinism in Moravia). David's eleven-point dogmatic-incompatibility list (Integrity pp. 6–7) extends Hrubý's negative argument into a systematic positive demonstration that any conceivable synthesis would be logically self-contradictory.

## Key Arguments (reconstructed from the title and from David's citations)

- **Negative thesis:** "Neo-Utraquism" as a distinctive theological formation does not exist in the documentary record. Czech Lutheran writings of the 16th–17th centuries contain no Taborite-derived theological elements distinct from standard Lutheran tenets (cited in `David_Integrity_2005` p. 7 fn. 23).
- **Documentary range:** The article surveys late-16th and 17th-century Czech-language Lutheran writings (precise corpus needs verification on access). Czech Lutheran texts are doctrinally indistinguishable from German Lutheran texts of the same period (cited in `David_Integrity_2005` p. 8 fn. 27).
- **Historiographical implication:** the Hrejsa-Krofta dichotomy of "Old Utraquism" vs. "Neo-Utraquism" should be replaced by a clearer two-confession picture: continuing Utraquists with their distinctive sacramental and ecclesiological commitments on one side, and Czech-speaking Lutherans (institutionally federated under the post-1609 Consistory) on the other. The "Neo-Utraquist" label conflates institutional federation with theological synthesis.
- **Methodological limit:** Hrubý does not address the *liturgical* and *hymnological* dimensions where Bohemian Reformation traditions clearly DID enter Czech Lutheran practice (the Cithara Sanctorum being the prime later case). His thesis is restricted to dogmatic prose. This is precisely the gap our chapter's *lex orandi* analysis fills (cited in `David_Integrity_2005` p. 23 fn. 99).

## Relation to TEPALIT Chapter

- **Which locus/section:** §3 (Confessional profiles, Utraquist + Czech Lutheran subsections); §3.2 (Utraquist profile specifically); supporting material for §4.1 (Eucharist locus, in establishing that Czech Lutheran Eucharistic doctrine is straightforwardly Lutheran rather than transitional).
- **How it supports our argument:** Hrubý's negative thesis hardens the confessional boundary the chapter's "Lutheranisation" argument crosses. If "Neo-Utraquism" as theological synthesis does not exist, then hymn material entering CS from Czech Reformation sources is being received not into a continuous Czech Lutheran-Utraquist hybrid, but across a real confessional boundary. Tranovský's editorial work in CS becomes more, not less, theologically loaded. The "Lutheranisation" we trace is the act by which Utraquist (and Brethren) hymn material is moved into a properly Lutheran register.
- **Points of agreement:** the argument from documentary absence (no Neo-Utraquist theology can be named or cited); the implication that the Hrejsa-Krofta historiographical dichotomy needs replacing; the methodological priority of asking what specific texts say rather than what historiographical categories presume.
- **Points of tension:** Hrubý's analysis is restricted to dogmatic prose. It does NOT address the liturgical and hymnological dimensions where Bohemian Reformation traditions clearly DID enter Czech Lutheran practice. Our chapter's *lex orandi* approach fills exactly this gap: hymns ARE doctrinally consequential (per `Brown_2005`), so the absence of a Neo-Utraquist theology in dogmatic prose does not automatically mean the absence of a hybrid in lived liturgical practice. The chapter's contribution is to show what hymnological transfer registers that dogmatic prose did not record.
- **Methodological note:** Hrubý's article, written by the editor of *ČČH* under occupation conditions in 1939, is itself a scholarly act of clarification rather than expansion — exactly what the chapter inherits in spirit.
