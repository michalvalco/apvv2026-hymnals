## Summary

David's four-article project, conducted between 1999 and ca. 2004, is the most ambitious English-language attempt to reframe the historiography of late Utraquism. The standard Czech account, derived from Hrejsa (1912) and Krofta (1909, 1914), reads the period 1575–1622 as the slow death of Utraquism — either by absorption into the Roman Church (the "Old Utraquist" thesis) or by dissolution into Lutheranism (the "Neo-Utraquist" thesis). David rejects both.

The four articles share a single architectural argument with three load-bearing columns. *First*, the 1575 Bohemian Confession was a political alliance, not a theological capitulation. Utraquists, Lutherans, and Brethren signed the same document because they faced the same Roman threat, not because they shared the same theology. The Confession's text is essentially the Augsburg Confession with minor concessions toward Calvinism — *not* toward Utraquism. The Utraquists subscribed to it as the legal umbrella under which the *sub utraque* party could operate, knowing perfectly well that its theological content was Lutheran, and reserving their own dogmatic and liturgical practice for their own consistory. *Second*, "Neo-Utraquism" as a confessional phenomenon never existed. No Neo-Utraquist theologian can be named; no Neo-Utraquist text can be cited; no synthesis of Utraquist and Lutheran tenets can even be coherently sketched. The eleven dogmatic incompatibilities David enumerates (sacramental priesthood, mass-as-sacrifice, infant communion, seven sacraments, intercession of saints, etc.) make synthesis a logical impossibility. The "Neo-Utraquist" label was applied either superfluously (to ordinary Czech Lutherans) or inaccurately (to mainline Utraquists), to serve historiographical and confessional agendas that had little to do with the documentary record. *Third*, in the period 1575–1609 the Utraquist Consistory maintained genuine independence vis-à-vis both the Roman Curia and the noble Protestant estates. The Rezek apostasy of 1593 was an episode, not a trend, and the Consistory's vigorous and decisive recovery from it actually *deepened* Utraquist suspicions of the Curia's intentions.

A consequential corollary follows. Because Utraquism remained a genuinely distinct confessional formation with its own ecclesiology (sacramental priesthood + apostolic succession via Roman bishops), liturgy (pre-Tridentine Roman rites + infant communion + Hus-as-saint), and self-understanding ("Catholic" in the patristic-Nicene sense, not the Tridentine sense) until the Counter-Reformation crushed it after 1620, the Counter-Reformation in Bohemia must be reread. It was not, as is conventionally assumed, primarily an anti-Protestant campaign. It was an "anti-Catholic crusade" — the displacement of "a customary, indulgent, plebeian, optimistic, and realistic Catholicism" by "a rigorist, aristocratic, ascetic, mystical, and ultimately an alien" one. David's coinage: the Counter-Reformation in Bohemia was a *De-Catholicisation*. Domestic, rooted Utraquist Catholicism was erased; imposed Tridentine Catholicism replaced it as "an incongruous, inorganic implant."

For the TEPALIT chapter the implications are decisive at three levels. *Methodologically*, David's case for a hardened confessional boundary between Utraquism and Lutheranism — sustained right up to White Mountain — undercuts any narrative in which Utraquist hymn material in *Cithara Sanctorum* could be read as a "natural" extension of "Neo-Utraquist" repertoire into Slovak Lutheran worship. There is no Neo-Utraquism. The hymns that pass into CS pass across a *real* confessional boundary. The "Lutheranisation" the chapter traces becomes, on David's reading, more striking and more theologically loaded, not less. *Substantively*, David supplies the dogmatic-comparative scaffolding the chapter's §3 (Utraquist profile) and §4.2 (ecclesiology) need: an eleven-point Utraquist/Lutheran incompatibility list, a closely-argued account of Utraquist sacramental priesthood and apostolic-succession claims, a careful reconstruction of Utraquist relations to the post-Tridentine Roman Church, and a reasoned demographic estimate (65–78% of Czech-speakers as non-Protestant Utraquists by 1620) that puts the *quantitative* weight where the dogmatic-comparative argument requires it. *Apparatus-wise*, David's footnote work surfaces a substantial corpus of late-Utraquist printed witnesses (Rvačovský, Cykáda, Štelcar Želetavský, Polon, Bruncvík, Bydžovský, Walkmberger, Žatecký, Šturm) which the field has under-engaged and which the chapter can profitably mine for its lex orandi argument.


## Key Arguments

### 1. The 1575 Bohemian Confession as a political-alliance umbrella, not a theological merger

David's foundational argument, articulated in the 1999 *Church History* article and consolidated in "Cohabitation," reframes the standard reading of the Confessio Bohemica. The CB was *not* a confessional capitulation by Utraquists to Lutheranism. It was a legal-political instrument under which three theologically-distinct *sub utraque* groups (Utraquists, Lutherans, Brethren) could shelter from a common Roman threat. Subscription to the document established legitimacy of religious dissent, not theological agreement. ("Cohabitation," p. 2)

Three pieces of evidence carry the argument:

(a) **The text itself is essentially the Augsburg Confession.** "The Bohemian Confession of 1575 has been cited at times as a quintessential example of a Neo-Utraquist text, but in fact it is virtually identical with the Augsburg Confession. Its modest tilt is not toward Utraquism, but toward Calvinism for the sake of the Brethren" ("Integrity," p. 7, fn. 23, citing Hrejsa, *Česká konfesse* 277, 279, 663). The Czech Lutherans themselves treated CB and AC as "identical, or virtually identical" — see Bidones z Bidenthalu's 1624 *Výstraha* (cited "Integrity," p. 7, fn. 23; assurances given to German Lutheran ministers in Prague October 1609, Hrejsa, *Česká konfesse* 483).

(b) **The Utraquist Consistory rejected CB officially in 1609.** Tomáš of Soběslav, the last Administrator of the Utraquist-only Consistory (January–July 1609), formally condemned both the Augsburg Confession and the Brethren's Confession on 7 February 1609, in response to a Royal Chancery inquiry, reiterating the same objections the Utraquist Consistory had raised in 1575 ("Cohabitation," p. 3, citing Vávra, "Katolíci a sněm český r. 1608 a 1609," *Sborník historického kroužku* 1 [1893] 15; text of Tomáš's reply in Slavata, *Paměti* 1:214–219). This is conclusive: the Utraquist Consistory's *official* theological position, on the eve of the Letter of Majesty, was a rejection of the Confession that Lutherans and Brethren claimed it had endorsed.

(c) **The Utraquists supported the Letter of Majesty *despite* opposing CB theologically because of "salami tactics" reasoning.** The Utraquists understood that if Lutherans and Brethren were eliminated, Utraquism would be eliminated next. David documents the explicit Curial strategy: Lanoy 1577 (suppress Brethren first, then Lutherans, then Utraquists), Bonomi 1584 (expel Pikharts and Lutherans, then force Utraquist union), Malaspina January 1585 (split the dissidents to extirpate them). The Utraquist alliance with Lutherans and Brethren in 1608–1609 was a textbook defensive coalition ("Cohabitation," pp. 4–5, citing *Druhá Apologie* 469–474, *Sněmy české* 11:40, 11:54–60).

The Utraquists' own grasp of the dynamics is recorded in Pavel Skála ze Zhoře's *Historie česká od r. 1602 do r. 1623* (ed. Tieftrunk, Prague 1865, 1:111): in May 1608 Roman emissaries approached Utraquists in the Diet, offered them special protection if they would break with the heterodox allies, and the Utraquists refused. Their stated reason: "If the priests not ordained by bishops [i.e. Lutheran] and the Brethren's clergy were banished, then almost certainly the Consistorial [i.e. Utraquist] clergy would be either banned or brought under the full jurisdiction of, and into full obedience to, the Archbishop" (cited "Cohabitation," p. 4).

For §3 and §4 of the chapter, this radically reframes how CB 1575 should be read. It is *not* the synthesis-document the Lutheranisation thesis sometimes treats it as. Its theological content is Lutheran, but its function is irenic-political. The Eucharistic formula *duchovně i podstatně, věrou i také ústy* — already analysed in `Sources/Analysis_Eucharistic_ThreeColumn.md` as a deliberately ambiguous bridging formula — sits inside this larger architecture: the Confession is wide enough to shelter parties that disagree on *what it means*, and the Utraquist Consistory's official position was that it did not bind them theologically at all.

### 2. The non-existence of "Neo-Utraquism" — the eleven incompatibilities

The "Integrity" article's core argument: nobody can name a Neo-Utraquist theologian or a Neo-Utraquist text, because the very concept is incoherent. The eleven points David enumerates ("Integrity," pp. 6–7) are an inventory of dogmatic incompatibilities so deep that synthesis is impossible:

1. **Sacramental priesthood vs. priesthood of all believers** — the foundational ecclesiological divergence.
2. **Adoration of the host outside the eucharistic service** vs. Lutheran condemnation of this as blasphemy.
3. **Mass as sacrifice** vs. Lutheran celebratory Last Supper.
4. **Infant communion** vs. Lutheran abhorrence of the practice.
5. **Activist fulfilment of God's law** vs. Lutheran passive acceptance of justification.
6. **Soteriological efficacy of good works** vs. Lutheran condemnation of works as "spiritual bribery."
7. **Seven sacraments** vs. Lutheran rejection of all but two (at most three).
8. **Intercession of saints** vs. Lutheran condemnation of this as "spiritual adultery."
9. **Veneration of images of saints** vs. Lutheran condemnation as idolatry.
10. **Acceptance of the Western Church as evolved during the first millennium** vs. Lutheran insistence on apostolic-times-only church.
11. **Retention of canon law** vs. Luther's vesting of ecclesiastical authority in the state.

David then names theologians on each side and asks who might span the divide. Utraquist theologians of the late 16th and early 17th centuries (named by David, p. 7): Jan Cykáda, Valentin Polon, Vavřinec Leander Rvačovský of Rvačov, Jakub Soffian Walkmberger of Walkmbergk, Jan Straněnský. Czech Lutheran theologians (named): Zacharyáš Bruncvík, Jiřík Dykastus (Miřkovský), Václav Slovacius, Jan Štelcar Želetavský of Želetava. Between these two lists, David notes, there is no synthesiser.

The Bohemian Confession does not bridge the gap because, as already noted, it is Lutheran-with-Calvinist-tilt, not Lutheran-with-Utraquist-synthesis. Czech Lutheran writers (Kolín 1574, Bruncvík 1613, Martinius 1618) attempted to claim the Hussite/Bohemian heritage, but the claims were superficial. Hrubý's meticulous research (cited "Integrity," pp. 7–8, fn. 27) showed that Czech Lutheran writings contain no Taborite-derived theological elements distinct from standard Lutheran tenets. Where Czech Lutherans deviated from Teutonic Lutheran orthodoxy, they borrowed not from the Bohemian Reformation but from English Puritanism (Bruncvík relied heavily on Foxe, Humphrey, Salnar's *Harmonia confessionum*, and on the writings of William Perkins, three of whose works were translated into Czech 1610–1620; "Integrity," p. 8, fn. 28).

For the chapter, this is methodologically crucial. If David is correct, then Utraquist-origin hymn material in CS cannot be smoothly absorbed into a Lutheran-Hussite continuum, because no such continuum existed at the level of *theology*. The hymns travel a real confessional gap. The "Lutheranisation" the chapter analyses is the more theologically loaded for it.

### 3. The Utraquist Consistory's institutional independence (1575–1609) and the meaning of "Catholic"

The "Curia" article systematically dismantles the assumption that the Utraquist Consistory drifted into Roman dependency between 1575 and 1609. Three institutional findings carry the case:

(a) **The Consistory was administratively a Royal office, not an archiepiscopal one.** It styled itself "administrator et parochi consistorii archiepiscopatus Pragensis sub utraque communicantium" (e.g. 1579, *Sněmy české* 5:516; cited "Curia," p. 9, fn. 46). The Royal Chancellery selected and appointed Administrator and Consistory members; the Archbishop functioned only as a clearing-house intermediary. Rudolf II preferred this arrangement because it gave the crown direct influence over Utraquists, in line with Matoušek's analysis ("Kurie a boj o konsistoř pod obojí," ČČH 37 [1931] 18, 30–31; cited "Curia," p. 10, fn. 53).

(b) **The Rezek defection of September 1593 was an *episode*, not a trend.** Administrator Fabian Rezek's apostasy, secured by Nuncio Speciano, required Rezek to renounce on oath "schisma, haereses et errores Hussitarum" (*Sněmy české* 8:338; cited "Curia," p. 8, fn. 43). The Consistory's recovery from this assault was rapid and decisive. David's verdict: the Rezek affair "deepened the Utraquists' suspicions of the Curia's intentions" rather than weakening their resolve ("Curia," p. 2).

(c) **"Catholic" in Utraquist usage is patristic-Nicene, not Tridentine.** The Utraquists declared adherence to "the Catholic faith" repeatedly between 1575 and 1609, and this has been read as evidence of Roman alignment. David shows it is not. "Catholic" in Utraquist usage refers to the church of the first millennium — the church the Utraquists believed they continued, against the medieval and Tridentine deformations of the Roman Curia. Bohuslav Bílejovský's *Kronyka cýrkevní* (early 16th c.) is the foundational articulation; Pavel Bydžovský's *Děťátka a neviňátka* (1541, ff. A5b–A6a, B8a) is a later witness ("Curia," p. 3, fn. 10).

Critically, even Roman authorities recognised this usage. Archbishop Berka in his letter to Rudolf II of 12 September 1595 (*Sněmy české* 9:183) wrote of "in hoc regno alia religio praeter antiquam catholicam sub una et alteram sub utraque communicantium toleretur" — explicitly distinguishing "antiquam catholicam sub una" from "alteram sub utraque communicantium" while applying "Catholic" to both. In an August 1595 letter (*Sněmy české* 9:178), Berka spoke of "katolickou pak víru a řád dobrej církevní starobylý" shared by both *sub una* and *sub utraque* parties. The category "Catholic" in 1590s Bohemia covered Utraquists, not just Roman adherents ("Curia," p. 8, fns. 37–38).

For §4.2 (ecclesiology): David's analysis of Utraquist ecclesiology is the densest single deposit in the corpus. The Utraquists looked to *the church of the first millennium*, like Erasmus, More, and Fisher — but unlike these humanists, they had institutional continuity to defend (the Prague archdiocese, transferred to Utraquist hands by Archbishop Konrad of Vechta in 1419, affirmed by the *Compactata* in 1436; "Curia," p. 7, fn. 32, citing *Sněmy české* 4:206). This is *not* a Lutheran ecclesiology. The Utraquists rejected papal *administrative and judicial* jurisdiction but acknowledged Rome's *sacerdotal* role as the priesthood-guarantor of the Western Church. They had their priests ordained by bishops in communion with Rome (when they could; otherwise abroad). They retained canon law. They retained sacramental priesthood as the load-bearing ecclesial concept. None of this maps onto a Lutheran or Calvinist ecclesiology.

The 1602 Administrator's report to Rudolf II is the institutional smoking gun: of all priests under the Consistory's jurisdiction in royal towns, only three were not properly episcopally ordained, and even for those three the Consistory had qualified replacements available. The priests pledged "podle mšálu a rubriky znění se říditi a spravovati" — to administer according to the missal and rubrics ("Integrity," p. 12, fn. 45, citing *Sněmy české* 10:371). The pre-Tridentine Prague-archdiocese missal and rubrics distinguished the Utraquists from the Lutherans as the Book of Common Prayer distinguished Anglicans from Puritans (David's analogy, "Integrity," p. 12).

### 4. The Letter of Majesty (1609) and the joint Consistory: federation, not absorption

"Cohabitation" is the centre-piece of the Utraquist post-1609 case. The standard reading — that the Letter of Majesty signalled the immediate doom of Utraquism — is documented from Tomek (1848), Krofta (1936), Winter (1991), and Borový (n.d.) in the article's opening pages ("Cohabitation," p. 1). David then dismantles each.

Five findings carry the case:

(a) **Václav Budovec of Budov, the architect of the 1609 settlement, defined the *sub utraque* party as containing three distinct groups.** On 25 June 1609 he specified: (1) the Utraquists, administered hitherto by the Prague Consistory; (2) those administered by priests ordained in Germany, i.e. the Lutherans; (3) the Brethren. The Utraquists were named first. Budovec compared the three groups to Pauline churches (Romans, Corinthians, Galatians, etc.) which differed in ritual and even doctrine without compromising their unity in Christ. Source: Glücklich, *O historických dílech Václava Budovce* (1911), p. 68; Skála ze Zhoře, *Historie česká* 1:108–109 ("Cohabitation," p. 6, fn. 22).

(b) **The 1610 Church Order that mandated abandonment of Utraquist liturgical practices was, in its operative force, addressed only to Lutheran clergy.** The Order's address — "priests of the Lord *sub utraque* **and** with heart and lips adhering to the Bohemian Confession" — should be read restrictively, not generally. The Brethren retained their own liturgical peculiarities. The Utraquists likewise. The injunctions against processions, host elevation, infant communion, and traditional liturgical books would have been "entirely unacceptable" to Utraquists if applied generally; their non-application to Utraquists is confirmed by the subsequent operation of the Consistory ("Cohabitation," p. 12, citing Bruncvík, *Kšaftu Večeře Páně* 213; Hrejsa, *Česká konfesse* 484 n. 1).

(c) **The new joint Consistory was confederative, not unitary.** Its founding commission of 30 July 1609 included four each of Lutheran and Unity clergymen *plus four Utraquist priests with canonical episcopal ordinations*, hitherto under the Utraquist Consistory ("Cohabitation," p. 10, citing Hrejsa, *Česká konfesse* 473–474). Budovec's formulation is that "different orders, ceremonies and ecclesiastical disciplines among them, do not and, God willing, will not destroy the unity of divine truth...or the bond of Christian love among them" (Glücklich, *O historických dílech* 68; cited "Cohabitation," p. 10, fn. 40).

(d) **Utraquism survived 1609 numerically robust.** David's reasoned demographic estimate ("Integrity," p. 14, fn. 59): 65–78% of the Czech-speaking population of Bohemia in 1620 was non-Protestant Utraquist; 5–10% Czech Lutheran or "Neo-Utraquist"; 5–10% Brethren; 12–15% *sub una*. Working from the population total of ca. 1.7 million (Kafka & Válka, *Dějiny Československa, 1437–1781*, 201), of whom 1.2 million were Czechs, this yields between 780,000 and 936,000 Utraquists in Bohemia on the eve of the 1618 uprising — a vastly larger figure than is implied by historiographical narratives of Utraquist demise.

(e) **Utraquism survived 1620 institutionally and showed itself in the post-White-Mountain enforcement record.** The papal instruction to nuncio Carafa of 12 April 1621 (*Die Hauptinstruktionen Gregors XV*, 2:621–622) classed the majority of Bohemian dissidents as "Hussiti" (i.e. Utraquists, *not* Lutherans) and treated them as schismatics eligible for "reunion," not heretics requiring full conversion. Most Utraquist clergy were accepted into Roman ranks without re-ordination because their episcopal ordinations were canonically valid ("Integrity," pp. 15–17, fns. 61–66). The cases of Jan Locika of Domažlice (5,000+ communicants at his Easter 1622 sub utraque masses at Týn; beheaded April 1622) and Vavřinec Hanžburský of Kopeček (executed April 1631 for secret sub utraque administration) document Utraquist resilience under suppression ("Integrity," pp. 16–18).

For §5 (Transfer and Reception): David's chronology decisively reframes the question of what Tranovský inherited when he compiled CS in the 1620s–1630s. The Utraquist hymn repertoire that flowed into CS came from a confession that, on David's reading, was *still institutionally and theologically distinct* from Lutheranism right up to the moment of its violent suppression. The transfer is not from a "dying" or "Lutheranising" Utraquism into a "naturally absorbing" Lutheran orbit. It is from a *suppressed* tradition with its own dogmatic profile into a Slovak Lutheran milieu performing the work of reception, filtering, and theological re-registration. The "Lutheranisation" the chapter traces takes place at exactly this transfer-moment, and David's argument hardens its theological significance.

### 5. The Counter-Reformation as De-Catholicisation — methodological consequence

David's culminating move ("Integrity," pp. 22–23) inverts the standard frame. If late Utraquism was a rooted, plebeian, indulgent, optimistic, realistic Catholicism — not a Protestant or Crypto-Protestant deviation — then the Counter-Reformation campaign that suppressed it was not anti-Protestant but *anti-Catholic*. The Tridentine Roman Catholicism that was imposed after 1620 was an "alien" formation: rigorist, aristocratic, ascetic, mystical, foreign. "Catholicism with a human face had to yield to a Catholicism with a face that was superhuman."

The methodological consequence for the chapter: the historical-theological register against which Slovak Lutheran reception of Czech-Reformation hymnody must be measured is not a binary "Catholic vs. Protestant" axis but a more layered confessional landscape in which (1) Utraquist Catholicism was a genuinely third option, (2) it was suppressed precisely because it was Catholic-but-not-Tridentine, and (3) its hymnological output entered Slovak Lutheran worship as material that had been formed inside a confessional space the Counter-Reformation took 200 years to dismantle. The chapter's "Lutheranisation" thesis sits on top of David's "De-Catholicisation" thesis: the same hymn could undergo *two* confessional re-registrations in two generations.

> "[T]he idea of Lutheranism's prevalence under the form of Neo-Utraquism conformed on the metahistorical plane with an *a priori* assumption characteristic of the liberal and the Marxist historiography about the inevitability and virtually irresistible force of the Protestant Reformation. In this light the refusal of the Utraquists to turn Protestant seemed to defy the laws of history. The cogency of this view, however, has much diminished in the twenty-first century, once history stopped adhering to the deterministic sweep of Hegel's Absolute Reason, Comte's positive law, or Marx's economic dialectic, and the issue of Protestantism's appeal could be examined in a more empirical way." ("Integrity," p. 5)
- **Context:** A historiographical-philosophical observation that should be quotable in §1 (introduction). David's diagnosis of why the "Neo-Utraquism" thesis acquired such tenacity is a useful frame for the chapter's own historiographical situating.

> "...if it were true what the Brethren ... write about us, who are administered by the general Holy Christian and Catholic Church, and about those, who are administered by the Czech and Prague priests [*Českými a Pražskými kněžími se spravují*], then none of us can be saved. Namely, that we Romans or Papists — as they call us — are idolators, and the Czechs [*Čechové*] are still worse, only because they give the venerable Sacrament of the Altar ... to little children." (Václav Šturm, *Krátké ozvání*, Prague 1584, p. 3; cited "Integrity," p. 11, fn. 44)
- **Context:** The Jesuit Šturm in 1584 uses "the Czechs" as a synonym for "Utraquists," and identifies infant communion as the distinguishing Utraquist marker. This is a *contemporary external testimony* — from a hostile witness — that Utraquists were neither Lutheran nor sub una. Direct support for §3 and §4.1.

> "The people are papist and especially the women folk who hold more onto the works than onto the faith." (Henrick Kilian, *Reise Beschreibung*, April 1592; cited "Integrity," p. 11, fn. 41, in Pánek, "Čechy, Morava a Lužice v německém cestopisu," FHB 13 [1990] 221)
- **Context:** A north-German Lutheran traveller's frustrated observation of late-Utraquist Bohemia. The "papist" character he describes is not Roman; it is Utraquist. The works-emphasis is the Utraquist soteriological accent. Direct support for §4.3 (soteriology locus).

> "The Hussites inhabit over two thirds of the city and the rituals of their mass are virtually the same as ours [Roman Catholic]. On the Feast Day of Corpus Christi they even conduct processions through the city and carry the host in the streets. The Jesuits and the others of our faith judge that they should not be impeded in adoring the host because, as far as known, it is touched by the hands of a genuine priest... The Hussite priests distribute communion in both kinds... The Hussites have no other images of saints than paintings on boards in their churches; they hold the chief temple of the city [Týn] and also all the other churches, while the Catholics can dispense the sacraments only in the monasteries." (Pierre Bergeron, French embassy secretary, 1600; cited "Integrity," p. 11, fn. 42, in *Tři francouzští kavalíři v rudolfínské Praze*, ed. Fučíková, 44–45)
- **Context:** The most vivid contemporary external testimony to late-Utraquist Eucharistic and ecclesial practice. Two-thirds of Prague was Utraquist in 1600; the Eucharistic rite was structurally Roman but conducted by canonically-ordained priests recognised by the Jesuits as performing valid consecration. Critical for §4.1 and §4.2.

> "Bohemian Utraquism might have served as a model for the reform of the Church of Rome which was liberal, yet non-Protestant, and which could be juxtaposed to the authoritarian model, which would prevail at Trent. Such a liberal alternative was not purely theoretical, it was envisaged particularly by the circle of Erasmus. It would have involved an 'Utraquistisation' of the Roman Church." ("Integrity," p. 22)
- **Context:** David's most ambitious counter-factual. Speaks directly to §3 (Utraquist profile) and the methodological framing of the chapter — Utraquism as a *real third option* in the 16th-century Western confessional landscape, not a transitional formation between Rome and Wittenberg.

> "If this is granted, the Counter Reformation can be viewed in Bohemia not as a Re-Catholicisation but as a De-Catholicisation. It was a process which aimed at replacing a customary, indulgent, plebeian, optimistic, and realistic Catholicism with a rigorist, aristocratic, ascetic, mystical, and ultimately an alien, one. The inherently trusting and tolerant Catholicism of Bohuslav Bílejovský had to yield to an inherently suspicious and intolerant Catholicism of Bohuslav Balbín. Metaphorically speaking, Catholicism with a human face had to yield to a Catholicism with a face that was superhuman." ("Integrity," pp. 22–23)
- **Context:** David's most quotable summary line. Indispensable for §2 (confessional context) and §5 (the post-1620 environment in which CS material was being canonised).

> "The Letter of Majesty assumed the continued coexistence of a variety of *sub utraque*. The basic assurance came from the main architect of the arrangement under the Letter, Václav Budovec of Budov, on 25 June 1609, who specifically defined the party *sub utraque* which rallied under the banner of the Bohemian Confession as consisting of three distinct groups: (1) the Utraquists, that is, those administered hitherto by the Prague Consistory; (2) those administered by priests ordained in Germany, that is, the Lutherans, and (3) the Brethren. Interestingly enough, the Utraquists were named first before the Lutherans and the Brethren." ("Integrity," p. 3, fn. 9)
- **Context:** The single most important institutional-historical datum on the actual confessional structure under the 1609 settlement. Direct evidence that the *sub utraque* category was understood as confederative-of-three, not unitary-Lutheran. Indispensable for §2 (confessional context) and §5 (the milieu Tranovský inherited).

## Relation to TEPALIT Chapter

### §1 — Introduction
- David's historiographical diagnosis of why "Neo-Utraquism" acquired such tenacity (liberal/Marxist a priori; "Integrity," p. 5) is quotable as part of the chapter's own situating gesture. The chapter's "Lutheranisation" lens is a methodological correction to the same family of assumptions.
- The chapter's contribution is sharpened by David's argument: where "Neo-Utraquism" frames hymnological transfer as natural confessional drift, the chapter's *lex orandi* lens reveals it as theological *work* performed at a real confessional boundary.

### §2 — Confessional context
- The "salami tactics" framing ("Cohabitation," pp. 4–5) is the operative confessional-political dynamic of the period.
- The 1609 Letter of Majesty as a *confederative* settlement of three distinct confessions ("Cohabitation," p. 6, fn. 22) is the institutional frame.
- The Counter-Reformation as De-Catholicisation ("Integrity," pp. 22–23) is the post-1620 environment in which Tranovský's CS was compiled and circulated.

### §3 — Dogmatic profiles (Utraquist locus, primarily)
- David's eleven-point Utraquist/Lutheran incompatibility list ("Integrity," pp. 6–7) is the readymade dogmatic-comparative scaffold for the Utraquist profile section.
- The named theologians (Cykáda, Polon, Rvačovský, Walkmberger, Straněnský for Utraquism; Bruncvík, Dykastus, Slovacius, Štelcar Želetavský for Czech Lutheranism; "Integrity," p. 7) are the contemporary witnesses of each tradition.
- The Schoen 2022 dissertation already in the chapter's bibliography (`resources/notes-Schoen-2022-Utraquismus.md`) supplements David with the Konfessionsbildung perspective and a more explicitly periodised account of Utraquist development.

### §4.1 — Eucharist locus
- Utraquist mass-as-sacrifice ("Integrity," p. 8, fn. 31, citing Holeton's BRRP 2 [1998] textual study at pp. 115–120) — direct evidence that the Utraquists *retained* what Luther had *banished* (offertory, canon).
- Infant communion as Utraquist confessional marker ("Curia," p. 6, fn. 31; "Integrity," p. 11, fn. 44) — flatly incompatible with Lutheranism (and Trent).
- Bergeron's 1600 testimony on Eucharistic processions, host adoration, communion sub utraque distribution by canonically ordained priests ("Integrity," p. 11, fn. 42) — concrete liturgical-practice testimony.
- The 1602 Administrator's report on priests serving "podle mšálu a rubriky" ("Integrity," p. 12, fn. 45, citing *Sněmy české* 10:371) — institutional confirmation that pre-Tridentine Roman liturgical practice was the Utraquist standard at the eve of the Letter of Majesty.

### §4.2 — Ecclesiology locus
- Utraquist sacramental priesthood + apostolic-succession-via-Roman-bishops as the "clearest line of demarcation" between Utraquism and Lutheranism ("Integrity," p. 22).
- The fn. 90 contemporary aside on the 1997 ELCA/Episcopal Concordat of Agreement and its blockage by Lutheran rejection of historical episcopate (already noted in your prior 1999-article extraction) confirms that the very ecclesiological boundary at issue in 1575 remains operative in modern Lutheran/Anglican ecumenical discussions.
- Utraquist "Catholic" identity as patristic-Nicene, not Tridentine ("Curia," p. 3, fn. 10; pp. 7–8, fns. 37–38) — refutes any reading of late Utraquist self-identification as evidence of Roman alignment.
- The institutional architecture of the Consistory ("Curia," p. 9, fn. 46): self-styled as "administrator et parochi consistorii archiepiscopatus Pragensis sub utraque communicantium," institutionally a Royal office, traceable to the 1419 transfer of the Prague archdiocesan seal by Konrad of Vechta.

### §4.3 — Soteriology and Ethics locus
- Utraquist activism in fulfilling God's law vs. Lutheran passive justification ("Integrity," p. 6, point 5; p. 7, point 6).
- Kilian's 1592 observation on women's works-orientation ("Integrity," p. 11, fn. 41).
- Štelcar Želetavský's *Kázání dvoje* (Prague 1586) and Polon's *Pomni na mne* (Prague 1589) on priestly responsibility, good works, and the activist Christian life — primary source pointers for §4.3.

### §4.4 — Authority of Scripture and Tradition locus
- Utraquist patristic-tradition orientation ("Curia," pp. 3–4, on Erasmus, More, Fisher parallel; the Cassiodorus 1594 and Josephus 1592 translations).
- Pavel Bydžovský's 1554 *Historiae aliquot Anglorum martyrum* — Utraquist Latin celebration of Thomas More and John Fisher ("Curia," p. 5, fn. 22). A Utraquist hagiographic engagement with Roman martyrs is theologically incompatible with any Lutheran or Calvinist position.
- The Hus-as-saint propers in late-16th-c. Utraquist missals (Holeton's "Office of Jan Hus" [Esztergom antiphonary], 1990; cited "Integrity," p. 9, fn. 32, with Novotný 1932 ed. as core source).

### §5 — Transfer and reception in *Cithara Sanctorum*
- The 1609–1622 confessional architecture David documents is the immediate background to Tranovský's compilation work. CS material drawn from Utraquist sources crosses a real boundary the Counter-Reformation was actively destroying after 1620.
- Locika of Domažlice's Easter 1622 Utraquist masses at Týn (5,000+ communicants) and Hanžburský of Kopeček's secret sub utraque administration at St. Henry's and St. Adalbert (1625–1631) are the *living Utraquist liturgical witnesses* against which the §5 reception narrative can be calibrated. The Lutheranised Utraquist hymns in CS were entering Slovak Lutheran worship at the very moment Bohemian Utraquism itself was being violently extinguished.
- The "decontextualisation of the hymn repertoire from its confessional home" — already noted in `Sources/notes-Schoen-2022-Utraquismus.md` — is given concrete institutional substance by David's "Integrity" article: the confessional home was not "vanishing" but *being suppressed*.

## Methodological Note for Chapter Drafting

David's argument has direct *strengthening* effect on the chapter's "Lutheranisation" thesis, contrary to what a superficial reading of the Phase 1 literature search might have suggested. The Phase 1 entry for `David_1999` flagged it as "potential challenge to thesis" — a reasonable initial assessment given only the abstract was visible. With the full corpus now in hand, the assessment reverses: David's denial of "Neo-Utraquism" *hardens* the confessional boundary the chapter's Lutheranisation thesis crosses. Tranovský's CS, on David's reading, draws on hymn material from a confession that was institutionally and theologically *distinct* from Lutheranism right up to the moment of its violent suppression. The "Lutheranisation" the chapter analyses is the *first* registration-shift many of these hymns underwent on entering CS; the *second* registration-shift was the De-Catholicisation of the Bohemian environment in which the original Utraquist forms had circulated. The chapter's *lex orandi* lens is uniquely positioned to read both shifts simultaneously.
