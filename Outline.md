## 1. Introduction

**Purpose:** Situate the chapter within the TEPALIT project; define objectives, methodology, scope.

### 1.1 Scope and approach (the chapter's standing pre-emption of the parity-objection)

This chapter undertakes a dogmatic comparison of three confessional currents — Augsburg Lutheranism, Old and Neo-Utraquism, and the Unity of Brethren — as these doctrinal positions surface in liturgical and hymnological practice between the consolidation of the Confessio Augustana (1530) and the late phase of Slovak Lutheran orthodoxy in the eighteenth century. The methodological wager of the chapter is that liturgical use and hymnic text constitute a more candid record of confessional theology than the apologetic genres of normative documents alone: what congregations sang and prayed reveals what they actually believed, often more faithfully than what their representatives signed. The methodology is enacted in David R. Holeton's "Evolution of Utraquist Liturgy" (*Studia Liturgica* 25/1, 1995) and the nine BRRP essays that built on it; this chapter extends Holeton's *lex orandi, lex credendi* approach into the Slovak Lutheran reception field that he never lived to complete.

That methodological commitment carries an evidentiary consequence. The *lived theology* record between these three confessional families is not symmetric. It is shaped by a directional flow of textual material — Czech Reformation sources moving into Slovak Lutheran reception, and there undergoing dogmatic re-framing. The Cithara Sanctorum (Tranovský, 1636+) is the most consequential instance of this reception, but not the only one: the Bardejov Catechism (1556) modifies Luther's Small Catechism within a Slovak humanist register, and Leonard Stöckel's annotations on Melanchthon's *Loci Communes* (1561) construct a Wittenberg–Bardejov Lutheran identity that defines itself partly against Czech Reformation alternatives. The doctrinal asymmetry that this directional flow produces is not an artefact of source availability — it is a feature of the historical phenomenon itself.

Accordingly, the chapter pursues comparison **through reception rather than parallel exposition**. The four dogmatic loci — Eucharistic theology, ecclesiology, soteriology and ethics, and scripture–tradition relations — are read primarily through Slovak Lutheran sources adapting Czech material. Where Utraquist or Brethren positions appear, they appear as positions being received, modified, or rejected. The chapter does not attempt parity of dogmatic-profile coverage between the three traditions, and it does not rehearse the full theological identities of Utraquism or the Unity of Brethren independently of this reception. Those independent profiles exist in the secondary literature (notably Holeton on Utraquism; Crews, Atwood, and Halama on the Brethren; Daniel and Csepregi on the Slovak Lutheran context) and are presupposed rather than reconstructed here. The chapter's contribution is to take seriously the proposition that in Slovak Lutheran territory between the late sixteenth and the late eighteenth century, the dogmatic difference between confessions was negotiated more durably in the liturgical and hymnological text than in the confessional document — and that this negotiation has its own genre conventions, its own thresholds of fidelity and divergence, and its own intelligible logic.

## 3. Dogmatic Profiles

**Purpose under Option B:** Asymmetric profile-construction. The Slovak Lutheran profile is reconstructed in some depth from the local primary corpus (it is the "receiving" side and must be carefully characterised). The Utraquist and Brethren profiles are presented more concisely, **as positions being received** — drawing on Holeton, Crews, Atwood, and Halama as the standard treatments rather than reconstructing each tradition's full theological identity from primary sources.

### 3.1 Slovak Lutheran identity (deeper reconstruction — the *receiving* side)
- CA and Formula Concordiae as normative backbone
- **Regional confessions: Pentapolitana (1549), Heptapolitana (1559), Scepusiana (1569)** — Csepregi 2003's source-critical revisions are decisive here (Stöckel cannot be sole 1549 author of Pentapolitana; documents show Konzilianz-marked Eucharistic register; never officially recognised, only *taktisch geduldet*)
- **Bardejov Catechism (1556)** as the document where Slovak Lutheran identity meets Brethren-influenced catechetical tradition (Ďurovič: how BK modified Luther; Skoviera "Preludium")
- **Stöckel's annotations on Melanchthon's *Loci Communes* (1561)** — Wittenberg–Bardejov filiation made visible at the dogmatic level
- Key distinctives shown in *lex orandi*: real presence; *sola fide* in the homiletic register of Stöckel's *Postilla* (1596) and *Meditatio passionis Christi* (1572); universal priesthood balanced with ordered ministry

### 3.2 Utraquism, old and neo- (presupposed, not reconstructed)
- Old-Utraquism: conservative liturgical reform within Catholic structures (Holeton's *Studia Liturgica* 1995 is the methodological anchor; Graham 2006's finding of NO Lutheran influence on the Utraquist Mass is a key control)
- Neo-Utraquism: Protestant turn, vernacular liturgy, Agenda Česká 1581 (Benka 2024 critical edition; Chalupský 2024 on baptismal rite)
- Schoen 2022's reframing as full Konfessionskirche via two-phase confessionalisation displaces David's "middle way" thesis — note in passing
- Ecclesiological middle position: episcopal structure without papal submission

### 3.3 Unity of Brethren (presupposed, not reconstructed)
- Radical origins (Chelčický), voluntary church model
- Lhotka 1467: independent episcopate via Waldensian succession
- Sacramental evolution: from early radicalism to mature Confessio Fidei (1535) — Crews 2018 + Atwood 2009 are the standard English-language treatments; Molnár 1948 the foundational Czech monograph
- Blahoslav generation: hymnological peak (Šamotulský 1561, Ivančický 1564 kancionáls); Blahoslav's *Musica* (1569) as theological-musical theory
- Halama 2003 / 2017 on Brethren ethics and social teaching; Atwood 2021 on Brethren introducing themselves to Lutheranism (the directional-reception-mirror moment)

**Sources:** `BookConcord_1580` (CA, FC), `ConfPentapol_1549`, `ConfHeptapol_1559`, `ConfScepus_1569`, `AgCeska_1581`, `Benka_2024`, `Chalupsky_2024`, `ConfFidei_1535`; Stöckel primary corpus (Catechesis 1556, Loci Communes 1561, Postilla 1596, Meditatio passionis 1572); secondary: Csepregi 2003, Csepregi-Konya 2013, Skoviera (multiple), Hajduk (multiple), Witt 2008, Guitman 2017/2019, `Atwood_CzBrethren`, Atwood 2021, Crews 2018, Halama 2003, Molnár 1948, Holeton (BRRP essays + 1995), Schoen 2022, Graham 2006.

## 4. Komparatívna analýza (Comparative Analysis) — CORE

**Purpose:** Systematic comparison organised by theological *loci*. Each locus: normative position → liturgical/hymnological manifestation → comparison → identification of convergence/divergence.

### 4.1 Sviatostná náuka — Eucharistia
- Real presence (Lutheran) vs. spiritual presence (mature Unity) vs. conservative Catholic-adjacent (old-Utraquist)
- *Sub utraque specie*: shared practice, different theological grounding
- Sacrificial character of liturgy: how each tradition handled the Mass/Lord's Supper
- **Hymn evidence:** Eucharistic hymns in CS vs. Brethren kancionáls — textual comparison
- **Liturgical evidence:** Agenda Česká 1581 Eucharistic rite (Benka 2024 critical ed., pp. 131–150) vs. Lutheran agendas; Bystrická agenda (1585) as witness to harder Eucharistic formulations — exploit moderate↔hard spectrum for *lex orandi* argument

### 4.2 Ekleziológia
- Church, ministry, apostolic succession
- Unity's Lhotka innovation; Utraquist Venice ordinations; Lutheran *successio doctrinae*
- Confessio Bohemica as convergence attempt — what it papered over
- **Hymn evidence:** ecclesiological themes in spiritual songs

### 4.3 Soteriológia a etika
- *Sola fide* (Lutheran) vs. ethico-practical emphasis (Unity's sanctification and discipline)
- How soteriological accents appear in hymn lyrics — are Brethren hymns "Lutheranised" in this dimension?

### 4.4 Autorita Písma a tradície
- Approaches to biblical authority and patristic sources across confessions
- Role of *bibličtina* (Kralice Bible language) as shared medium

**Sources:** `BookConcord_1580`, `AgCeska_1581`, `Benka_2024`, `KancSamotul_1561`, `KancIvanc_1564`, `KancJisteb_1420`, `CitharaSanct_1636`; secondary: `David_1999`, `Holeton_Lit`, `GresovaSek_2023`

## 5. Transfer and Reception — ARGUMENTATIVE PILLAR

**Purpose under Option B:** Elevated from "case study" to argumentative pillar. The directional reception thesis is demonstrated here in fine-grained textual detail. §4's locus-by-locus comparison and §5's reception case studies are mutually load-bearing: §4 establishes what differs theologically; §5 shows what those differences look like as editorial decisions in the Slovak Lutheran reception.

### 5.1 Three reception cases (organised by genre, not chronology)

**Case A — Hymn corpus: Cithara Sanctorum (1636+) absorbing Brethren and Utraquist hymnody**
- Tranovský's editorial method: selection, adaptation, theological filtering (Grešová-Sekelská 2021, 2023; Vajda 1944; Ruščin 2019 on the post-1671 Levoča editions; Augustínová 2011 on the bibliographic infrastructure)
- The Sehnal four-stratum analytical grid for Czech baroque kancionál song
- Concrete textual comparisons (PAIR_001 *Ay Panna* / PAIR_004 *Otče nebeský* / PAIR_005 *Nejsvětější* / PAIR_006 *AC-BA Eucharist*): vocabulary, theology, structure
- The *bibličtina* medium: linguistic proximity as both enabler and concealer of theological difference
- The Weisse 1531 → Lukáš → Tranovský → 19c-English transmission chain (Crews 2018) as a documented hymnological-transfer template

**Case B — Catechetical reception: the Bardejov Catechism (1556) modifying Luther's Small Catechism**
- Ďurovič: "Ako Bardejovský katechizmus modifikoval Luthera"
- Skoviera "Preludium Bardejovského katechizmu a Steckelov reformačný humanizmus"
- The Stöckel circle's adaptation in a Slovak humanist register
- What is Lutheranised, what is retained, what is silently dropped

**Case C — Dogmatic reception: Stöckel's annotations on Melanchthon's *Loci Communes* (1561)**
- The annotated *Loci* as a meta-reception document (Wittenberg theology being naturalised in Bardejov)
- Stöckel's *Postilla* (1596) as the homiletic register where this naturalised dogmatics meets congregational use
- Cross-reference to the `stockel_annotation` corpus pipeline producing OCR'd, NLP-annotated versions

### 5.2 What the three cases jointly demonstrate
- The boundaries of acceptable theological diversity for Slovak Lutheran orthodoxy in the 16–18c
- How Czech Reformation material persisted in Slovak Lutheran use long after the Czech-Reformation institutional matrix collapsed
- Why the Czech-Slovak linguistic-confessional history is a distinctively *receptive* phenomenon, not a derivative one

**Sources:** `GresovaSek_2023`, `GresovaSek_Prispevok` (2021), `GresovaSek_2019`, `Vajda_1944`, `Ruscin_2019`, `Augustinova_2011`, `Sehnal_1972`, `Mladejovska_2008`, `CitharaSanct_1636`, `KancSamotul_1561`, `KancIvanc_1564`, Bardejov Catechism 1556 + Ďurovič + Skoviera Preludium + Valčo "Dogmatická pozícia BK", Stöckel *Loci Communes* 1561 + Stöckel *Postilla* 1596 (corpus pipeline outputs), `Weisse_1531` (Stadtbibliothek Nürnberg digital), `Crews_2018`; database: HTB (http://www.clavmon.cz/htb/).
