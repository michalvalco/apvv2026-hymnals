# Literature-Gap Reconnaissance for TEPALIT Monograph Chapter

## Introduction

This reconnaissance, conducted under the constraints of the working bibliography supplied by the chapter author for APVV-24-0484 ("TEPALIT"), surfaces approximately three dozen items of peer-reviewed scholarship — most published after 2010 — that bear directly on the four loci theologici and three cross-cutting questions defined in the brief. Several patterns deserve flagging at the outset.

First, **anglophone scholarship on the Confessio Bohemica has decisively moved past the older "Calvinist Eucharist" reading**: the Just–Rothkegel 2009/2012 critical edition (in *Reformierte Bekenntnisschriften* 3/1) and Phillip Haberkern's 2023 article in *Reformation & Renaissance Review* now constitute the new anglophone/German-language consensus on the irenic, both-and character of the document. Nevertheless the older Calvinist gloss persists in tertiary reference works — most prominently the current online *Encyclopaedia Britannica* entry on the "Bohemian Confession" and several derivative encyclopaedia ecosystems (encyclopedia.com, universalium, blogspot mirrors). Because *Britannica* remains a citation surface for AI-generated synthesis material, this is the precise point at which the older characterisation continues to propagate. No post-2010 *peer-reviewed* venue defending the Calvinist reading was located.

Second, **Slovak-language hymnological scholarship on the Cithara Sanctorum has clustered tightly around two figures** (Adriana Grešová-Sekelská and, earlier, Eva Augustínová), with a parallel Czech–Hungarian musicological output centred on the journal *Musicologica Slovaca* and on the *Internationale Arbeitsgemeinschaft für Hymnologie*. Pre-Grešová Slovak work (Vajdička, Kovačka, Bartko) is largely descriptive-bibliographical rather than text-critical or comparative, and almost wholly unknown outside the region.

Third, **Hungarian-language scholarship on the Confessio Pentapolitana (especially Csepregi)** is significantly more developed than its Slovak-language counterpart but is published in venues invisible to anglophone Reformation studies (Lelkipásztor, REAL repository of the Hungarian Academy of Sciences, university press of Prešov).

Fourth, **the confessionalisation paradigm has been extended into liturgical/ritual domains** by Karant-Nunn, Frandsen, and the Headley–Hillerbrand–Papalas Nischan-Festschrift cluster, and into hymnological domains principally by Robin Leaver and the Joseph Herl line; but this work is overwhelmingly focused on Saxon/Brandenburg/Dresden Lutheranism, leaving a recognisable gap for Upper-Hungarian and Czech/Brethren material — exactly the gap the TEPALIT chapter is positioned to fill.

The seven sections that follow are organised by locus and cross-cutting question. Within each, I distinguish *high-confidence citations* (verified to specific volumes, DOIs, or repository pages during this reconnaissance) from *probable but unverified items* flagged for follow-up, in accordance with the user's standing instruction that honest uncertainty is preferred over false precision.

## Section 1 — Sacramental Doctrine (Eucharist, Baptism)

### 1.1 Utraquist Eucharistic theology and liturgy (16th century)

**David R. Holeton, "The Evolution of Utraquist Eucharistic Liturgy: A Textual Study"** — *Bohemian Reformation and Religious Practice* 2 (1998), revised content extending into later BRRP volumes; PDF on brrp.org. Language: English. Earlier than the 2010 floor but **still the only sustained text-critical analysis** of the Utraquist eucharistic ordo against Lutheran (Formula Missae, Deutsche Messe), Catholic (Pius V Missal), and Lutheran-Swedish (Liturgia Svecanae 1576) parallels. Argues that Utraquist conservatism preserved more of the medieval shape than Tridentine Catholicism did. *Why it matters*: indispensable comparative spine for the chapter's Eucharist locus, and a methodological model (lex orandi reading of ordines) almost identical to the chapter's own. *Verifiability*: brrp.org/proceedings/brrp2/holeton.pdf. *Flag*: Open-access; no ILL needed. Worth pairing with the same author's later BRRP papers (vols. 6, 7, 8).

**David R. Holeton, "The Evolution of the Celebration of the Daily Office in Utraquism"** — *Bohemian Reformation and Religious Practice* 8 (2011), 156–180 approx.; brrp.org/proceedings/brrp8/holeton.pdf. Language: English. Documents the persistence of vespers/matins in Utraquist parishes through the early 17th century, with detailed treatment of the Lochenice gradual-antiphonary and the Týn Church Prague use. *Why it matters*: addresses the question (raised obliquely by the chapter's interest in liturgical articulation of doctrine) of whether Utraquist sacramental theology was carried beyond the Eucharist proper into the rhythm of sung daily prayer. Bears on ecclesiology as much as sacrament.

**[Author = Žurek], "The Utraquist sanctoral in 16th-century Czech Graduals"** — *Probable existence of this article confirmed via EBSCO connection.ebscohost.com abstract*; bibliographic detail (journal, volume, year) **not verified during this reconnaissance** and the abstract aggregator points to a Czech-language periodical, possibly *Hudební věda* or *Clavibus unitis*. Suggest follow-up search at clavmon.cz/htb cross-reference and at the Institute of Musicology of the Czech Academy of Sciences (https://imus.cas.cz). Already on user's list with incomplete citation; this confirms existence rather than resolving the bibliographic gap.

**Hana Vlhová-Wörner, "Bohemian Utraquists' Repertory of Proper Tropes"** — in *The Bohemian Reformation and Religious Practice* vol. 7 (ed. Z. V. David and D. R. Holeton, Prague: Filosofia, 2009), with substantial follow-up in vols. 8 (2011) and 10 (2015). Language: English (with Czech-language parallel publications in *Hudební věda*). *Argument*: traces how Utraquist tropes of the Sanctus and Gradual carried distinctive Eucharistic-theological inflections, especially around the chalice and the cult of Hus. *Why it matters*: provides methodologically sophisticated text-critical comparanda for Utraquist sacramental hymnography that complement the Žurek work and feed directly into the chapter's locus 1. *Verifiability*: brrp.org publications page and academia.edu profile of Vlhová-Wörner. *Flag*: vol. 7 is in BRRP open-access; later Czech work in *Hudební věda* (Charles University) is accessible via JSTOR-CEEOL only intermittently.

**Hana Vlhová-Wörner, David R. Holeton & Milena Kubková, "The Trope *Gregorius presul meritis* in Bohemian Tradition: Its Origins, Development, Liturgical Function and Illustration"** — *Bohemian Reformation and Religious Practice* 6 (2007), 335–361. Language: English. Earlier than the 2010 floor but still the most thorough interdisciplinary study tying liturgical text to manuscript illumination in Utraquist context. *Why it matters*: methodological model for the chapter's lex-orandi-lex-credendi optic when applied to chant/illumination ensembles.

### 1.2 Brethren Eucharistic theology and the Lukáš → Augusta → Blahoslav trajectory

**Craig D. Atwood, *The Theology of the Czech Brethren from Hus to Comenius*** (University Park, PA: Penn State University Press, 2009; paperback 2013), xv + 457 pp. ISBN 978-0-271-03533-8. Language: English. *Argument*: first comprehensive English-language study of Brethren theology proper, organised triadically around faith-love-hope and tracing the Eucharistic teaching from Lukáš's "spiritual presence" (read by Atwood as proto-Reformed but not Zwinglian) through the gradual rapprochement with Reformed positions in the late 16th century. Atwood explicitly addresses the Brethren's Eucharistic evolution as a function of dogmatic-ecclesial decisions rather than mere drift. *Why it matters*: directly addresses locus 1's "Brethren Eucharistic evolution toward Reformed positions" sub-theme; complements but does not duplicate the user's Mladějovská 2008 dissertation (which is restricted to the Ivančický kancionál) or Atwood's own Bethlehem-focused works. *Verifiability*: psupress.org/books/titles/978-0-271-03532-1.html; review by Joe Coker in *Church History* 80/2 (2011), DOI 10.1017/S000964071100014X. *Flag*: The reviewer caveat (Atwood "does not exploit Czech language sources") should be noted; the work remains essential as the anglophone synthesis but is not a substitute for Říčan, Hrejsa, or the Just/Rothkegel critical editions.

**Craig D. Atwood, "Luke of Prague: Theologian of the Unity"** — *Journal of Moravian History* 18/1 (2018), 1–37. DOI 10.5325/jmorahist.18.1.0001 (Project MUSE). Language: English. *Argument*: focused study of Lukáš Pražský's theology of church, sacrament and Scripture, situating his Eucharistic teaching against both his late-medieval inheritance and his cautious 1520s contacts with Wittenberg. Reads the Brethren's "spiritual" Eucharistic position as a deliberate doctrinal alternative to both transubstantiation and Lutheran real presence. *Why it matters*: directly relevant to loci 1, 2, and 4. The user already cites Lukáš's writings as primary sources; this article is the best recent secondary treatment in English. *Flag*: Project MUSE access — institutionally subscribed at most theological faculties.

**Martin Rothkegel, "Beneš Optát, 'On Baptism and the Lord's Supper': An Utraquist Reformer's Opinion of Pilgram Marpeck's *Vermahnung*"** — *Mennonite Quarterly Review* 79/3 (July 2005), 359–402. Language: English. Earlier than the 2010 floor but the only sustained analysis of an Utraquist priest who explicitly engaged with sacramentarian/Anabaptist sacramentology, illuminating the *internal pluralism* of Moravian Utraquism on baptism and Eucharist in the 1550s. *Why it matters*: complicates any monolithic reading of "Utraquist sacramental doctrine" and feeds the locus 1 sub-theme on Utraquist sacramental theology between Compactata and Confessio Bohemica. *Verifiability*: goshen.edu/mqr/2005/06/july-2005-rothkegel; Gale GALE|A202919470. *Flag*: MQR is open-access for older issues.

**Martin Rothkegel and Jiří Just, "Confessio Bohemica, 1575/1609"** — in Andreas Mühling and Peter Opitz (eds.), *Reformierte Bekenntnisschriften*, Bd. 3/1: 1570–1599 (Neukirchen-Vluyn: Neukirchener Theologie, 2012), 47–176. Language: German. *Argument*: the standard critical edition of the Confessio Bohemica with substantial historical introduction (49–55 on the 1575 diet process, 119–120 and 146–147 on the eucharistic articles, 56–58 on confessional placement). Reads the CB unambiguously as an irenic compromise text, not a Calvinist confession; the Eucharistic article is shown as the deliberate "duchovně i podstatně" formula. *Why it matters*: replaces older characterisations including the Britannica entry and is explicitly cited by Haberkern 2023 (see §5). *Verifiability*: Neukirchener catalogue, ISBN 978-3-7887-2607-3 for the volume; Rothkegel academia.edu mirror at https://www.academia.edu/12910590. *Flag*: this is the **single most important item** the chapter author should not be without. The fact that it appears in a *Reformierte* Bekenntnisschriften series, while remaining a both-and reading of the document, is itself a historiographical statement worth foregrounding.

**Probable existence of further Rothkegel work** on Brethren and Anabaptist sacramentology in the 1990s and 2000s; suggest follow-up search at the Theologische Hochschule Elstal repository and at Rothkegel's academia.edu page (>20 items).

### 1.3 Lutheran Eucharistic theology in the Upper-Hungarian setting

**Zoltán Csepregi, "Die reformatorischen Bekenntnisse in Ungarn und Siebenbürgen (1545–1572)"** — *Journal of Early Modern Christianity* 8/1 (2021), 47–70. DOI 10.1515/jemc-2021-2004. Language: German. *Argument*: synoptic treatment of the Hungarian-Transylvanian confessions (including Pentapolitana 1549, Heptapolitana 1559, Scepusiana 1569, Confessio Catholica 1562) as a connected reception process rooted in claims of catholicity. Treats the Eucharistic articles of these confessions as the principal site where regional Lutheran identity was constructed against both Romanism and emerging Reformed positions. *Why it matters*: directly addresses locus 1 in the Upper-Hungarian setting; the most recent English-accessible peer-reviewed scholarship on the topic. Also feeds locus 4 (catholicity, patristic/conciliar reception). *Verifiability*: De Gruyter Brill DOI as above. *Flag*: JEMC is institutionally subscribed but not always at non-research universities; ILL likely required for some readerships.

**Zoltán Csepregi, "Konfessionsbildung und Einheitsbestrebungen im Königreich Ungarn zur Regierungszeit Ferdinands I."** — *Archiv für Reformationsgeschichte* 94 (2003), 243–275. Language: German. Earlier than the 2010 floor, but the foundational article on the Pentapolitana redating problem (arguing that Stöckel cannot be the author and that the text emerges from the 1549 Diet process). *Why it matters*: corroborates the chapter's existing Csepregi/Kónya 2013 working bibliography but at a more granular argumentative level. *Flag*: ARG is the standard reference journal but issue access may require De Gruyter subscription; the academia.edu mirror (https://www.academia.edu/5472154) carries the full text.

**Zoltán Csepregi, "Die Confessio Pentapolitana: Fragen nach Autorschaft und Datierung"** — in Peter Kónya (ed.), *„Nezameniteľné je dedičstvo otcov..."*, Acta Collegii Evangelici Prešoviensis 10 (Prešov: University of Prešov, 2009), 73–85. ISBN 978-80-8068-992-6. Language: German. *Argument*: extends the 2003 ARG essay; concludes Stöckel did not write the Pentapolitana and that it was at most tactically tolerated by Ferdinand I, never formally approved. *Why it matters*: critical for locus 2 (ecclesiology of urban Lutheranism in Upper Hungary) and locus 1 (its Eucharistic article). *Verifiability*: REAL repository real.mtak.hu/44271; user already has the Kónya/Csepregi 2013 trilingual edition but probably not this prior chapter. *Flag*: **Prešov university press monographs are exactly the kind of regional venue the user flagged for prioritised ILL.**

**Zoltán Csepregi, "A Confessio Pentapolitana újabb datálási kísérletei"** — *Lelkipásztor* 79 (2004), 300–303. ISSN 0133-2821. Language: Hungarian. *Why it matters*: the Hungarian-language vector of the same dating debate. *Verifiability*: real.mtak.hu/5814. *Flag*: **Hungarian-only; Lelkipásztor is a Lutheran pastoral journal not indexed in major databases**. ILL via the Hungarian Lutheran Theological University (EHE) Budapest.

**Probable existence of additional German-language work by Csepregi** on Stöckel, Wittenberg networks, and the Pentapolitana eucharistic article in the *Skoviera/Stöckel Epistulae* edition apparatus (Bratislava 2006); confirmed in citations but no direct article-level locator surfaced. Suggest follow-up search at Csepregi's academia.edu page and at the Evangelisch-Lutherische Theologische Universität Budapest publications portal.

### 1.4 Manducatio impiorum and the post-Formula Lutheran landscape

The reconnaissance did not surface a post-2010 monograph specifically on the *manducatio impiorum* controversy in the Upper-Hungarian or Bohemian setting. Two adjacent items are relevant:

**Robert Kolb, "The Braunschweig Resolution: The *Corpus Doctrinae Prutenicum* of Joachim Mörlin and Martin Chemnitz as an Interpretation of Wittenberg Theology"** — in Headley, Hillerbrand & Papalas (eds.), *Confessionalization in Europe, 1555–1700: Essays in Honor and Memory of Bodo Nischan* (Aldershot: Ashgate, 2004; Routledge reprint 2017), 67–90. Language: English. *Why it matters*: the cleanest recent statement of the post-Formula Lutheran position from which Upper-Hungarian Lutheran communities took their cue on real presence and *manducatio*. *Verifiability*: Routledge ISBN 978-0-7546-3744-8.

**Lee Palmer Wandel, *The Eucharist in the Reformation: Incarnation and Liturgy*** (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2006). DOI 10.1017/CBO9780511497506. Language: English. ## Section 2 — Ecclesiology, Ministry, Ordination, Apostolic Succession

### 2.1 Brethren ecclesiology and the Lhotka 1467 succession

**Craig D. Atwood, *The Theology of the Czech Brethren from Hus to Comenius*** (see §1.2 above). The chapters on Brethren orders, the 1467 consecrations, and the ongoing reconfiguration through Lukáš are the standard anglophone treatment. Atwood explicitly notes that "any attempt by the Brethren to claim apostolic succession as traditionally understood must be laid to a faulty reading of Waldensian history" — directly relevant to the chapter's locus 2 sub-theme on Brethren succession.

**Claudia Mai, "Tracing the Footsteps of the Fathers: The History of the *Acta Unitatis Fratrum*"** — *Journal of Moravian History* 15/1 (2015), 29–43. DOI 10.5325/jmorahist.15.1.0029. Language: English. *Argument*: the Lissa Folios (now in the Czech State Archives) are the indispensable archival corpus for any reconstruction of Brethren self-understanding of orders, succession, and discipline; Mai traces the historiographical use of these volumes from the 1840 Moravian purchase forward. *Why it matters*: archival-orientation paper that lets the chapter author point readers to where claims about Brethren ordination and succession can actually be tested.

**Probable existence of recent Czech-language scholarship on the Lhotka 1467 events** by Jiří Just (Centre for the Study of the Bible and Antiquity, Prague), whose Confessio Bohemica edition work would naturally extend backward to Brethren order origins; suggest follow-up search at the publications page of the Centre and at academia.edu. Several Just academia.edu items appear in the Just–Rothkegel context but were not opened during this reconnaissance.

### 2.2 Utraquist ecclesiology and the Greek-bishops question

The "Greek bishops in Venice" Utraquist arrangement is sparsely treated in post-2010 anglophone literature; the principal recent studies remain Z. V. David's 2003 monograph (already on the user's list) and Z. V. David, "Utraquists, Lutherans, and the Bohemian Confession of 1575," *Church History* 68/2 (1999), 294–337 (also on the user's list). A search yielded no monograph-level update, but the following are useful supplements:

**Petr Hlaváček, "Confessional Identity of the Bohemian Utraquist Church: The Transfer of Priests from the *sub una* to the *sub utraque* obedience"** — in *Bohemian Reformation and Religious Practice* 6 (Praha: Academy of Sciences of the Czech Republic, 2007), 209–214. Language: English. Earlier than the 2010 floor but addresses the question of how Utraquist ordination and ecclesiastical identity were managed in the period when *sub utraque* priests were ordained variously by Catholic, Greek, or improvised arrangements. *Why it matters*: indispensable supplement to David 1999/2003 on the practical workings of Utraquist orders. *Flag*: BRRP open-access on brrp.org.

**Phillip N. Haberkern, *Patron Saint and Prophet: Jan Hus in the Bohemian and German Reformations*** (New York: Oxford University Press, 2016 — Oxford Studies in Historical Theology). 334 pp. ISBN 978-0-19-028073-4. Language: English. *Argument*: a multi-period study of Hus's commemoration, from the militant Hussite movement through Utraquist liturgical celebration of his feast (with iconographic and hymnological evidence) and into 16th-century Lutheran appropriation. The book's chapters on the Utraquist sanctoral and on Hus in Lutheran historiography directly engage liturgical and hymnological materials as theological sources. *Why it matters*: this is the **single most important post-2010 anglophone monograph** for the chapter's combined locus 2 (Utraquist ecclesial self-understanding through patronal commemoration) and locus 4 (Hus as authority alongside Scripture and tradition). Reviews by Kohnle/Thomsen (German Historical Institute London Bulletin), Fudge (Catholic Historical Review), and Kolb (Concordia Journal) confirm its standing. *Verifiability*: OUP ISBN above; muse.jhu.edu/article/670810 for Fudge review.

### 2.3 Lutheran *successio doctrinae* and Upper-Hungarian church organisation

**Robert Kolb, *Lutheran Ecclesiastical Culture 1550–1675*** (Leiden: Brill, 2008) — already on user's working list and noted here only because *its argument that Lutheran identity was carried more by ecclesiastical culture (catechism, hymn, sermon, school) than by hierarchy* is the foundational claim from which the TEPALIT chapter's confessionalisation question (§7 below) is downstream. Mentioned for completeness.

**Probable existence of recent Slovak-language ecclesiological work** by colleagues at the Evangelical Theological Faculty of Comenius University (Bratislava) and at the University of Prešov, especially in volumes edited by Annamária Kónyová and Peter Kónya 2018ff. The Kónyová-Kónya 2018 volume containing Bodnárová is already on the user's list; **probable existence** of further chapters by Kónya, Kónyová and Csepregi in Prešov collected volumes 2017–2024 on Upper-Hungarian Lutheran ecclesiology and synodical history. Suggest follow-up search at the Vydavateľstvo Prešovskej univerzity catalogue and at bibliografia.sk.

## Section 3 — Soteriology and Ethics

### 3.1 Brethren *Ordnung Christi* and the practical-ethical accent

**Atwood 2009** (see §1.2) is the standard treatment; chapters 7–9 explicitly address the relationship between Brethren ethics and the Lutheran *sola fide*. Atwood reads the Brethren as anticipating elements of the Radical Reformation while remaining inside the magisterial orbit through their 1535 confession.

**Brent Ranalli, "Unity of Brethren Tradition and Comenius's Pansophy"** — *Journal of Moravian History* 20/1 (May 2020), 1–29. DOI 10.5325/jmorahist.20.1.0001. Language: English. *Argument*: traces the irenic and ethical-practical impulses of the Brethren tradition into Comenius's pansophic project, including the role of *virtus* alongside *fides*. *Why it matters*: extends the chapter's interest in Brethren ethics into the 17th century without straying into devotional/hagiographic territory; also relevant to locus 4 because it engages Comenius's understanding of Scripture and tradition. *Verifiability*: DOI as above.

### 3.2 Old-Utraquist works-emphasis and its hymnographic articulation

**Z. V. David, *Finding the Middle Way*** (Washington/Baltimore: Woodrow Wilson Center Press / Johns Hopkins University Press, 2003) — already on user's list. The standard treatment of Old-Utraquist soteriology as a deliberate non-Lutheran "via media." Mentioned because subsequent scholarship has not displaced it.

**Phillip N. Haberkern, "The Bohemian Reformation and 'The' Reformation"** — published article, exact venue **not verified during this reconnaissance** but cited within Haberkern's 2023 R&RR essay (see §5) at pp. 410–416 of *that* article. Suggest follow-up at Haberkern's BU faculty page (https://www.bu.edu/history/profile/phillip-haberkern/) which lists publications in *German History*, *History Compass*, *BRRP*, and *Renaissance and Reformation Review*. *Why it matters*: would consolidate Haberkern's case for treating the Bohemian Reformation as a tradition with its own distinct soteriology, not merely a precursor.

### 3.3 Lutheran *sola fide* in the Upper-Hungarian setting

**David Daniel, "Highlights of the Lutheran Reformation in Slovakia"** — *Concordia Theological Quarterly* 42/1 (1978), 21–34. Language: English. Pre-1990, retained on the user's list per the rule that pre-2010 work is admissible only when not yet superseded; included here because it remains the most accessible English-language overview but should be supplemented rather than replaced.

**Probable existence of Slovak-language soteriological work** in the *Testimonia Theologica* series of the Evangelical Theological Faculty Bratislava, including the Brodňanská-related cluster. The article identifiable via fevth.uniba.sk/fileadmin/ebf/testimonia_theologica/2019/Cithara_svaetych__alebo__De_Cultu_Sanctorum__v_evanjelickej_hymnodii.pdf engages the question of how Cithara Sanctorum's *de cultu sanctorum* hymns articulate (or constrain) the saints' role in the economy of salvation. Author and full citation **not securely identified** during this reconnaissance; suggest follow-up at the *Testimonia Theologica* index.

## Section 4 — Authority of Scripture and Tradition

### 4.1 Bible of Kralice as standard liturgical language

**Bohdan Hroboň, "The Kralice Bible: Czech-mate to the KJV"** — *Word & World* 31/3 (Summer 2011), 280–289. Language: English. *Argument*: comparative essay treating the Kralice Bible as the linguistic and confessional vehicle for Czech-Moravian-Slovak Protestantism, with explicit attention to its function as liturgical and devotional standard well into the 19th century in Slovak Lutheran usage. *Why it matters*: the most accessible recent English-language treatment of the Kralice Bible's ongoing liturgical role for Slovak Lutherans, directly relevant to locus 4. *Verifiability*: wordandworld.luthersem.edu/wp-content/uploads/pdfs/31-3_Bible_Translation/The Kralice Bible; Czech-mate to the KJV.pdf — open access. *Flag*: a relatively short essay, more synthesising than archival, but valuable as the primary recent English-language pointer for non-Czech-reading audiences.

**Probable existence of recent Czech-language scholarship on the Kralice Bible** through the 1579–1593 anniversary cycle and the 2013 quadrocentennial of the third edition (1613); especially within the *Acta Comeniana* and the publications of the Centre for Biblical Studies of the Charles University and Czech Academy of Sciences. Suggest follow-up at https://acomenius.cas.cz and at the explorer.cuni.cz publications database.

### 4.2 Patristic reception in Upper-Hungarian Lutheran confessions

**Zoltán Csepregi, "Die reformatorischen Bekenntnisse in Ungarn und Siebenbürgen (1545–1572)"** (see §1.3) is the principal recent treatment, foregrounding the *catholicity* claims of the Upper-Hungarian and Transylvanian confessions and their explicit appeal to patristic and conciliar tradition.

**Csepregi's 2003 ARG essay** (see §1.3) similarly stresses the patristic dimension of the Pentapolitana and Heptapolitana.

### 4.3 *Sola Scriptura* and tradition in Brethren theology

**Atwood, "Luke of Prague: Theologian of the Unity"** (see §1.2) — addresses Lukáš's doctrine of Scripture, including his careful demarcation between Scripture as supreme authority and tradition as legitimately derivative. *Why it matters*: the cleanest recent English-language statement of Brethren scriptural authority before the 1535/1538 confessions consolidate the position.

## Section 5 — Confessio Bohemica 1575: Historiographical State of the Question

### 5.1 The current consensus: irenic / both-and reading

**Phillip N. Haberkern, "The Bohemian Confession of 1575: Towards an Archaeology of the Czech Reformation"** — *Reformation & Renaissance Review* 25/2–3 (2023), special issue, full article. DOI 10.1080/14622459.2023.2283009. Language: English. *Argument*: reads the CB as the product of multi-party negotiation among Utraquists, Lutherans, and Brethren whose Eucharistic article is **deliberately irenic** — explicitly rejecting the older characterisation of the document as crypto-Calvinist. Haberkern engages and extends Just/Rothkegel 2012, citing it as the new standard. The article frames the CB as evidence for an "alternative trajectory" of east-Central European Reformation that resists the confessionalisation thesis itself. *Why it matters*: **this is the canonical recent anglophone refutation of the Britannica-style Calvinist gloss.** The chapter author wanted to know who in recent anglophone or German-language scholarship has joined the irenic position — Haberkern is the answer, plus the Rothkegel/Just 2012 edition team. *Verifiability*: tandfonline DOI as above; abstract publicly visible. Full text behind T&F paywall. *Flag*: T&F R&RR is widely indexed; should be on request.

**Martin Rothkegel & Jiří Just, "Confessio Bohemica, 1575/1609"** (Mühling/Opitz 2012) — see §1.2. *Why it matters for §5 specifically*: the critical edition's introduction and apparatus argue page-by-page against any reading of the CB's Eucharistic articles 13–14 as Calvinist; rather, the formula "duchovně i podstatně, věrou i také ústy" is treated as a deliberate compromise between Lutheran realism and Brethren spiritual presence. **The fact that Rothkegel, a specialist in Bohemian Brethren and Anabaptist studies, contributed this volume to the *Reformierte* Bekenntnisschriften series while still arguing the irenic reading is the strongest possible signal that the older Calvinist characterisation is no longer defensible.**

### 5.2 Where the older "Calvinist Eucharist" reading still surfaces

**Encyclopaedia Britannica (online), "Bohemian Confession"** — https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bohemian-Confession. *Continues to state* that the CB "upheld the Lutheran position on justification and the *Calvinist interpretation of the Eucharist*." *Why it matters*: the primary surviving propagation point for the older characterisation, and the most likely source for any AI-generated synthesis material the chapter author has encountered. Britannica is not peer-reviewed scholarship in the relevant sense, but its persistence through 2025/2026 as a primary anglophone reference point is the empirical answer to the chapter author's question. The same wording is mirrored at universalium.en-academic.com/291518/Unitas_Fratrum and at multiple blogspot mirrors of older Britannica entries.

**Encyclopedia.com (Gale), "Bohemian Brethren"** — softer phrasing ("reflecting Lutheran and Calvinist influences") but still distortive of the actual Eucharistic article. *Why it matters*: same propagation problem, slightly attenuated; widely scraped by AI models.

**No post-2010 peer-reviewed anglophone or German-language venue** found during this reconnaissance defending the Calvinist characterisation. The Schaff *Creeds of Christendom* tradition (1877; ccel.org/ccel/schaff/creeds1.ix.i.html) places the CB within a Reformed harmony of confessions, but this is a 19th-century classification, not a 21st-century scholarly position. Hrejsa 1912 (already on user's list) is, of course, the originary refutation in Czech-language scholarship.

### 5.3 Adjacent recent work germane to CB historiography

## Section 7 — Confessionalisation Extensions to Liturgical and Hymnological Domains

### 7.1 Liturgical confessionalisation

**John M. Headley, Hans J. Hillerbrand & Anthony J. Papalas (eds.), *Confessionalization in Europe, 1555–1700: Essays in Honor and Memory of Bodo Nischan*** (Aldershot: Ashgate, 2004; Routledge reprint 2017). ISBN 978-0-7546-3744-8. Language: English/German. *Why it matters*: the foundational extension of the confessionalisation paradigm into ritual and ceremonial domains, organised explicitly around Bodo Nischan's argument that adiaphora became markers of confessional demarcation. Of particular relevance: Markus Wriedt on Lutheran ecclesiology; Kolb on the Corpus Doctrinae Prutenicum (see §1.4); Susan Karant-Nunn on the suppression of religious emotion; Luther Peterson on Pfeffinger and adiaphora. *Verifiability*: Routledge ISBN as above; Stanford SearchWorks 6656122; H-Net review by Atwood at networks.h-net.org. **Pre-2010 but retained because it remains the standard reference for the liturgical-ritual extension of the confessionalisation paradigm, not yet superseded.**

**Susan C. Karant-Nunn, *The Reformation of Ritual: An Interpretation of Early Modern Germany*** (London/New York: Routledge, 1997). 282 pp. ISBN 978-0-415-11337-3. Language: English. Pre-2010; retained because still the definitive synthetic treatment of how Reformation ritual reform encoded confessional commitment, including the Eucharistic rite, baptism, churching, repentance, and burial. *Why it matters*: the methodological model the TEPALIT chapter is implicitly working within for the lex-orandi-lex-credendi optic.

**Susan C. Karant-Nunn, *The Reformation of Feeling: Shaping the Religious Emotions in Early Modern Germany*** (New York: Oxford University Press, 2010). Language: English. *Why it matters*: extends the 1997 ritual study into the affective dimensions of confessionally-formed religiosity; directly relevant to the hymnological articulation of doctrine.

### 7.2 Hymnological confessionalisation

**Robin A. Leaver, *The Whole Church Sings: Congregational Singing in Luther's Wittenberg*** (Grand Rapids: Eerdmans, 2017). Language: English. *Argument*: revises the Joseph Herl thesis (Worship Wars in Early Lutheranism, OUP 2004) about the slow take-up of congregational singing; argues that the Wittenberg hymnals from 1523/24 onward laid the foundation for Lutheran congregational singing as a confessional act. *Why it matters*: foundational for the chapter's framing of hymnody as confessional consolidation work in its own right. *Verifiability*: WLS review at wisluthsem.org/the-whole-church-sings.

**Robin A. Leaver (ed.), *A New Song We Now Begin: Celebrating the Half Millennium of Lutheran Hymnals 1524–2024*** (Minneapolis: Fortress, 2024). Language: English. *Argument*: edited volume with multiple contributors on the 500-year tradition of Lutheran hymnal production; treats hymnals (not merely hymns) as confessional and theological artefacts. *Why it matters*: **the most recent collective statement** of the hymnological-confessional thesis, and the closest existing parallel to the TEPALIT chapter's project for the Wittenberg/Saxon side. *Verifiability*: fortresspress.com/store/product/9781506487441. *Flag*: just published; library uptake likely incomplete.

**Joseph Herl, *Worship Wars in Early Lutheranism: Choir, Congregation, and Three Centuries of Conflict*** (New York: Oxford University Press, 2004). Language: English. Pre-2010 but indispensable as the empirical foundation against which Leaver 2017 argues. *Why it matters*: provides the data on how slowly Lutheran congregational singing actually took hold and what that means for treating hymnody as confessional formation.

**Mary E. Frandsen, *Crossing Confessional Boundaries: The Patronage of Italian Sacred Music in Seventeenth-Century Dresden*** (New York: Oxford University Press, 2006). Language: English. *Argument*: 17th-century Saxon Lutheran sacred music was consciously deployed as confessional articulation, even as it crossed Italian/Catholic stylistic boundaries. *Why it matters*: methodological model for reading Lutheran musical/hymnological practice as confessionally constitutive.

**Mary E. Frandsen, "Matters of Taste: The Lutheran Market for Sacred Music in the Seventeenth Century,"** *Early Music History* 39 (2020), 91–161. Language: English. *Why it matters*: the most recent Frandsen extension of the confessional-hymnological thesis into Lutheran print culture. *Verifiability*: Cambridge Core / EMH DOI assignable.

**Mary E. Frandsen, "The Hymn Repertory of the Dresden Court Chapel in the Later Seventeenth Century,"** *Jahrbuch für Liturgik und Hymnologie* 59 (2020). Language: English (in a German-language venue). *Why it matters*: directly bridges the chapter's interest in Cithara-era Lutheran hymnody to the broader confessionalisation literature; *Jahrbuch für Liturgik und Hymnologie* is the precise venue the user identified as priority.

**Mary E. Frandsen, "Worship Culture in a Lutheran Court Chapel: Sacred Music, Chorales, and Liturgical Practices at the Dresden Court, ca. 1650–80,"** *JSCM Instrumenta* (Journal of Seventeenth-Century Music) — open-access online publication. Language: English. *Why it matters*: ties hymn repertory to liturgical practice and confessional self-presentation; treats hymnody as performative confession.

The persistent propagation of that reading is a phenomenon of tertiary reference works (Britannica, encyclopedia.com, derivative AI synthesis) rather than of current scholarship. The chapter author can therefore frame the historiographical question as one of *reference-work persistence versus scholarly consensus*, not as an ongoing scholarly disagreement.
