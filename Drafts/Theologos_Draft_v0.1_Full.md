## Abstrakt

*Súčasný výskum slovenského luteránskeho spevníka* Cithara Sanctorum *(1636) spresňuje štandardný „luteranizačný" výklad Tranovského kancionála kvantifikáciou zloženia prameňov a zmapovaním jeho diferencovanej editorskej stratégie. Tento článok prináša komplementárny nález v menšom meradle. Bratislavský lyceálny exemplár* Agendy českej *1581 +* Bystrickej agendy *1585 — uchovaný zviazaný v jedinom fyzickom kódexe a po prvý raz kriticky vydaný Petrom Benkom v roku 2024 — vykazuje konfesionálnu stratifikáciu v rámci samotného luteránskeho horizontu. Tlačená* Agenda česká *používa miernejší eucharistický register — výraz „tělo a krev" bez kvalifikujúcich prívlastkov „pravý" či „substanciálny", ktoré charakterizovali polemicky vyhrotený luteránsky diskurz —, pričom hodné prijímanie zakladá na viere v slová ustanovenia. Slovenský rukopisný doplnok, napísaný o štyri roky neskôr, vyostruje tú istú teológiu explicitnou luteránskou* manducatio impiorum*: nehodní prijímajú telo a krv „ne k spasenij, ale k zatraczenij wěčnemu". Vyostrenie sa neudeje na medzikonfesionálnej hranici, ale vnútri samotného luteránskeho horizontu — od miernejšieho českého tlačeného registra s utrakvistickým liturgickým reziduom smerom k ostrejšiemu hornouhorskému luteránskemu rukopisnému registru. Pre tézu luteranizácie z toho vyplýva, že konfesionálne vyostrenie je fenoménom posunu registra pozorovateľným na úrovni jediného fyzického kódexu, a nielen fenoménom medzikonfesionálneho prenosu.*

**Kľúčové slová:** *Cithara Sanctorum*, Tranovský, *Agenda česká*, Bystrická agenda, Banská Bystrica, *manducatio impiorum*, *lex orandi lex credendi*, reformačná hymnológia, vnútrokódexová stratifikácia, konfesionalizácia.

## Abstract

*Recent scholarship on the Slovak Lutheran* Cithara Sanctorum *(1636) refines the standard "Lutheranisation" account of Tranovský's hymnal by quantifying the source mix and mapping his differential editorial strategy. This paper offers a complementary finding at smaller scale. The Bratislava Lyceal Library exemplar of* Agenda Česká *1581 +* Bystrická agenda *1585 — preserved bound together in a single physical codex and edited critically for the first time by Peter Benka in 2024 — exhibits a confessional stratification within the Lutheran orbit itself. The printed Czech* Agenda Česká *uses a moderate Eucharistic register — "tělo a krev" without the qualifying "pravý" or "substanciálny" adjectives that characterised polemically sharpened Lutheran discourse, and grounds worthy reception in faith in the words of institution. The manuscript Slovak supplement, written four years later, sharpens the same theology with explicit Lutheran* manducatio impiorum*: the unworthy receive the body and blood "ne k spasenij, ale k zatraczenij wěčnemu". The intensification operates not at the cross-confessional boundary but within the Lutheran orbit itself, from a moderate Czech printed register with Utraquist liturgical residue towards a sharper Upper-Hungarian Lutheran manuscript register. The implication for the Lutheranisation thesis is that confessional sharpening is a phenomenon of register shift, observable at the granularity of a single physical codex, not exclusively a phenomenon of cross-confessional transfer.*

**Keywords:** *Cithara Sanctorum*, Tranovský, *Agenda Česká*, Bystrická agenda, Banská Bystrica, *manducatio impiorum*, *lex orandi lex credendi*, Reformation hymnology, intra-codex stratification, confessionalisation.

## Zusammenfassung

*Die jüngere Forschung zum slowakisch-lutherischen Gesangbuch* Cithara Sanctorum *(1636) hat das Standardbild der „Lutheranisierung" — verstanden als die konfessionelle Schärfung, die Tranoscius bei der grenzüberschreitenden Übernahme tschechisch-reformatorischen Liedguts in den slowakisch-lutherischen Gebrauch vollzog — durch zwei wesentliche Differenzierungen verfeinert: durch die Quantifizierung des Quellenmaterials, das zu etwa der Hälfte utraquistischer Provenienz ist, und durch die Kartierung von Tranoscius' differenzierter Bearbeitungsstrategie, die in konfessionell neutralen Rubriken zurückhaltend blieb und in den eucharistisch, mariologisch und ekklesiologisch gewichteten Bereichen entschieden eingriff. Der vorliegende Aufsatz fügt diesem Bild einen komplementären Befund auf kleinerem Maßstab hinzu. Das Bratislavaer Exemplar der Lyzealbibliothek der Slowakischen Akademie der Wissenschaften bewahrt die gedruckte* Agenda Česká *1581 zusammen mit der handschriftlichen* Bystrická agenda *1585 in einem einzigen Kodex, den die zeitgenössischen Benutzer beim Binden bewusst zusammengestellt haben. Peter Benkas kritische Edition von 2024 (Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X) hat beide Zeugen erstmals philologisch zugänglich gemacht. Im Vergleich gelesen weisen die beiden Zeugen eine konfessionelle Stratifikation innerhalb des lutherischen Horizonts selbst auf. Der gedruckte tschechische Agendentext arbeitet in einem mäßigen tschechisch-lutherischen Register: Er bezeichnet die eucharistischen Elemente als „tělo a krev" ohne die qualifizierenden Adjektive „pravý" und „substanciálny", die den polemisch zugespitzten lutherischen Diskurs in der Folge der Konkordienformel (1577) kennzeichneten, und gründet das würdige Empfangen im Glauben an die Einsetzungsworte. Das vier Jahre später entstandene slowakische handschriftliche Supplement schärft dieselbe Theologie durch die explizite Aufnahme der lutherischen* manducatio impiorum*: Die Unwürdigen empfangen Leib und Blut Christi „ne k spasenij, ale k zatraczenij wěčnemu". Die Verschärfung vollzieht sich nicht an der konfessionsübergreifenden Grenze, sondern innerhalb des lutherischen Horizonts selbst — vom mäßigen tschechischen Druckregister mit utraquistischem liturgischem Residuum hin zu einem schärferen oberungarisch-lutherischen Handschriftenregister. Für die Lutheranisierungsthese ergibt sich daraus, dass konfessionelle Schärfung ein Phänomen der Registerverschiebung ist, das auf der Ebene eines einzelnen physischen Kodex beobachtbar wird, und nicht ausschließlich ein Phänomen konfessionsübergreifender Übertragung.*

**Schlüsselwörter:** *Cithara Sanctorum*, Tranoscius, *Agenda Česká*, Bystrická agenda, Banská Bystrica, *manducatio impiorum*, *lex orandi lex credendi*, Reformationshymnologie, intra-codiziale Stratifikation, Konfessionalisierung.

## Introduction

The standard account of Juraj Tranovský's *Cithara Sanctorum* (Levoča, 1636) — the foundational Slovak Lutheran hymnal of the early modern period and the principal vehicle through which Czech-Reformation hymnody entered Slovak Lutheran worship for the next three centuries — treats it as the receptor of a confessional sharpening that Tranovský performed on the materials he absorbed.[^2] The Czech and Moravian sources from which Tranovský drew were heterogeneous in their confessional provenance: Utraquist kancionáls, Brethren hymnody from the Šamotulský and Ivančický editions, and Lutheran translations made within the Czech-speaking lands during the previous century. Tranovský's editorial work was the work of integration. He selected, modified, sometimes silently corrected, and on occasion replaced what he found, in order to produce a single hymnological corpus that could serve a confessionally Lutheran community on the eastern frontier of the Habsburg lands. Recent scholarship has refined this picture in important ways: Adriana Grešová-Sekelská has quantified the older Czech repertoire absorbed into the *Cithara Sanctorum* and shown that approximately half of it is Utraquist in origin, a far larger proportion than the older hymnological tradition — which tended to assume Brethren primacy — had recognised.[^3] She has also mapped Tranovský's differential editorial strategy: he was hands-off in confessionally neutral rubrics (the doxologies, the morning and evening hymns, the catechetical settings), and heavily interventionist in the Eucharistic, Marian, and ecclesiological sections, where the cross-confessional source material carried theological weight that an Augsburg-Lutheran compiler had to handle with care.[^4] Peter Benka's critical edition (2024) of the *Agenda Česká* 1581 together with the manuscript *Bystrická agenda* 1585, published as volume X of the *Monumenta Linguae Slovacae* series, has for the first time given scholars a fully edited witness to the liturgical environment in which the Slovak Lutheran reception of Czech-Reformation worship took place.[^5]

These three contributions — Vajda's foundational survey, Grešová-Sekelská's quantification of the source mix and her mapping of Tranovský's editorial differential, and Benka's critical edition of the immediate liturgical context — have each kept the focus on cross-confessional transfer. The interesting confessional movement, on this account, occurs at the moment when material *crosses* between confessional traditions: from Utraquist or Brethren source into Slovak Lutheran reception, where Tranovský performs the work of theological filtering. The *Cithara Sanctorum* is the destination; the cross-confessional transfer is the event; the filtering is the action.

This paper offers a complementary finding at smaller scale. The Bratislava Lyceal Library exemplar of the *Agenda Česká* 1581 — administered today by the Central Library of the Slovak Academy of Sciences — preserves the printed Czech agenda bound together with its manuscript Slovak supplement, the *Bystrická agenda* of 1585, in a single physical codex. Benka's critical edition is the first scholarly work to have published both witnesses together, with full apparatus, in their codicological co-presence as the early users had bound them. Read against each other — and not, as is more usual, in isolation — the two witnesses exhibit a confessional stratification *within the Lutheran orbit itself*. The printed Czech *Agenda Česká* 1581 operates in a moderate Czech-Lutheran register: it uses identificatory language for the Eucharistic elements ("tělo a krev") but without the qualifying adjectives ("pravý", "substanciálny") that characterised polemically sharpened Lutheran anti-Reformed discourse in the wake of the Formula of Concord (1577); it grounds worthy reception in faith in the words of institution rather than in any specifically articulated metaphysical doctrine of the mode of presence; and it is silent on the *manducatio impiorum* — the teaching that the unworthy also truly receive Christ's body and blood, to their condemnation, which the Formula of Concord had just made the boundary marker between Lutheran and Reformed Eucharistic theology. The manuscript Slovak supplement, written approximately four years later by an unknown hand for local use in the mining-town environment of Banská Bystrica and its surroundings, sharpens the same theology in precisely the dimensions where the printed agenda had remained moderate. The *Bystrická agenda* introduces explicit *manducatio impiorum* language: communicants receive the body and blood of the Son of God, the worthy "k spasenij" (for salvation), the unworthy "ne k spasenij, ale k zatraczenij wěčnemu" (not for salvation, but for eternal damnation).[^6]

[^6]: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia: Agenda Czeská 1581 a Bystrická agenda 1585*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Documenta Theologico-Religionistica, Tomus 2. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, pp. 78 – 79. The verbatim quotations from both witnesses, with Benka's analytical observations, will be examined in detail in §§3–4 below.

The intensification operates not at the cross-confessional boundary — both witnesses are Lutheran-aligned, the *Agenda Česká*'s preface declares Augsburg Confession alignment explicitly — but within the Lutheran orbit itself. From a moderate Czech printed register, with traces of Utraquist liturgical residue carried over into the Czech translation of the source German agendas, to a sharper Upper-Hungarian Lutheran manuscript register four years later. From a printed text intended for broad Czech-language pastoral use to a hand-written supplement intended for local use in the Banská Bystrica mining-town milieu. From a 1581 register to a 1585 register, across the years in which the Formula of Concord (1577) was disseminating across the Lutheran territories and the anti-crypto-Calvinist policing of contemporary Lutheran clergy was intensifying.

The implication for the Lutheranisation thesis, this paper argues, is that confessional sharpening is not exclusively a phenomenon of cross-confessional transfer (Brethren or Utraquist source → Slovak Lutheran *Cithara Sanctorum* via Tranovský's editorial hand) but also a phenomenon of intra-confessional register shift, observable at the granularity of a single physical codex bound together by its early users. The *Cithara Sanctorum*'s editorial Lutheranisation in 1636 sits in continuity with the manuscript supplementation that had already been intensifying the Czech printed Lutheran register a half-century earlier in the same broader Slovak Lutheran liturgical landscape. Read as a continuum, the AC 1581 → BA 1585 → CS 1636 trajectory traces a single direction of confessional consolidation. What changes across the three texts is not so much the doctrinal substance — all three are Lutheran-aligned by the standards of their authors and their intended users — as the polemical sharpness with which the doctrinal substance is articulated. *Lex orandi*, in the milieu out of which the *Cithara Sanctorum* eventually emerged, was not a static record of confession but an active site of confessional work.

The paper proceeds as follows. §2 introduces the codex and its scholarly framing: the Bratislava Lyceal Library exemplar, the editorial situation as Benka has reconstructed it, and the historical context of the mining-town Reformation in which both witnesses were used. §§3 and 4 read the *Agenda Česká* and the *Bystrická agenda* respectively, focusing on their Eucharistic articulations and drawing on Benka's verbatim quotations and analytical observations. §5 reads the stratification theologically, locating it within the Formula-of-Concord period's disciplinary expectations of confessional precision. §6 returns to the broader Lutheranisation thesis and registers a brief but consequential observation about the *Confessio Bohemica* of 1575, the trans-confessional document whose own register has been similarly misread in a parallel direction. §7 concludes.

## The codex and its scholarly framing

The Bratislava Lyceal Library — the *Lyceálna knižnica* in the historic Lutheran lyceum of the city, today administered by the Central Library of the Slovak Academy of Sciences (Ústredná knižnica SAV) — preserves a copy of the *Agenda Česká* of 1581 bound together with a manuscript supplement, the *Bystrická agenda* of 1585. The print and the manuscript are not codicologically twins: they were produced separately, four years apart, and brought together by the early users of the codex who recognised them as belonging to a single liturgical use. The print itself is a translation into Czech of liturgical orders compiled from German Lutheran agendas — primarily Wittenberg, Brandenburg, and Württemberg — assembled for the use of clergy who had been ordained in Wittenberg but who served Czech-speaking populations in territories that had passed through the religious experience of the Hussite revolution and its long Utraquist aftermath.[^7] The preface to the printed *Agenda Česká* declares its alignment with the Augsburg Confession and the three ecumenical creeds (Apostolicum, Nicenum, Athanasianum) explicitly; it claims doctrinal unity ("not even the smallest article", in Benka's reading of the preface) among the clergy whose pastoral practice it was to standardise, while acknowledging that ceremonial diversity was the practical problem the agenda sought to resolve.[^8] The print was therefore not a neutral instrument: it was a confessionally identified Lutheran liturgical order, expressed in Czech, for the use of a clergy whose institutional formation was Wittenberg-ordained and whose pastoral remit included a population whose religious memory still carried Utraquist liturgical sensibilities.

The manuscript *Bystrická agenda* is something else. It is a supplement, not a substitute. Its scribe — anonymous, but evidently working within the same Lutheran liturgical-pastoral world as the printed agenda's compilers — assumes the printed agenda and writes alongside it, supplying material for confession and Eucharistic preparation, marriage, and funerals.[^9] It is in the Slovak vernacular of the mining towns rather than the Czech of the printed agenda. It is hand-written rather than printed, with the implication that it was produced for local use rather than for the wider distribution that print enabled. It is dated four years later than the print, which means that it was composed during the period when the disciplinary effects of the Formula of Concord (1577) were still consolidating across the Lutheran territories and when, as Benka documents from the contemporary anti-crypto-Calvinist literature, denial of *manducatio impiorum* was being explicitly listed as a marker for the identification of hidden Calvinists within otherwise Lutheran-aligned clergy.[^10] The supplement was made in this disciplinary atmosphere; it is a local document responding to a regional theological climate.

What matters for this paper is that the early users of the Bratislava codex bound the print and the manuscript together. The act of binding is the codicological encoding of a theological judgement: that these two witnesses belonged to one another, that they served a single liturgical practice, that the supplement was meant to be read alongside, and not against, the printed agenda. Read in isolation — as both witnesses were ordinarily read in earlier scholarship, when they had been catalogued separately and consulted for separate purposes — the print's moderate register and the manuscript's sharper register would each have been taken as freestanding documents of their respective confessional moments. Read together, as the binding records the early users' practice of reading them, they become something else: a witness to a confessional movement *within* a single liturgical use, across the four years that separate the print from the manuscript supplement.

The historical context of the mining-town Reformation in which both witnesses were used must be sketched briefly. The seven Upper-Hungarian mining towns (Banská Bystrica, Banská Štiavnica, Kremnica, Ľubietová, Pukanec, Nová Baňa, Banská Belá) had embraced the Lutheran Reformation in stages from the 1520s onwards, in a process whose pace and form was conditioned by the towns' privileged legal status, their German-speaking patriciate, and their wealth from copper and silver extraction. By the late 1550s the towns were under direct royal pressure to give an account of their religious practice; in 1559 they composed the *Confessio Heptapolitana*, the seven-towns confession, modelled on the Pentapolitana (1549) of the five eastern royal cities and aligned with the Augsburg Confession.[^11] By the 1570s and 1580s, when the *Agenda Česká* was printed and the *Bystrická agenda* was composed, the mining towns' Lutheran identity was institutionally settled, but the confessional disciplinary atmosphere had intensified considerably. The 1548 Pressburg Diet, contrary to a long-standing protestant historiographical assumption, had not tolerated the Augsburg Confession; as Zoltán Csepregi has argued, the diet's measures were aimed at *reformatio catholica*, the restoration of Catholic worship, with anabaptists and "sacramentarians" explicitly named as heretics to be expelled and Lutheran clergy left to defend their orthodoxy under sustained pressure.[^12] The 1558 mining-town synod and subsequent agreements pursued ceremonial uniformity while preserving "Christian freedom" in adiaphora. The 1580 regulations and the *Bystrická agenda*'s own treatment of the relationship between confession and Eucharist link private confession to worthy reception of the Lord's Supper, which in this disciplinary atmosphere served the double confessional function of distinguishing Lutheran practice from both Catholic obligatory confession and Reformed elimination of private confession.[^13]

The codex is therefore not a neutral physical object. It is a witness — and, in Benka's edition, a finally readable witness — to a moment in the consolidation of Lutheran confessional identity in the Upper-Hungarian mining-town milieu, where a Czech-language printed agenda translated from German Lutheran sources met a Slovak-language manuscript supplement composed locally four years later under intensifying disciplinary pressure. The print and the supplement together form a single liturgical use, bound by their early users into a single codex; read against each other, they exhibit a confessional movement that is invisible if either is read alone. The chapters that follow examine that movement in the dimension where it is most clearly visible: the rite of the Lord's Supper.

## The printed Czech register: *Agenda Česká* 1581

The Eucharistic rite of the *Agenda Česká* occupies pp. 131 – 150 of Benka's edition; it is titled "Pořádek Msse weliké, kterýžby při posluhowánij Welebné Swátosti Wečeře Páně se zachowáwati měl" — "Order of the Great Mass, to be observed when administering the Venerable Sacrament of the Lord's Supper".[^14] The structural inheritance is unmistakably Lutheran: the rite preserves the framework of Luther's *Formula Missae* (1523) and *Deutsche Messe* (1526), with all the standard components — Confiteor and Absolution at the entrance, the Liturgy of the Word (Introit, Kyrie, Gloria, Collect, Epistle, Gospel, Credo, sermon), the General Prayer, the Preface and Sanctus, the Admonition to communicants (*Napomenutí*), the Verba Institutionis, distribution sub utraque, the Thanksgiving, and the Aaronic Benediction.[^15] What is striking is not the structure but the language in which the Eucharistic action is articulated within that structure. The structural inheritance commits the rite to a Lutheran liturgical lineage; the language register opens, or fails to open, the question of which Lutheranism — moderate or polemically sharpened — the rite serves.

[^15]: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, pp. 131 – 150 *passim*. The structural homology with the Lutheran Mass-orders of the German lands is discussed in Benka's introductory study at pp. 60 – 70.

A first marker is terminological. The rite carries two designations of the Eucharistic action that coexist in its title and throughout its rubrics: "Msse" (Mass), the traditional Catholic term retained for the liturgical framework, and "Wečeře Páně" (Lord's Supper), the evangelical designation reserved for the sacramental action itself. Two registers coexist. The retention of "Msse" signals liturgical continuity with the late-medieval Czech and Hungarian liturgical inheritance; the specification "Wečeře Páně" signals the confessional reinterpretation that empties the Mass of sacrificial content while preserving its ceremonial framework. This is the inheritance of Luther's own *Formula Missae*, where the term "Mass" was retained even as the canon's sacrificial language was excised. The *Agenda Česká* follows that pattern at the level of vocabulary, and to a 16th-century cleric familiar with the *Formula Missae*'s precedent the terminological doubling would have read as confessional clarity rather than ambiguity.[^16] The sacrament is consistently called "Welebná Swátost" (Venerable Sacrament) — language of high reverence, but reverence whose theological content is left to be specified by the rite's substantive articulations.

[^16]: For Luther's terminological retention, see LUTHER, M.: *Formula Missae et communionis pro ecclesia Vuittembergensi* (1523). In: *D. Martin Luthers Werke. Kritische Gesamtausgabe* [WA], vol. 12. Weimar: Hermann Böhlau, 1891, pp. 205 – 220. The *Agenda Česká*'s adoption of this convention is consistent with the Wittenberg-derived agenda traditions on which Benka shows it depends: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, pp. 14 – 17.

The substantive articulation occurs most fully in the rite's *Napomenutí* — the admonition to communicants placed between the Sanctus and the Words of Institution, at pp. 142 – 144 of Benka's edition. This placement is itself confessionally significant. The congregation receives a sustained theological address explaining what the sacrament is *before* the consecration is performed. Understanding precedes reception. The admonition is therefore the place in the rite where the *lex orandi* speaks most clearly about its own *lex credendi*. Read carefully, it speaks in a register that is identifiably Lutheran in its pastoral accent but moderate in its dogmatic specification.

The admonition's central Eucharistic gloss reads as follows:

> *Wezměte a gezte / to gest Tělo mé / kteréž se za wás dáwá / To gest / že gsem Cžlowěkem včiněn / a cožkoliw činijm a trpijm / to gest wssecko wasse dědičné / a pro wasse lepssij se děge. Toho k gistotnému dokázanij a swědomij dáwám wám Tělo mé za pokrm.*

[Take and eat, this is my Body which is given for you — that is, that I have been made man, and whatever I do and suffer, all of it is your inheritance, and is done for your benefit. For certain proof and testimony of this, I give you my Body for food.][^17]

[^17]: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, pp. 142 – 143. The translation is mine. An exactly parallel formula immediately follows for the cup: "Toho na gisté dokázanij a swědomij / dáwám wám mau Krew k nápogi" ("For sure proof and testimony of this, I give you my Blood for drink"), p. 143.

This is theologically decisive. The Words of Institution — "this is my body" — are interpreted not in terms of the *mode* of presence (transubstantiation, consubstantiation, *in/with/under*) but in terms of *function*: the body is given "k gistotnému dokázanij a swědomij" — "for certain proof and testimony". The sacrament is understood as a seal or pledge of the salvific work accomplished in the incarnation and passion. The body given in the Supper is the same body that was "made man" and that "suffered"; the identification is christological. But the mode by which that body is given is described functionally rather than metaphysically. The rite says enough to be recognisably Lutheran in its insistence that what is given is the body of the incarnate, crucified Christ. It does not say what the contemporary Formula of Concord (1577) had just made the boundary marker of Lutheran Eucharistic theology against Reformed alternatives: that the body is bodily present "in, with, and under" the bread, that it is received orally as well as spiritually, that the unworthy also receive it to their condemnation.

The rite's definition of worthy reception confirms this register choice. Benka extracts the operative formula at p. 79 of his introductory study, where he is precisely concerned to characterise the *Agenda*'s Eucharistic vocabulary:

> *Kdo giž z toho Chléba gij a z Kalicha pige a těm slowům, kteráž od Krysta slyssij a tomu nařízenij, kteréž od Krysta přigijmá, sylně wěřij, tenť gest hodný Host k stolu Páně.*

[Whoever now eats of this Bread and drinks from the Chalice and firmly believes those words which he hears from Christ and that ordinance which he receives from Christ — he is a worthy guest at the Lord's table.][^18]

[^18]: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, p. 79. Benka cites the formula from the *Agenda Česká* itself; the parallel passage in the rite proper is at p. 143 of the edition.

The criterion for worthy reception is *faith in the words of institution*. Not faith in a specific doctrine about the mode of presence; not faith in the universal scope of the bodily reception across worthy and unworthy alike; not faith that articulates itself through any of the polemical markers that the post-1577 Lutheran territories would shortly come to insist upon. A communicant who held the Formula's full position on real presence and a communicant who held a more moderate, Melanchthonian, or even broadly Reformed-spiritual-presence view could both "firmly believe the words" — they would simply understand them differently. The rite does not police the boundary between them.

This silence is what Benka's own analytical observation captures with precision. Discussing the *Agenda Česká*'s Eucharistic passages, he notes:

> *Príslušné pasáže v nej [Agende českej] obsahujú napríklad zmienky o tele a krvi, bez prívlastkov typu 'pravý' či 'substanciálny'.*

[The relevant passages in it [the *Agenda Česká*] contain, for example, mentions of body and blood, without qualifiers of the type 'true' or 'substantial'.][^19]

[^19]: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, p. 79.

The qualifying adjectives — "pravý" (true), "substanciálny" (substantial) — are precisely the markers that the polemically sharpened Lutheran discourse of the post-Formula period attached to "Tělo a Krev" to differentiate the orthodox Lutheran position from the Reformed denial of bodily presence. Their absence from the *Agenda Česká* is not a doctrinal weakness. It is a register choice. The rite operates in a moderate Czech-Lutheran register: theologically anchored in the words of institution and the *Agenda Česká*'s own confessional alignment with the Augsburg Confession, but pragmatically silent on the polemically sharpened distinctives. To put it slightly differently: the *Agenda Česká* says enough to be confessionally Lutheran. It does not say enough to be polemically Lutheran. The two are not the same.

Several further confirmations of this register reading can be drawn from the rite's other rubrics, briefly. The pastoral framing of the sacrament's purpose is distinctly Lutheran: "ta přeswatá Swátost gest k obzwlásstnijmu potěssenij a posylněnij dána / bijdným a zarmauceným swědomijm" — "this most holy Sacrament is given for the special consolation and strengthening of poor and troubled consciences".[^20] This is *consolatio conscientiae* in its purest form, the pastoral accent that distinguishes Lutheran Eucharistic piety from both the sanctifying-transformative emphasis of Utraquist sacramental practice and the disciplinary-communal emphasis of the Unity of Brethren. The longer of the two thanksgiving prayers offered after distribution moves a step further towards presence-language: "že gsy skrze Syna swého milého / tu welebnau Swátost vstanowiti ráčil / w nijžto Tělo geho Swaté gijme / a Krew geho Swatau pigeme" — "that through your beloved Son you have deigned to establish this venerable Sacrament, in which we eat his Holy Body and drink his Holy Blood".[^21] This is the most explicit presence-language in the entire rite. Yet attend to the syntax: "w nijžto Tělo geho Swaté gijme" — "in which [in the Sacrament] we eat his Holy Body". The body and blood are located in the *sacramental action*, not identified specifically with the material elements of bread and wine. The formulation is real-presence-compatible; it is also spiritual-presence-compatible. It does not commit the rite to either side of the boundary the Formula of Concord had drawn.

[^20]: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, p. 142.

[^21]: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, pp. 149 – 150 (Form 2 of the Thanksgiving). Form 1 of the Thanksgiving (p. 149) speaks more generically of "tyto spasytedlné dary" ("these salvific gifts") — even more open in its identification of what has been received.

Located in the broader confessional landscape of 1581, the *Agenda Česká*'s register makes strategic sense. The agenda was compiled by clergy ordained at Wittenberg, drawing on Wittenberg, Brandenburg, and Württemberg agenda sources, for use by clergy ministering to populations whose religious memory carried a Hussite-Utraquist liturgical inheritance and whose confessional identity, while institutionally Lutheran, was practised in a multi-confessional milieu where polemically sharpened formulations would have been pastorally counterproductive. The Czech-language environment in which the *Agenda* was used was not a mono-confessional Lutheran environment of the kind the German territorial churches were consolidating in the same years. It was a frontier environment in which Lutheran clergy ministered to populations whose grandparents might still have communicated *sub utraque* without distinguishing carefully between Utraquist and emerging Lutheran reception. The moderate register absorbs that pastoral situation into its liturgical practice. What the *Agenda Česká* will not do is hand the polemicists of either side — neither the Augsburg-Lutheran disciplinarians nor the residual Utraquist sensibilities of the Czech parish populations — the rhetorical edges they would need for a confessional rupture.

That moderate register is the printed *lex orandi* of the codex. It is not, as the bound supplement will show, the only *lex orandi* the codex preserves.

## The manuscript Slovak supplement: *Bystrická agenda* 1585

The *Bystrická agenda* occupies pp. 265 – 368 of Benka's edition. It is a supplement, not a substitute. Its scribe — anonymous, but evidently working within the same Lutheran liturgical-pastoral world as the printed *Agenda Česká*'s compilers — assumes the printed agenda and writes alongside it, supplying material for confession and Eucharistic preparation (pp. 265 – 302), marriage with a *Haustafel*-influenced moral exhortation (pp. 302 – 340), and funeral hymns (pp. 340 – 368).[^22] The supplement is in the Slovak vernacular of the mining towns rather than the Czech of the printed agenda. It is hand-written rather than printed, with the implication that it was produced for local use rather than for the wider distribution that print enabled. It is dated four years later than the printed *Agenda*, which means that it was composed during the period when the disciplinary effects of the Formula of Concord (1577) were still consolidating across the Lutheran territories and when, as Benka documents from the contemporary anti-crypto-Calvinist literature, denial of *manducatio impiorum* was being explicitly listed as a marker for the identification of hidden Calvinists within otherwise Lutheran-aligned clergy.[^23]

[^22]: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia: Agenda Czeská 1581 a Bystrická agenda 1585*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Documenta Theologico-Religionistica, Tomus 2. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, pp. 265 – 368. The structural layout is summarised at p. 105 of Benka's editorial methodology.

[^23]: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, pp. 77 – 78. Benka discusses Georg Creutzer's handbook, in which the denial of *manducatio impiorum* was listed among the doctrinal markers for identifying crypto-Calvinist clergy.

The Eucharistic-preparation material in the *Bystrická agenda*'s opening section is where the register shift is most clearly visible. Benka extracts the operative formulae at pp. 78 – 79 of his introductory study, locating them precisely in the comparative analytical context that asks how the manuscript supplement relates to its printed companion. The communicants, the *Bystrická agenda* says, receive *Tělo a Krew Syna Božijho* — the Body and Blood of the Son of God. The worthy receive it *k spasenij* — for salvation. The unworthy receive it *ne k spasenij, ale k zatraczenij wěčnemu* — not for salvation, but for eternal damnation.[^24]

[^24]: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, pp. 78 – 79.

This is the explicit Lutheran *manducatio impiorum*. The formulation specifies what the *Agenda Česká* had left unspecified. Both worthy and unworthy receive the *same body and blood*; what differs is the salvific outcome. The body is not selectively present — present to faith and absent to unbelief — but universally present in the Sacrament's action, with the worthiness of the recipient determining whether that bodily presence is received for life or for judgement. This is precisely the formulation that the Formula of Concord, Solid Declaration, Article VII, makes the boundary marker between Lutheran and Reformed Eucharistic theology. FC SD VII §16 (corresponding to Epitome §7) states the position with maximum precision: "Not only the true believers and the worthy, but also the unworthy and unbelievers, receive the true body and blood of Christ; however, not for life and consolation, but for judgement and condemnation."[^25] The *Bystrická agenda*'s formulation is the same position in different words, in Slovak rather than in Latin, in a manuscript supplement to a Czech printed agenda rather than in a continental confessional document. The position is held; the language register has moved.

[^25]: For the Latin and German texts of the Formula of Concord, Solid Declaration, Article VII (1577), see KOLB, R. – WENGERT, T. J. (eds.): *The Book of Concord: The Confessions of the Evangelical Lutheran Church*. Minneapolis: Fortress Press, 2000, pp. 591 – 615; for the older Triglot edition (Latin, German, English in parallel), see BENTE, F. – DAU, W. H. T. (eds.): *Concordia Triglotta*. St. Louis: Concordia Publishing House, 1921, pp. 971 – 1015. The corresponding Latin formula of FC SD VII reads: "*Veracissime non solum vere credentes et digni, sed etiam indigni et increduli verum corpus et verum sanguinem Christi accipiant; sed non ad vitam et consolationem, verum ad iudicium et condemnationem*" (Triglot ed., p. 990, §16).

Benka's own analytical framing makes the comparative point with characteristic compression. The manuscript *Bystrická agenda*, he observes, contains "*formulácie ktoré explicitnejšie smerujú k luteránskemu manducatio impiorum*" — formulations that more explicitly point towards Lutheran *manducatio impiorum*.[^26] The comparative adjective is the analytical move. Not "formulations that introduce", or "formulations that depart from the printed Czech original", but "formulations that *more explicitly point towards*" the Lutheran distinctive. The same theology is being said, with sharper edges. The implicit position of the *Agenda Česká* — that the rite is Lutheran-aligned, that the words of institution are decisive, that worthy reception requires faith in those words — is in the manuscript supplement made *explicit*, with the polemical specifications attached that the print had left unspoken. The continuity between the two witnesses is preserved. What has changed is the disciplinary register in which that continuity expresses itself.

The temporal context matters. Four years separate the print (1581) from the manuscript supplement (1585). Those four years lie within a confessional disciplinary moment that the German Lutheran territories had begun to enforce more systematically after the Formula of Concord's promulgation in 1577 and its subsequent territorial reception through the Book of Concord (1580). Within that period, the visitation literature, the polemical handbooks, and the synodal proceedings of the Lutheran lands were all converging on a sharpened set of doctrinal markers by which clergy were to be tested for confessional reliability. *Manducatio impiorum* was prominent among them, precisely because its denial was the diagnostic that polemicists like Creutzer used to identify hidden Calvinist sympathies among nominally Lutheran clergy.[^27] In the Upper-Hungarian mining towns of the same period, this disciplinary atmosphere would have been mediated through several channels: the clergy's own continuing connections to Wittenberg and the German territorial churches, the visiting clergy who circulated between the Pentapolitana cities and the mining-town centres,[^28] and the polemical printed material that Benka's introductory study documents reaching the Banská Bystrica milieu in this period. The *Bystrická agenda*'s explicit *manducatio impiorum* is an act recognisable within that atmosphere. The manuscript supplement does what the printed agenda did not: it equips the local clergy with the polemically sharpened formulae that the contemporary Lutheran disciplinary moment had begun to require.

Leiden: Brill, 2008.

The print-versus-manuscript distinction is not incidental to the register shift. Manuscript supplementation allowed local adaptation that print constrained. A printed Czech agenda, intended to circulate among Wittenberg-ordained clergy across the Czech-speaking Lutheran territories, had to be theologically usable in many local pastoral situations; its register could not be too sharp without alienating parish populations whose Utraquist liturgical memory was still living. A manuscript Slovak supplement, written for local use in a single mining-town milieu where the Lutheran identity was institutionally settled and the disciplinary atmosphere intensifying, could afford to be sharper. The codex preserves both registers because the binding records the early users' practice of moving between them: the printed *Agenda Česká* for the broader liturgical structure and the moderate pastoral framing, the manuscript *Bystrická agenda* for the sharpened confessional articulation that the mining-town clergy needed for their own work. Print and manuscript were not in tension within the codex. They were complementary registers within a single liturgical use.

A final feature of the *Bystrická agenda*'s register is the linkage between confession and Eucharist. Private confession (*súkromná spoveď*) is foregrounded in the manuscript supplement as the proper preparation for communion: it is to take place the evening before or the early morning of communion, and the link is explicit in the rubrics.[^29] Benka documents the same linkage in the 1558 mining-town synod and the 1580 regulations.[^30] Within the contemporary Lutheran disciplinary atmosphere, this linkage performed a double confessional function. It distinguished Lutheran practice from Catholic obligatory confession, since confession in the Lutheran sense was pastoral rather than legally enforced; and it distinguished Lutheran practice from the Reformed elimination of private confession altogether, which by the 1580s had become a confessional marker on the Reformed side. The *Bystrická agenda*'s sustained treatment of confession-as-Eucharistic-preparation is therefore not merely a pastoral provision but a confessional positioning. The same theology of communion that the printed *Agenda Česká* had articulated in moderate terms is, in the manuscript supplement, embedded in a disciplinary practice — confession before communion — that polemically marks the rite as Lutheran against both Catholic and Reformed alternatives. The register shift extends across the rite's disciplinary structure as well as its dogmatic articulation.

[^30]: BENKA, P. (ed.): *Agenda Neosoliensia*. Monumenta Linguae Slovacae X. Bratislava: Univerzita Komenského, 2024, pp. 73 – 77.

The Bratislava codex therefore preserves, in its bound co-presence of print and manuscript, two registers of the same Lutheran Eucharistic theology. The printed Czech *Agenda* operates moderately: identificatory but not polemically specified, anchored in faith in the words of institution but silent on *manducatio impiorum*, accommodating to a Czech-language pastoral environment that still carried Utraquist liturgical memory. The manuscript Slovak supplement operates sharply: the same theology articulated with the polemical specifications that the post-Formula disciplinary atmosphere had begun to require, embedded in a confession-and-Eucharist linkage that polemically marks the rite as Lutheran against both Catholic and Reformed alternatives. The register shift between the two witnesses is not a doctrinal break. It is the same confessional commitment articulating itself with sharper edges as it crosses the boundary from print to manuscript, from Czech to Slovak, from a 1581 register to a 1585 register, from a translation of German agendas to a local supplement composed in the mining-town milieu under intensifying confessional discipline.

What this stratification means for the broader Lutheranisation thesis — and for the *Confessio Bohemica* of 1575, whose own register has been similarly misread in a parallel direction — is the question to which §§5 and 6 turn.

## Reading the stratification

The codex preserves a stratification, not a contradiction. What the *Agenda Česká* says and what the *Bystrická agenda* says are not in tension; they are the same theological commitment articulated in different registers. The interpretive task is therefore not to adjudicate between the two witnesses but to read them as a structured spectrum — to ask why a single liturgical use accommodated both, and what the accommodation reveals about the period's confessional work.

A first clarification. Register and doctrine are distinct things. Doctrine is the substance of theological commitment — the propositions a community holds true, the boundaries it draws, the formulations it confesses. Register is the manner in which doctrine is articulated — its lexical choices, its level of polemical specification, its silences, its emphases. Two texts can hold the same doctrine and articulate it in different registers; what they share is the substance, what differs is the shape. To say that the *Agenda Česká* and the *Bystrická agenda* hold the same Eucharistic doctrine is not to flatten the difference between them. It is to identify what the difference *is* — a difference of register, not of substance — and to attend to what register-difference does in the lived practice of a community.

The shape of the *Agenda Česká*'s register is moderate Czech-Lutheran. It is the register of a print intended for clergy ministering across the Czech-language Lutheran territories, where the pastoral situation included parish populations whose grandparents had communicated *sub utraque* without the polemical specifications that the Formula of Concord had just made the diagnostic of confessional Lutheran identity. The *Agenda*'s silence on *manducatio impiorum* and on the "in, with, and under" formula is therefore not theological reticence but pastoral pragmatism — a register choice, made by Wittenberg-ordained compilers, that allowed the printed agenda to function across a wider range of Lutheran pastoral situations than a sharper register would have admitted. The shape of the *Bystrická agenda*'s register, by contrast, is sharper Upper-Hungarian Lutheran. It is the register of a manuscript supplement composed for local use in the mining-town milieu, in 1585, four years after the Formula of Concord's territorial reception through the Book of Concord (1580) had begun to consolidate confessional discipline in the German Lutheran territories. The *Bystrická agenda*'s explicit *manducatio impiorum* equips the local clergy with the polemically sharpened formulae that the contemporary disciplinary moment had begun to require — formulae that the *Agenda Česká*'s broader pastoral remit had constrained it from supplying.

Read theologically, the codex is therefore a *lex orandi* document with two voices. The classical formula — *legem credendi lex statuat supplicandi*, "let the law of prayer establish the law of belief", attributed to Prosper of Aquitaine — assumes a single voice in liturgy that establishes the corresponding belief.[^31] What the codex shows is that liturgy can hold multiple voices simultaneously, even within a single bound use. The early users who bound the print and the manuscript together did not perceive a tension between the two registers; they perceived a complementarity. The print supplied the broader liturgical structure and the moderate pastoral framing; the manuscript supplied the sharper confessional articulation that the local clergy needed for their disciplinary work. Both belonged to the *lex orandi* of the mining-town Lutheran community in 1585. Both established, in their distinct ways, the corresponding *lex credendi*.

This has implications for how the period's confessional sharpening should be understood. The standard narrative of confessionalisation, articulated most influentially by Heinz Schilling and Wolfgang Reinhard, treats confessional consolidation as the work of normative documents — confessions of faith, catechisms, church orders — and of the disciplinary infrastructures that enforced them.[^32] The codex extends this picture in an instructive direction. Confessional consolidation is also the work of register intensification within liturgical practice — and that work is observable at the granularity of a single physical codex, in the four-year gap between a printed pastoral resource and its hand-written local supplement, in the lexical move from "tělo a krev" to "Tělo a Krew Syna Božijho", in the introduction of *manducatio impiorum* into a rite that had previously not articulated it. Register work is not less serious than doctrinal work. It is what doctrinal work looks like in the lived practice of a community making the substance of confession into a usable shape for liturgical action.

What the *Agenda Česká* / *Bystrická agenda* stratification shows, then, is not that Lutheranism between 1581 and 1585 was developing a new doctrine of the Eucharist — it was not — but that the same Lutheran doctrine was being expressed at different registers within a single regional confessional community, with the manuscript supplement intensifying what the printed agenda had left moderate. This is confessional consolidation as register shift. It operates not at the cross-confessional boundary but within the Lutheran orbit itself. It is visible only at the codex level, where the bound co-presence of print and manuscript records the early users' practice of moving between registers. And it is invisible at the level of the period's normative documents — the Formula of Concord, the Heptapolitana, the *Agenda Česká*'s preface — which are univocally Lutheran and offer no purchase for the diagnostic that codicological co-presence supplies.

## Implications for the Lutheranisation thesis

**The Confessio Bohemica 1575**

A separate but related finding from the broader research programme of which this paper is part deserves brief mention here, because it traces a parallel pattern at a different scale and in a different document. The *Confessio Bohemica* of 1575 — the irenic compromise document drawn up by Utraquists, Brethren, and Lutherans of the Bohemian crown lands and presented to Maximilian II as evidence of confessional consensus among the non-Roman religious parties — has been characterised in older anglophone reference works as a Calvinist document.[^33] The characterisation does not survive a careful reading of the Czech text. The CB's Eucharistic article uses the formula *duchovně i podstatně, věrou i také ústy* — "spiritually and substantially, by faith and also by mouth" — which explicitly affirms both substantial presence and oral reception (the Lutheran distinctives) and spiritual reception by faith (the Reformed emphasis).[^34] This is not a Calvinist position. It is a deliberate both/and irenic register that holds the Lutheran and Reformed distinctives together in a single confessional formula. A Lutheran could subscribe to it without conscience; so could a Reformed theologian; so, for that matter, could a moderate Utraquist. The CB's irenic register was the achievement of the 1575 compromise, not a confessional concession to either side.

The CB is also conspicuously silent on *manducatio impiorum*. The very test the Formula of Concord makes the Lutheran boundary marker against Reformed Eucharistic theology in 1577 is absent from the CB's 1575 articulation. The silence is the same silence the *Agenda Česká* will keep in 1581 within a Lutheran-aligned codex. The *Bystrická agenda* of 1585 is the document that breaks that silence locally. Read together, the CB 1575 → AC 1581 → BA 1585 trajectory traces a pattern: a cross-confessional irenic document, a moderate Lutheran printed agenda, a sharper Lutheran manuscript supplement, each negotiating in its own way the question of how the Eucharistic distinctive should be articulated. The CB and the *Agenda Česká* share a register choice — silence on *manducatio impiorum* — that the *Bystrická agenda* breaks. The cross-confessional irenicism of the CB 1575 and the intra-confessional moderation of the *Agenda Česká* 1581 are not the same thing, but they share a register strategy: holding the polemical specification at bay in the interest of broader pastoral and confessional usability. The *Bystrická agenda* 1585 abandons that strategy under local disciplinary pressure.

A fuller treatment of the *Confessio Bohemica*'s register and its consequences for understanding the Czech-Reformation confessional landscape is reserved for the forthcoming TEPALIT chapter. The brief mention here registers the parallel pattern at a different scale and offers a suggestive observation: the older characterisation of the CB as Calvinist is not just historiographically inaccurate but conceptually misleading. It misses the very thing that makes the CB an interesting document — its deliberate both/and register, its silence on the post-1577 confessional markers that did not yet exist when it was drafted, and its kinship in register strategy with the moderate Lutheran liturgical documents that would follow it in the same broader regional setting.

**The Cithara Sanctorum's editorial method**

Tranovský's differential editorial strategy in the *Cithara Sanctorum* of 1636, as Grešová-Sekelská has mapped it, fits naturally within the broader pattern this paper documents. Tranovský was hands-off in confessionally neutral rubrics and heavily interventionist in the Eucharistic, Marian, and ecclesiological sections, where the cross-confessional source material carried theological weight that an Augsburg-Lutheran compiler had to handle with care.[^35] The action Tranovský performs at cross-confessional crossings — Brethren or Utraquist source moving into Slovak Lutheran reception, with editorial filtering attaching the polemical specifications that the Slovak Lutheran community required — is the same kind of action the *Bystrická agenda*'s anonymous scribe had performed a half-century earlier at a smaller intra-confessional crossing. Both are register work. Both are confessional sharpening. The action is the same; only the scale differs. The *Cithara Sanctorum* of 1636 is therefore not the first instance of Slovak Lutheran register work on Czech-Reformation source material; it is the most visible instance of a longer pattern that the codex preserves at finer granularity.

The finding documented in this paper — the *Agenda Česká* 1581 / *Bystrická agenda* 1585 stratification within the Bratislava Lyceal Library exemplar — is recorded in the open *TEPALIT Digital Companion* dataset under the identifier `PAIR_006_AC_BA_EUCHARIST`.[^36] The dataset is published under a Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International licence. The record carries the trust signature FACTUAL / DOCUMENTED / VERIFIED — the strongest combination available in the dataset's three-axis epistemic-transparency framework — with a verification dossier documenting the warrant. The mention here gives readers a path to the underlying data and registers the open-scholarship infrastructure within which this finding was developed.

Two directions for further work suggest themselves. The first is internal to Benka's edition: a systematic textual comparison of the *Agenda Česká* and the *Bystrická agenda* outside the Eucharistic rite, examining the baptism, marriage, and funeral sections to see whether the intra-codex register shift documented in this paper is Eucharist-specific or operates more generally across the rite. This work is feasible from Benka 2024 alone; it would extend the diagnostic developed here to additional dimensions of Lutheran liturgical articulation. The second is external: examination of additional bound-codex situations in Upper-Hungarian Lutheran archives — Bardejov, Levoča, Kežmarok, and the Spiš towns — for similar intra-codex stratifications. This work requires archival access and codicological reading attentive to the kind of register-difference that the Bratislava codex preserves. Both directions, pursued together, would test whether the intra-codex confessional stratification documented here is a distinctive feature of one bound exemplar or a more widespread pattern in the period's liturgical-pastoral practice.

## Conclusion

The Bratislava Lyceal Library exemplar of *Agenda Česká* 1581 + *Bystrická agenda* 1585 preserves, in its bound co-presence of print and manuscript, two registers of the same Lutheran Eucharistic theology. The printed Czech agenda operates moderately, anchored in faith in the words of institution but silent on the polemical distinctives the post-Formula disciplinary atmosphere had begun to require. The manuscript Slovak supplement, written four years later in the mining-town milieu, articulates the same theology with explicit *manducatio impiorum* and direct identification of the body and blood of the Son of God in worthy and unworthy reception alike. The early users of the codex bound the print and the manuscript together because they served a single liturgical use. Read together, as the binding records their practice of reading them, the two witnesses exhibit a confessional movement that is invisible if either is read alone.

What the stratification means for the Lutheranisation thesis is the substance of this paper's contribution. Confessional sharpening, on the standard account, is the work performed at the moment when material crosses between confessional traditions — Brethren or Utraquist source into Slovak Lutheran reception, the editorial filtering that produces a confessionally Lutheran corpus from a confessionally heterogeneous inheritance. The codex shows the same action operating at smaller scale and at finer granularity, within the Lutheran orbit itself, in the four-year gap between a printed pastoral resource and its hand-written local supplement. The action is the same; only the scale differs. Confessional consolidation is also intra-confessional register work, observable at the codex level when the witnesses are read against one another rather than in isolation. Where this leaves the *Cithara Sanctorum*'s own editorial register, and what the same diagnostic might reveal when applied to Tranovský's hymnodic work rather than to the printed agenda that preceded it, is the work of the forthcoming TEPALIT chapter.

The *lex orandi*, in the milieu out of which the *Cithara Sanctorum* eventually emerged, was not a static record of confession. It was an active site of confessional work. Sometimes that work happens at cross-confessional crossings; sometimes it happens within a single bound use. The codex preserves both, and the preservation matters.
