Topic of the chapter
The chapter performs a comparative dogmatic analysis of three early-modern confessional traditions — Lutheranism (specifically the Augsburg-Confession Lutheranism that consolidated in Upper Hungary / present-day Slovakia), Utraquism (in both its Old- and Neo-Utraquist forms), and the Unity of Brethren (Unitas Fratrum) — through the lens of liturgical and hymnological sources from the sixteenth, seventeenth, and eighteenth centuries. The methodological framework treats the lex orandi, lex credendi tradition as a productive analytical optic: dogmatic commitments are read through their liturgical and hymnological articulation, not only through normative confessional documents.
The chapter's geographical centre of gravity is Bohemia, Moravia, and Upper Hungary (today's Slovakia), with extensions into the broader Holy Roman Empire where the comparative material requires it (Wittenberg, Leipzig, Augsburg, Prague). The chapter language is Slovak; the audience is the TEPALIT monograph readership (Central European Reformation historians, Lutheran systematic theologians, hymnologists).
The four theological loci
The chapter's comparative analysis is organised around four loci theologici:

Sacramental doctrine — Eucharist (real presence, manducatio impiorum, spiritual presence, sacramental substantiality, sub utraque specie), and to a lesser extent baptism.
Ecclesiology — doctrine of the church, ministry, ordination, and apostolic succession (with attention to the particular Utraquist arrangements involving Greek bishops in Venice, the Brethren's Waldensian succession via Lhotka 1467, and the Lutheran successio doctrinae position).
Soteriology and ethics — the relation between the Lutheran sola fide / forensic justification accent and the Czech Reformation's ethical-practical accents (Brethren Ordnung Christi, Old-Utraquist works-emphasis, the question of libertas christiana).
Authority of Scripture and tradition — sola scriptura, the role of patristic reception, vernacular Bible translation (especially the Bible of Kralice, 1579–1593, and its use as the standard liturgical language for both Czech Protestants and Slovak Lutherans).
