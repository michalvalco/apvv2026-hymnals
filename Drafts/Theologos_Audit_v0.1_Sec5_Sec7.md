## A. Substantive issues (recommend addressing now)

### A.1. The §6 "Implications for the Lutheranisation thesis" section runs four sub-arguments without sub-headings, which makes the section's structure harder to follow than necessary

**Location:** §6, the entire section.

**Issue:** §6 contains four distinct moves: (i) the CB 1575 paragraph (~480 words), (ii) the CS editorial method re-read (~200 words), (iii) the PAIR_006 dataset reference (~150 words), (iv) future work directions (~150 words). The outline explicitly distinguishes these four sub-arguments. In the draft as written they appear as five paragraphs of unbroken prose under a single heading, with no internal markers. A reader following the argument has to do extra work to parse where one sub-argument ends and the next begins.

The Theologos house style as observed in the Stöckel 2022 article does not use numbered sub-headings within sections. It uses bold sentence-case sub-headings sparingly. The §6 of our draft is the section that most clearly *needs* internal markers, because it explicitly does multiple distinct things. Other sections (§§1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7) flow as single arguments and do not need sub-headings.

**Fix options:**
- (a) Add two or three short bold sentence-case subheadings within §6 to mark the transitions: e.g., "**The Confessio Bohemica 1575**", "**The Cithara Sanctorum's editorial method**", "**The PAIR_006 dataset and future work**".
- (b) Add a bridging sentence at each sub-argument transition that signals the move (e.g., "Beyond the Confessio Bohemica, the broader pattern this paper documents has implications for…", "This finding is recorded in…", "Two directions for further work…").
- (c) Leave as is and trust the reader.

**Recommendation:** Option (a). The Theologos audience is academic but not all readers will be Reformation specialists; sub-headings within §6 would make the structure easier to follow and would also signal at a glance that §6 is doing multiple things, not one. The Stöckel 2022 article uses bold sentence-case sub-headings sparingly within longer sections; this is consistent with house style.

If Michal prefers no sub-headings, option (b) is the next-best move — adding three or four bridging sentences would do most of the navigational work without breaking the prose flow.

### A.2. The Conclusion (§7) repeats the central claim from §1 closely enough that some readers may experience it as redundant

**Location:** §7, the entire conclusion.

**Issue:** §7's three paragraphs recapitulate (1) the codex finding, (2) the Lutheranisation-thesis implication, and (3) the *lex orandi* note. These three moves are exactly what §1's introduction laid out. §7 is doing what conclusions are supposed to do — summarising — but in the current draft the summary is close enough to §1's statement that a reader who has just finished §§5 and 6 may experience §7 as familiar territory.

The standard fix for this is to do at the conclusion what an introduction does *not* do: gesture beyond the paper, not just synthesise within it. §7's first two paragraphs are synthesis; §7's third paragraph (the *lex orandi* sentence) is the only place that does forward-looking work.

**Fix options:**
- (a) Compress §7 from ~330 words to ~250 words by trimming the synthesis material in paragraphs 1–2, leaving the *lex orandi* paragraph as the load-bearing close.
- (b) Add a forward-looking sentence in paragraph 2 — something that gestures toward what the chapter (TEPALIT) or the broader research programme will do with this finding. E.g., "The further question — what the same diagnostic, applied to the Cithara Sanctorum's hymnodic content rather than to its agenda companion, would reveal about Tranovský's editorial register — is the work of the forthcoming TEPALIT chapter."
- (c) Leave as is.

**Recommendation:** Option (b). Keeping §7 at its current length (~330 words) is fine for a paper of this length; the issue is not bulk but redundancy. Adding one forward-looking sentence about what comes next gives §7 a different orientation from §1 and earns its place as a distinct section. The TEPALIT chapter forward reference is appropriate and aligns with the funding acknowledgment at footnote 1.

## B. Minor inconsistencies (fold into revision pass)

### B.1. The §5 use of "post-Formula" appears three times in one section without variation

**Locations:** §5, paragraph 3 ("the polemical specifications that the Formula of Concord had just made the diagnostic of confessional Lutheran identity"); §5, paragraph 3 ("four years after the Formula of Concord's territorial reception through the Book of Concord (1580) had begun to consolidate confessional discipline"); §5, paragraph 5 (footnote 32 reference and "the post-Formula disciplinary atmosphere"); §6, paragraph 2 ("the very test the Formula of Concord makes the Lutheran boundary marker"); §6, paragraph 3 ("the post-1577 confessional markers that did not yet exist when it was drafted"); §7, paragraph 1 ("the polemical distinctives that the Formula of Concord had just made the boundary marker").

**Issue:** "Formula of Concord" appears six times across §§5–7. "Post-Formula" appears twice. "Post-1577" once. "FC SD VII" zero times in §§5–7 (it appears in §4). The terminological variation is fine; the issue is that three sections in a row (§§5, 6, 7) all anchor their argument around the FC, which gives the closing third of the paper a slightly repetitive doctrinal framing.

**Fix:** Minor pruning would help. The §7 first paragraph's invocation of the FC ("the polemical distinctives that the Formula of Concord had just made the boundary marker between Lutheran and Reformed Eucharistic theology") could be compressed to "the polemical distinctives the post-Formula disciplinary atmosphere had begun to require" — using the same shortened phrase that §5 uses, avoiding repetition of the longer formula. This would also tighten §7 by ~12 words, helping the conclusion compactness identified in A.2.

### B.3. Inconsistent use of "AC" / "Agenda Česká" and "BA" / "Bystrická agenda" abbreviations across sections

**Locations:** §§1–4 mostly spell out *Agenda Česká* and *Bystrická agenda* in full, with abbreviations "AC" and "BA" appearing in specific contexts (§1: "AC 1581 → BA 1585 → CS 1636 trajectory"; §4: "the AC and BA"). §§5–7 mix more freely: §5 uses "the AC/BA stratification" once; §6 uses "AC 1581" and "BA 1585" in the trajectory but spells out the full names elsewhere; §7 spells out the full names.

**Issue:** Mild. The mix is not actively inconsistent — the full names dominate, and the abbreviations cluster around the trajectory diagram (CB 1575 → AC 1581 → BA 1585 → CS 1636) where short-form is appropriate. But for a paper of ~7,200 words, the convention should be: spell out at first use in each section, abbreviate thereafter only when unambiguous.

**Fix:** In §5, change "the AC/BA stratification" (paragraph 6) to "the *Agenda Česká* / *Bystrická agenda* stratification" — already done in the current draft. **Re-checking the file confirms this fix is already in place.** Other instances are clean.

**No fix required.**

## C. Stylistic items (Michal's judgment)

### C.1. The §5 paragraph 4 sentence "Both belonged to the *lex orandi* of the mining-town Lutheran community in 1585. Both established, in their distinct ways, the corresponding *lex credendi*."

**Location:** §5, end of the *lex orandi* paragraph.

**Issue:** This is a rhetorical pair — two short sentences each beginning with "Both." It is the strongest rhetorical move in §5 and arguably the strongest in the whole paper. It is also slightly poetic for a Theologos paper, which usually maintains a more sober register.

The rhetorical pair earns its place because §5 is making a conceptual claim (the codex preserves two voices that together establish a single *lex credendi*) that needs a memorable formulation. The two-sentence rhythm makes the claim land.

**My judgment:** Keep as is. The rhetorical pair is the right move at the right place. Theologos articles do allow occasional rhetorical highlights (the Stöckel 2022 article has a couple of comparable moments). If Michal wants to soften, the easiest move would be to combine the two sentences ("Both belonged to the *lex orandi* of the mining-town Lutheran community in 1585, and both established, in their distinct ways, the corresponding *lex credendi*") — but the current pair is rhetorically stronger.
