## Argument in one paragraph

The standard Lutheranisation account of the Slovak Lutheran *Cithara Sanctorum* (1636) treats confessional sharpening as something Tranovský performed on Czech-Reformation hymnody at the moment of cross-confessional transfer. This paper documents a complementary phenomenon at smaller scale: the Bratislava Lyceal Library exemplar of *Agenda Česká* 1581 + *Bystrická agenda* 1585, preserved bound together as a single physical codex, witnesses an intra-codex confessional stratification entirely *within the Lutheran orbit*. The printed Czech *Agenda Česká* uses a moderate Eucharistic register — "tělo a krev" without the "pravý" or "substanciálny" qualifiers — and grounds worthy reception in faith in the words of institution. The manuscript Slovak supplement, written four years later, sharpens the same theology with explicit Lutheran *manducatio impiorum*: the unworthy receive the body and blood "ne k spasenij, ale k zatraczenij wěčnemu". The intensification operates not at the cross-confessional boundary but within the Lutheran orbit itself, from a moderate Czech printed register with Utraquist liturgical residue to a sharper Hungarian-Lutheran manuscript register. The implication for the Lutheranisation thesis is that confessional sharpening is a phenomenon of register shift observable at the granularity of a single physical codex, not exclusively a phenomenon of cross-confessional transfer.

## Structure (with target word counts)

### 1. Introduction (~600 words)

Open with the standard view: Tranovský's *Cithara Sanctorum* as receptor of Czech-Reformation hymnody, with a confessional sharpening at the moment of compilation. Cite Vajda 1944 and Grešová-Sekelská 2021 / 2023 as the canonical articulations. State that recent work (Grešová-Sekelská 2021 quantifying source mix at 49 % Utraquist; Benka 2024 publishing the first critical edition of the Agenda Česká + Bystrická agenda) has refined this picture but kept the focus on cross-confessional transfer.

Pivot: this paper offers a complementary finding at smaller scale. The Bratislava Lyceal Library exemplar of AC 1581 + BA 1585 — preserved bound together as one codex — exhibits a confessional stratification *internal to the Lutheran orbit*. The paper's claim: the AC→BA shift is itself a Lutheranisation, occurring not across confessional lines but within them.

State methodology briefly: close textual reading of both witnesses in Benka's critical edition, with theological comparison to FC SD VII (1577) as the contemporaneous Lutheran normative benchmark.

End the introduction with a roadmap: §2 introduces the codex and the editorial situation; §§3–4 read the two Eucharistic rites; §5 reads the stratification theologically; §6 situates the finding within the broader Lutheranisation thesis.

### 2. The codex and its scholarly framing (~900 words)

The Bratislava Lyceal Library exemplar (administered by the Central Library of the Slovak Academy of Sciences) preserves both documents bound together. Benka 2024 (*Monumenta Linguae Slovacae* X) is the first critical edition: the AC 1581 occupies pp. 107–264 of the edition; the BA 1585 occupies pp. 265–368.

Provenance and historical context (drawing on Benka 2024 pp. 10–90):
- The mining-town Reformation in Banská Bystrica and surrounding areas — gradual, pragmatic, with the 1558 synod and subsequent agreements pursuing ceremonial uniformity while preserving "Christian freedom" in adiaphora.
- The 1570s–80s anti-crypto-Calvinist policing intensifying. Georg Creutzer's handbook for identifying hidden Calvinists listed denial of *manducatio impiorum* as a suspicion marker.
- The AC's preface (pp. 108–112 in Benka 2024 ed.) explicitly declares alignment with the Augsburg Confession and the three ecumenical creeds; names its sources as Wittenberg, Brandenburg and Württemberg agendas. Both witnesses are therefore confessionally Lutheran-aligned at the institutional level.

Why two witnesses bound together matters: the binding records what the early users took to belong together. A printed Czech agenda compiled from German Lutheran sources (Wittenberg / Brandenburg / Württemberg), translated into Czech for clergy ordained in Wittenberg but ministering to a Czech-speaking population in mining towns; supplemented four years later by a manuscript in Slovak addressing local needs. The codex thus encodes a transition, geographically and linguistically — from Wittenberg via Czech translation to a Slovak supplement — that has not been read as theologically significant *within* the Lutheran orbit.

Editorial situation: where the Bratislava exemplar is defective (missing leaves of the Czech and Latin prefaces), Benka reconstructs from other surviving copies and flags this in the apparatus. The Mass rite (AC 1581, ed. pp. 131–150) and the confession/Eucharistic preparation sections of the BA (pp. 265–302) are both fully preserved.

### 3. The printed Czech register: Agenda Česká 1581 (~1,500 words)

Read the AC's Eucharistic theology in detail.

**Verbatim quotation 1** (Benka 2024, p. 79, AC text):
> *Kdo giž z toho Chléba gij a z Kalicha pige a těm slowům, kteráž od Krysta slyssij a tomu nařízenij, kteréž od Krysta přigijmá, sylně wěřij, tenť gest hodný Host k stolu Páně.*

[Translate: Whoever now eats of this Bread and drinks from the Chalice and firmly believes those words which he hears from Christ and that ordinance which he receives from Christ — he is a worthy guest at the Lord's table.]

Theological reading:
- The criterion for worthy reception is **faith in the words of institution**. Not the *mode* of presence; not the bodily reality apart from faith; not the universal reach of the body across worthy and unworthy alike. Faith in the words is the operative concept.
- This is recognisably Lutheran in its anchor (the words of institution are decisive) but does not specify what FC SD VII would specify.

**Verbatim quotation 2** (Benka 2024, p. 79, Benka's analytical observation about the AC):
> *Príslušné pasáže v nej [Agende českej] obsahujú napríklad zmienky o tele a krvi, bez prívlastkov typu 'pravý' či 'substanciálny'.*

[Translate: The relevant passages in it [the Agenda Česká] contain, for example, mentions of body and blood, without qualifiers of the type 'true' or 'substantial'.]

This is Benka's own analysis: the AC's Eucharistic vocabulary lacks the qualifiers ("pravý" = true; "substanciálny" = substantial) that characterised polemically sharpened Lutheran anti-Reformed discourse.

**What the AC does NOT contain** (this needs to be argued, not just asserted):
- No explicit *manducatio impiorum* statement (the strong claim that the unworthy *truly receive* Christ's body to their judgment).
- No "in/with/under" (in pane, sub pane, cum pane) language.
- No anti-Calvinist polemical markers (no explicit rejection of "spiritual eating only", no insistence on "true and substantial" as polemical correctives).
- No *ubiquitas* / *communicatio idiomatum* technical apparatus.

This absence is not a doctrinal weakness — it is a register choice. The AC operates in what we can call a **moderate Czech-Lutheran register**: theologically anchored in the words of institution and the AC's confessional alignment, but pragmatically silent on the polemically sharpened distinctives that the contemporary Formula of Concord (1577) had just made the test cases for "real" versus "subtle Sacramentarian" Lutheranism.

Locate this register: it is the register of a Czech translation of German Lutheran agendas for clergy ministering to a population that included many former Utraquists, in a polity (the Habsburg / Hungarian frontier) where ceremonial diversity had practical reasons to be tolerated. The register does what registers do: it absorbs theological substance into pastoral practicability without renouncing the substance.

### 4. The manuscript Slovak supplement: Bystrická agenda 1585 (~1,500 words)

Read the BA's Eucharistic theology in detail.

**Verbatim quotation 3** (Benka 2024, pp. 78–79, BA text on worthy / unworthy reception):
> The communicants receive *Tělo a Krew Syna Božijho*; the worthy receive it *k spasenij*, the unworthy receive it *ne k spasenij, ale k zatraczenij wěčnemu*.

[Translate: The communicants receive the Body and Blood of the Son of God; the worthy receive it for salvation, the unworthy receive it not for salvation but for eternal damnation.]

This is the **explicit Lutheran *manducatio impiorum* formula**. Both worthy and unworthy receive the *same body and blood*; what differs is the salvific outcome. This is precisely the formulation that FC SD VII makes the boundary marker between Lutheran and Reformed Eucharistic theology (FC SD VII §2: "received with the mouth by all those who use this Sacrament, whether they be worthy or unworthy ... by the believing for consolation and life, by the unbelieving for judgment").

**What the BA introduces that the AC lacked:**
- Explicit *manducatio impiorum*: bodily reception by the unworthy, to their condemnation.
- A more polemically sharpened register: the language now does the work that the Czech printed register left unspecified.
- A liturgical-theological alignment with the FC SD VII boundary markers.

Provide Benka's own analytical framing (Benka 2024, pp. 78–79): the BA contains "*formulácie ktoré explicitnejšie smerujú k luteránskemu manducatio impiorum*" (formulations that more explicitly point toward Lutheran *manducatio impiorum*).

Theological reading: the BA represents not a *change* in confessional commitment (both witnesses are Lutheran-aligned) but a **register intensification**. The same theology is being said with sharper edges. The four years between AC's printing (1581) and BA's manuscript composition (1585) include the period in which the Formula of Concord (1577) was disseminating across the Lutheran territories; the BA's sharper register is plausibly responsive to that broader Lutheran confessional consolidation, even at the level of a manuscript supplement to a Czech printed agenda in a Hungarian mining town.

The register shift is also linguistic and material: from Czech print to Slovak manuscript. The shift in register accompanies the shift in language and medium. The print-vs-manuscript distinction is not incidental — manuscript supplementation allowed local adaptation that print constrained.

Confession-and-Eucharist linkage in the BA: private confession is foregrounded as preparation for communion. Benka 2024 pp. 73–77 documents the linkage in the 1558 synod, the 1580 regulations, and the BA itself. The double confessional function: distinguishing Lutheran practice from both Catholic obligatory confession and Reformed elimination of private confession. The BA encodes this double boundary explicitly; the AC encodes it less explicitly.

### 5. Reading the stratification: confessional sharpening within Lutheranism (~1,000 words)

Synthesise the AC→BA shift as a case of intra-Lutheran register intensification.

The standard Lutheranisation thesis (e.g., as articulated by Grešová-Sekelská 2021 and 2023; Vajda 1944) treats confessional sharpening as the differential editorial work Tranovský performed on cross-confessional source material — selecting, modifying, removing material from Brethren and Utraquist sources to align with Augsburg Lutheran norms. This is **cross-confessional Lutheranisation**.

The AC→BA stratification within the Bratislava codex demonstrates a complementary phenomenon: **intra-confessional Lutheranisation**. Both witnesses are Lutheran. The AC's preface declares Augsburg alignment. Yet the BA represents a register shift toward the polemically sharper end of contemporary Lutheran discourse. The same confessional commitment intensifies its textual expression as it crosses the boundary from print to manuscript, from Czech to Slovak, from a 1581 register to a 1585 register, from a translation of German agendas to a local supplement.

What does this add to the Lutheranisation thesis?

(a) **Lutheranisation is not exclusively a cross-confessional event.** It also operates as an internal sharpening within the Lutheran orbit. The CS's confessional sharpening of cross-confessional sources (Brethren / Utraquist hymns → Lutheran CS) sits in continuity with intra-Lutheran sharpening (moderate Czech AC → sharper BA). Both are register shifts within a broader confessional consolidation.

(b) **The granularity matters.** A single physical codex preserves both registers. This is not the kind of finding visible at the level of grand confessional narratives or even at the level of individual texts read in isolation. It is visible only when the textual witnesses are read against each other in their physical co-presence — which is exactly what the Bratislava codex preserves and what Benka's critical edition reveals.

(c) **Register, not doctrine, is the variable.** Both witnesses affirm the real presence; both are Lutheran-aligned. What differs is the polemical sharpness of expression and the explicitness of *manducatio impiorum*. Treating this as "the same theology in different registers" is more accurate than treating it as "two different theologies"; treating it as "no theological difference at all" misses the confessional work that register intensification does.

(d) **The *lex orandi, lex credendi* lens earns its keep.** The normative documents of 16th-century Lutheranism (CA, FC) say what they say; the liturgical witnesses show how that confession operated in mining-town pastoral practice. The AC→BA shift is invisible in the normative documents — they are univocally Lutheran. It is visible only in the lived theology of the codex.

### 6. Implications for the Lutheranisation thesis (~1,000 words)

(i) **The CB 1575 register at the cross-confessional level (~400 words).** A separate but related finding from this research programme indicates that the Confessio Bohemica 1575 itself shows a sophisticated bridging register at the cross-confessional level — one that the older Britannica characterisation as "Calvinist" misreads. The CB's formula *duchovně i podstatně, věrou i také ústy* (spiritually and substantially, by faith and also by mouth) explicitly affirms substantial presence and oral reception (the Lutheran distinctives) while also affirming spiritual reception by faith (the Reformed emphasis). This is a deliberate both/and irenic register, not a Calvinist position; the formulation could satisfy Lutherans (substantial, oral) while also reassuring Reformed sensibilities (spiritual, by faith). On the other hand, the CB 1575 remains conspicuously silent on the *manducatio impiorum* — the very test FC SD VII makes the Lutheran boundary marker. The silence is the same silence the AC 1581 keeps four years later within a Lutheran-aligned codex; the BA 1585 supplement is then the document that breaks that silence locally. A fuller treatment of the CB 1575's register and its consequences for understanding the Czech-Reformation confessional landscape is reserved for the forthcoming TEPALIT chapter; the brief mention here registers the parallel pattern at a different scale and offers a suggestive observation. The Britannica entry's characterisation has shaped a generation of anglophone reference work on the Bohemian Confession; the Czech text does not support it. (Footnote pointing to forthcoming chapter; a more detailed lexical defence is to appear there.)

(ii) **The Cithara Sanctorum's editorial method, re-read.** Tranovský's differential editorial strategy — hands-off in confessionally neutral rubrics, heavy intervention in Eucharistic / Marian / ecclesiological sections (per Grešová-Sekelská 2021) — fits naturally within the broader pattern this paper documents. Confessional sharpening is the action a Lutheran editor performs when crossing between registers, whether the crossing is from Brethren source to Lutheran CS or from Czech printed AC to Slovak manuscript BA. The action is the same; only the scale differs.

(iii) **The PAIR_006 record.** The finding documented here is recorded in the open *TEPALIT Digital Companion* dataset (Zenodo concept DOI `10.5281/zenodo.20081435`, CC BY 4.0) as `PAIR_006_AC_BA_EUCHARIST` under a three-axis epistemic-transparency framework. The record's trust signature is FACTUAL / DOCUMENTED / VERIFIED — the strongest available combination — with verification dossier (`Verification_PAIR_006.md`) recording the warrant. Mention this briefly: it gives readers a path to the underlying data, and it documents the open-scholarship infrastructure within which this finding was developed.

(iv) **What further work would test or extend this finding.** Two directions: (a) examination of additional bound-codex situations in Hungarian-Lutheran archives (Bardejov, Levoča, Kežmarok) for similar intra-codex stratifications; (b) systematic textual comparison of AC and BA outside the Eucharistic rite (baptism, marriage, funeral) to see whether the intra-codex register shift is Eucharist-specific or more general. The second is straightforwardly possible from Benka 2024 alone; the first requires archival work.

### 7. Conclusion (~300 words)

Synthesise without repeating.

The Bratislava Lyceal Library exemplar preserves a confessional stratification that the standard cross-confessional account of Lutheranisation does not predict and that the normative confessional documents of the period do not record. Confessional sharpening can operate within a confession's own orbit, register intensifying as the textual medium shifts (print → manuscript), as the language shifts (Czech → Slovak), and as the temporal context shifts (1581 → 1585). The codex records this stratification because its early users bound the two witnesses together as one liturgical object. Reading the binding as theologically significant, rather than as merely codicological happenstance, reveals a *lex orandi* witness to confessional consolidation that the *lex credendi* of either witness alone could not have shown.

End with a brief gesture toward the broader programme: this paper offers one finding within a research project (TEPALIT, APVV-24-0484) that examines the theological paradigm of liturgical and hymnological sources in Central European Protestantism. The fuller treatment of the Cithara Sanctorum's transfer and reception, and of the Confessio Bohemica's irenic register, awaits the project's monograph chapter.
