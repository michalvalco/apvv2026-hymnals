## Introduction

This reconnaissance, conducted under the constraints of the working bibliography supplied by the chapter author for APVV-24-0484 ("TEPALIT"), surfaces approximately three dozen items of peer-reviewed scholarship — most published after 2010 — that bear directly on the four loci theologici and three cross-cutting questions defined in the brief. Several patterns deserve flagging at the outset.

First, **anglophone scholarship on the Confessio Bohemica has decisively moved past the older "Calvinist Eucharist" reading**: the Just–Rothkegel 2012 critical edition (in *Reformierte Bekenntnisschriften* 3/1) and Phillip Haberkern's 2023 article in *Reformation & Renaissance Review* now constitute the new anglophone/German-language consensus on the irenic, both-and character of the document. Nevertheless the older Calvinist gloss persists in tertiary reference works — most prominently the current online *Encyclopaedia Britannica* entry on the "Bohemian Confession" and several derivative encyclopaedia ecosystems (encyclopedia.com, universalium, blogspot mirrors). Because *Britannica* remains a citation surface for AI-generated synthesis material, this is the precise point at which the older characterisation continues to propagate. No post-2010 *peer-reviewed* venue defending the Calvinist reading was located.

Second, **Slovak-language hymnological scholarship on the Cithara Sanctorum has clustered tightly around two figures** (Adriana Grešová-Sekelská and, earlier, Eva Augustínová), with a parallel Czech–Hungarian musicological output. Importantly, a third figure — **Peter Ruščin** of the Institute of Musicology of the Slovak Academy of Sciences — is the most active recent contributor in the journal *Musicologica Slovaca*. Pre-Grešová Slovak work (Vajdička, Kovačka, Bartko) is largely descriptive-bibliographical rather than text-critical or comparative, and almost wholly unknown outside the region.

Third, **Hungarian-language scholarship on the Confessio Pentapolitana (especially Csepregi)** is significantly more developed than its Slovak-language counterpart but is published in venues invisible to anglophone Reformation studies (*Lelkipásztor*, REAL repository of the Hungarian Academy of Sciences, university press of Prešov).

Fourth, **the confessionalisation paradigm has been extended into liturgical/ritual domains** by Karant-Nunn, Frandsen, and the Headley–Hillerbrand–Papalas Nischan-Festschrift cluster, and into hymnological domains principally by Robin Leaver and the Joseph Herl line; but this work is overwhelmingly focused on Saxon/Brandenburg/Dresden Lutheranism, leaving a recognisable gap for Upper-Hungarian and Czech/Brethren material — exactly the gap the TEPALIT chapter is positioned to fill.

The seven sections that follow are organised by locus and cross-cutting question. Within each, I distinguish *high-confidence citations* (verified to specific volumes, DOIs, or repository pages) from *probable but unverified items* flagged for follow-up. Pages, ISBNs, and author attributions have all been re-checked in the audit pass; where a detail (page range, ISBN) could not be independently verified, that fact is now marked rather than papered over.

## Section 2 — Ecclesiology, Ministry, Ordination, Apostolic Succession

### 2.1 Brethren ecclesiology and the Lhotka 1467 succession

**Craig D. Atwood, *The Theology of the Czech Brethren from Hus to Comenius*** (see §1.2 above). The chapters on Brethren orders, the 1467 consecrations, and the ongoing reconfiguration through Lukáš are the standard anglophone treatment. Atwood explicitly notes that any attempt by the Brethren to claim apostolic succession as traditionally understood rests on a faulty reading of Waldensian history — directly relevant to the chapter's locus 2 sub-theme on Brethren succession.

**Claudia Mai, "Tracing the Footsteps of the Fathers: The History of the *Acta Unitatis Fratrum*"** — *Journal of Moravian History* 15/1 (2015), pp. 29–43. DOI 10.5325/jmorahist.15.1.0029. Language: English. *Argument*: the Lissa Folios (now in the Czech State Archives) are the indispensable archival corpus for any reconstruction of Brethren self-understanding of orders, succession, and discipline; Mai traces the historiographical use of these volumes from the 1840 Moravian purchase forward. *Why it matters*: archival-orientation paper that lets the chapter author point readers to where claims about Brethren ordination and succession can actually be tested.

**Probable existence of recent Czech-language scholarship on the Lhotka 1467 events** by Jiří Just (Centre for the Study of the Bible and Antiquity, Prague), whose Confessio Bohemica edition work would naturally extend backward to Brethren order origins; suggest follow-up search at the publications page of the Centre and at academia.edu. **Atwood 2009** (see §1.2) is the standard treatment; chapters on Brethren ethics explicitly address the relationship to the Lutheran *sola fide*. Atwood reads the Brethren as anticipating elements of the Radical Reformation while remaining inside the magisterial orbit through their 1535 confession.

**Brent Ranalli, "Unity of Brethren Tradition and Comenius's Pansophy"** — *Journal of Moravian History* 20/1 (May 2020), pp. 1–29. DOI 10.5325/jmorahist.20.1.0001. ## Section 5 — Confessio Bohemica 1575: Historiographical State of the Question

### 5.1 The current consensus: irenic / both-and reading

**Phillip N. Haberkern, "The Bohemian Confession of 1575: Towards an Archaeology of the Czech Reformation"** — *Reformation & Renaissance Review* 25/2–3 (2023), beginning at p. 136. DOI 10.1080/14622459.2023.2283009. Language: English. *Argument*: reads the CB as the product of multi-party negotiation among Utraquists, Lutherans, and Brethren whose Eucharistic article is **deliberately irenic** — explicitly rejecting the older characterisation of the document as crypto-Calvinist. Haberkern engages and extends Just/Rothkegel 2012, citing it as the new standard. ### Pattern analysis (for the project methodological record)

Two failure patterns explain most of the original errors:

1. **Author confusion within tightly bibliographically-linked clusters.** The Atwood/Crews swap on Luke of Prague and the Grešová-Sekelská/Ruščin swap on Cithara editions both happened because the wrong author from the same scholarly conversation got attached to a verified article. This is the more dangerous failure pattern because the resulting citation looks plausible to anyone not already deeply embedded in the local literature.

2. **Page-range and ISBN fabrication.** When the AI had high confidence about an article's existence, it sometimes filled in plausible-looking but actually unverified details. This is the failure pattern Michal's standing instruction specifically anticipates: "All AI-generated research reports or literature summaries require full citation verification before being used in any academic output."

The implication for the TEPALIT workflow is straightforward: **no citation surfaced by AI-generated reconnaissance should be entered into the project's source notes (`Sources/*.md`) without an independent verification step against the publisher catalogue, journal index, DOI, or repository entry.** This audit confirms that the methodology the project already uses (one verified `Source/*.md` file per cited work, written only after the source has been actually consulted or its bibliographic detail confirmed against authoritative metadata) is the right one. The error was in the prior reconnaissance step, not in the source-notes pipeline.
