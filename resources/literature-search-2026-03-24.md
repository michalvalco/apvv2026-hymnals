## SYNTHESIS: Challenges to the Thesis

Several findings from this search potentially complicate or challenge the working thesis ("systematic theological adaptation — a 'Lutheranisation'"). These require careful engagement:

### 1. The Irenic Counter-Narrative (Haberkern, David)
Haberkern (2023) and David (1999) both argue that the Bohemian Reformation was characterised by pragmatic toleration and theological common ground, **not** rigid confessional boundaries. If the Confessio Bohemica of 1575 was a genuine compromise rather than a Lutheran takeover, the "Lutheranisation" thesis needs nuance. **Our response:** The CB is a normative document; the *lex orandi* lens may reveal tensions that the document papered over. The hymnal transfer (CS) happened *after* the CB and in a different context (Upper Hungary, not Bohemia proper). Geographic and temporal specificity matters.

### 2. Liturgical Persistence (Graham, Holeton)
Graham (2006) finds no Lutheran influence on the Utraquist Mass even into the early 17th century. Holeton's work shows Utraquist liturgy evolved gradually without programmatic reform. **Our response:** This actually *supports* our argument — if liturgical practice remained distinct even where confessional documents converged, then Tranovský's editorial work on hymns was performing confessional boundary-work that the normative documents alone did not accomplish. The *lex orandi* lens reveals what the *lex credendi* concealed.

### 3. Atwood's "Long Bows" (Harmes review)
Atwood's sympathetic reading of the Brethren may understate the distance between Brethren and Lutheran soteriology. **Our response:** Read Atwood critically on soteriology and cross-reference with the Confessio Bohemica and Brethren confessional documents directly.

### 4. Schoen's Dissertation (2022)
If Schoen's comprehensive analysis of Utraquist confessionalisation is available, it may reshape the field before our chapter appears. **Action:** Obtain and read.
