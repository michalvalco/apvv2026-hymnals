## Core Theses

### 1. From Ritual Deviation to Konfessionskirche
Schoen argues that Utraquism begins as a ritually-liturgically deviant but theologically and juridically recognized variant within Latin Christendom, based on the Basel Compacts (1433/36). It only becomes a distinct *Konfessionskirche* over the course of the 16th century. This undercuts older narratives that project a fully distinct "Hussite confession" into the 15th century and pushes proper confessionalization into the Reformation era.

**Implication for our chapter:** When we speak of "Utraquist theology" in our comparative analysis, we must be precise about which *phase* of Utraquism we mean. Pre-1540s Utraquism is not the same confessional animal as post-1575 Utraquism.

### 2. Two-Phase Konfessionsbildung Model
Schoen reconstructs a multi-phase process:
- **Vorphase** (1520s): Initial engagement with Wittenberg, Lichtmessartikel 1524
- **Phase I** (early 1540s–1562): Culminating in the *Antiqua et constans Confessio fidei* (1562)
- **Phase II** (1560s–1609): Confessio Bohemica (1575) as the central identity document; 1609 Majestätsbrief as formal closure

This yields a finer periodisation than the usual pre-/post-1575 or pre-/post-1609 schemes.

**Implication:** Tranovský's CS (1636) postdates the *entire* confessionalisation process. By the time he compiles CS, the Utraquist Konfessionskirche has been fully formed, suppressed at White Mountain (1620), and is effectively extinct as an institutional entity. What survives is the *hymn repertoire* — now decontextualised from its confessional home.

### 3. Interplay with Wittenberg — Not Simple Dependence
Utraquist identity is shaped *in interaction with* Wittenberg rather than in simple dependence on it. Luther's calls for a break with Rome, the reception *and selective rejection* of his theology (e.g., the 1524 Lichtmessartikel), and Melanchthonian mediation lines (Pressius) function as catalysts that push Utraquists toward explicit doctrinal boundary-drawing — but on their own terms.

**Implication:** This reframes "Lutheranisation" as a more complex dynamic than simple absorption. Utraquists were not passive recipients; they selectively engaged Wittenberg theology while maintaining distinctives. When Tranovský later incorporates Utraquist-origin hymns into CS, he is not simply "claiming" passive material — he is appropriating material that had its own active confessional identity.

### 4. Against the Verfallsthese
Schoen explicitly targets the long-dominant *Verfallsthese*, which saw Utraquism after legal recognition as moral and theological degeneration saved only by Lutheranism. On her reading, 16th-century Utraquism is neither a hollow shell nor "crypto-Lutheran"; it is a dynamic, internally contested tradition that develops its own confessional profile, distinct from both Roman Catholics and the Unitas Fratrum.

**Implication:** We cannot treat Utraquist hymn material as theologically inert or doctrinally vague. These hymns come from a tradition that was actively constructing its own confessional identity. The theological content of the hymns matters — and the *changes* Tranovský makes to them are changes to texts with confessional weight.

### 5. Adiaphorism and Legal Status as Structuring Factors
In Schoen's synthesis, adiaphorism and the peculiar legal status of Utraquists (still claiming membership in the *Catholica* while being "tolerated schismatics") explain why doctrinally significant shifts could proceed under a rhetoric of continuity and *Nationalkirche*. The "publicly visible break with Rome" (1567) and the events of 1575/1609 are then read as the late crystallisation of much longer inner-Utraquist reconfigurations.

**Implication:** The concept of adiaphora is directly relevant to our *lex orandi* methodology. If Utraquists treated certain liturgical and hymnological practices as *adiaphora*, while Lutherans did not (or vice versa), this creates a space where hymn adaptation reveals confessional commitments that formal theology leaves ambiguous.

## Historiographical Repositioning

### Schoen's Ch. I: Anatomy of Czech and German Scholarship

Schoen shows how nationalist, Catholic, Marxist, and confessional agendas shaped two dominant historiographical lines:

**a) Continuity line** (Palacký → Masaryk): Hussitism as early "Reformation," Utraquism as early "confession." Schoen insists this is anachronistic — Basel's recognition shows 15th-c. Utraquism was treated as a deviant but still Catholic rite, not a separate Konfessionskirche.

**b) Verfall line** (Tomek, Winter, Denis, later Kaiserová): Utraquism degenerates until rescued by Lutheranism. Schoen rehabilitates 16th-c. Utraquist elites and theologians as active agents of reform, not simply "Bohemian Lutherans" in disguise.

This also indirectly balances the long historiographical privileging of the Unitas Fratrum (Gindely, Molnár) over Utraquism.

## Where This Leaves Key Interlocutors

### Zdeněk V. David — Partially Displaced
David's *Finding the Middle Way* depicts Utraquism as a liberal, irenic "third way" resisting hard confessionalisation. Schoen's phased model suggests what David treats as enduring "middle way" may be a *transitional configuration*, especially by the 1540s–1570s. David's emphasis on moderation and anti-dogmatism fits parts of Schoen's narrative (early 16th-c. adiaphorism, integrative projects), but Schoen's detailed reconstruction of inner-Utraquist conflict — "truth against truth" dynamics, explicit boundary-drawing (Causa Gelasti, Antiqua et constans Confessio fidei, Confessio Bohemica) — challenges any claim that Utraquism *as a whole* remained structurally anti-confessional.

**Operational consequence:** David becomes a history of *one strand* (the long survival of irenic, legalistic, middle-position Utraquism) rather than a global account of Utraquist identity. Use David for mapping moderate currents, but mark that Schoen shows a more decisive move into full confessionalisation than David's "middle way" suggests.

### Phillip Haberkern — Complementary, Different Scale
Haberkern's 2023 article approaches the Bohemian Confession as a key moment in Czech Reformation "archaeology." Schoen's Chapter IV supplies the *longue durée* backstory: CB as the "Identitätsdokument der Konfessionsutraquisten," emerging from decades of internal debates, failed integrative attempts, and growing exclusivist truth-claims.

**Operational consequence:** Haberkern = micro-focused on the confession and its immediate context. Schoen = macro-focused on the process that produced the milieu for that confession. Both converge on the centrality of 1575 but with different temporal and methodological horizons. Together, they allow us to insist that 1575 is not a sudden innovation or purely political compromise but the apex of long, internally driven Utraquist Konfessionsbildung.

### Older Czech Historiography — Reframed
More recent Czech-language work that still leans on "hidden Lutheranism" or treats Utraquism mainly as a vehicle of Lutheran theology appears, in Schoen's framing, as insufficiently attentive to Utraquist self-descriptions, legal strategies, and sustained claims to a special status in the *Catholica*.

## Deployment in Our Chapter

### For §2 (Konfesionálny kontext)
Schoen provides the historiographical scaffolding. Structure the field review:
1. Palacký → early "Hussite-Reformation continuity" model
2. Verfallsthese line (Tomek, Winter, Denis) → Utraquism as decayed, rescued by Lutheranism
3. David → Utraquism as long-lasting liberal middle way
4. Haberkern → Bohemian Confession as key textual moment
5. **Schoen → confessionalisation-based reconstruction that historicises and partially displaces all of the above**

### For §3 (Dogmatické profily) — Utraquist Profile
Schoen gives us:
- **Conceptual grid:** Vorphase → Phase I → Phase II → 1609 closure. Use this to periodise Utraquist theological development precisely.
- **Terminological precision:** Reserve *Konfession* and *Konfessionsbildung* for post-Reformation dynamics, while acknowledging Hussite continuities.
- **A corrective:** Utraquism is neither proto-Reformed nor proto-Lutheran; it has a distinct confessional profile under strong Wittenberg influence but retaining its own agency.

### For §4 (Komparatívna analýza) — Eucharistic Locus
The Eucharist (*sub utraque specie*) is the founding marker of Utraquist identity. Schoen's argument that adiaphorism structured how Utraquists processed doctrinally significant shifts is directly relevant to our Eucharistic comparison. The question: when a Utraquist Eucharistic hymn enters CS, what happens to its sacramental theology? Does Tranovský alter the Eucharistic language? Does he select only those hymns already compatible with FC Article VII?

### For §5 (Transfer a recepcia)
The key insight from Schoen for our reception-history argument: **by 1636, Utraquism is institutionally dead (post-White Mountain), but its hymn repertoire lives on as cultural memory.** Tranovský's editorial work on Utraquist-origin hymns is not a contemporary inter-confessional dialogue — it is a post-mortem appropriation. The hymns have been orphaned from their Konfessionskirche. This changes the dynamics of "Lutheranisation": it is not negotiation between living traditions but a dominant tradition selectively inheriting from a defeated one.

## Critical Questions for Our Chapter

1. **Does Schoen address hymnody at all?** Her focus appears to be confessional documents, legal instruments, and theological polemics. If she does not discuss hymns or liturgical music, there is a gap we fill — the *lex orandi* dimension of the very confessionalisation process she reconstructs.

2. **Does she discuss the relationship between Utraquism and the Unitas Fratrum in detail?** The three-way comparison (Lutheran / Utraquist / Brethren) is our chapter's distinctive contribution. If Schoen treats the Brethren only as a foil for Utraquist identity, we need to supplement with Atwood.

3. **Does she engage with liturgical sources?** Holeton's work on Utraquist liturgy is referenced in all the relevant scholarship. If Schoen integrates or supersedes Holeton, we need to know.

4. **What is her position on Utraquist Eucharistic theology specifically?** The Compactata guaranteed communion *sub utraque* — but what about real presence, the sacrifice of the Mass, the relationship between Eucharistic practice and Eucharistic doctrine?
