## Summary of Argument

The article traces the presence and gradual disappearance of hymns about saints in Slovak Lutheran hymnody, with CS (1636) as the centrepiece. Grešová shows that the cult of saints was not simply absent from early Lutheran worship — it was actively present in 16th–17th c. hymnals and faded only over subsequent centuries.

### Key Findings

1. **Theological framework (§2):** CA Art. XXI ("De Cultu Sanctorum") is conciliatory — it affirms remembering saints as examples of faith and good works, but rejects invocation. Melanchthon's Apology elaborates: saints deserve threefold honour (thanksgiving for God's mercy, strengthening faith, imitating their faith). The Upper Hungarian confessions (Pentapolitana 1549, Montana 1559, Scepusiana 1568) echo this. The Unity of Brethren (Bishop Lukáš Pražský) similarly rejects external veneration of saints as idolatry but allows commemorative celebration.

2. **Comparative hymnal analysis (§3):** Four hymnals compared:
   - **Kunvaldský kancionál (1576):** 60+ hymns on OT/NT figures and some non-biblical saints, in sections "O Služebnijcych Božijch" etc.
   - **Závorkov kancionál (1602):** Full standalone section "O Swatých Božijch" — includes Mary, apostles, evangelists, martyrs, confessors, AND non-biblical saints (Nicholas, Lawrence, Wenceslaus, Martin, Catherine). Even includes hymns for the Assumption of Mary (reinterpreted as commemorating Mary's death, not bodily assumption).
   - **Anonymous collection ed. Daniel Karel z Karlšperka (1620):** Fewer saint-themed hymns; more general formulations; shift away from individual saint focus. Includes angels, apostles, Czech martyrs.
   - **Cithara Sanctorum (1636):** Continues the trend of 1620 — fewer hymns, more general. Three sections: angels, Mary, apostles/martyrs.

3. **CS 1636 — specifics (§4):**
   - **Title:** Tranovský explains "Cithara Sanctorum" (Harp of the Saints) from Rev 5:8 — "saints" = the whole church, the community of believers. This is a *Lutheran* redefinition.
   - **Preface on saints:** The 1636 first edition contains a little-known passage (4 theses on veneration of saints), later dropped by editors. Tranovský rejects prayer to saints, affirms sole mediation of Christ, argues from *sola scriptura*.
   - **Angels (5 hymns):** Two by Tranovský, one his translation from German, one from Utraquist sources, one from Unity of Brethren. Didactic/narrative character.
   - **Mary (5 hymns):** Three by Tranovský, two from Závorkov kancionál. Christocentric — "SYN" (SON) printed in majuscule to redirect attention from Mary to Christ. Two versions of Magnificat preserved.
   - **Apostles and Martyrs (7 hymns):** Two by Tranovský, two from Závorkov kancionál, others from various sources. Generally formulated; exception: Tranovský's "Pamatůgmež na Swatých" with individual strophes for 16 saints.

4. **Source provenance of CS hymns on saints:**
   - Tranovský's own compositions
   - German Lutheran originals (translated by Tranovský)
   - **Utraquist sources** (at least 1 confirmed: "Činíme tobě Pane Bože díky" from 1584 Plzeň print of Valentín Polon)
   - **Unity of Brethren sources** (at least 1 confirmed: "Chváltež Pána anjelé" from Ján Roh's *Písně chval božských*, 1541)
   - Závorkov kancionál (itself drawing on older Czech sources)

5. **Later reception (§5):** Only 6 new saint-themed hymns added across all subsequent CS editions (1636–1741). The Hruškovic 1741 edition (final form, used until 1992) more than doubled the total hymn count but barely expanded the saints section. The Senický kancionál (1692, manuscript) — a partial copy of CS — shows selective reception: apostle hymns fully copied, Mary hymns mostly copied, angel hymns almost entirely ignored.

6. **Conclusion:** Luther himself predicted that once saints lost their intercessory function, they would be forgotten. The article confirms this trajectory across 16th–20th c. Lutheran hymnody, while demonstrating that the process was much slower than commonly assumed — saint-themed repertoire was "truly alive" in the 16th–17th centuries, and confessional differences in liturgical practice were less stark than modern perceptions suggest.

## Relevance for TEPALIT Chapter

### HIGH relevance for §4 (Comparative Analysis) and §5 (Transfer and Reception)

- **Demonstrates cross-confessional hymn transfer in CS:** Grešová identifies specific hymns from Utraquist and Brethren sources incorporated into CS 1636 — precisely the kind of evidence our chapter needs for the reception-history *locus*.
- **Shows Tranovský's editorial method:** His treatment of saint-themed material reveals a theological programme — Christocentric redirection (majuscule "SYN"), sola scriptura argumentation in the preface, selective inclusion of Czech-origin hymns.
- **Documents the "Lutheranisation" process concretely:** The gradual reduction and generalisation of saint-themed hymns from the rich Závorkov/Kunvaldský repertoire to the leaner CS 1636 is precisely the kind of theological filtering our thesis posits.
- **Provides comparative data:** The four-hymnal comparison is a model for what we need to do across all four *loci*, not just the saints theme.

### Points of caution
- Grešová's focus is hymnological, not dogmatic-comparative. She does not systematically analyse the *theological* content of the adaptations (what was changed, what doctrinal commitments drove the changes).
- The article does not engage with Eucharistic or ecclesiological hymn content — our core *loci*.
- **This is where our chapter adds value:** systematic dogmatic analysis of the transfers Grešová documents musicologically.
