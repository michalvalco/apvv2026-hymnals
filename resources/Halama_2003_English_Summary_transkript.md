## SUMMARY — The social teaching of the Czech Brethren

*[p. 289]*

The social teaching of the Czech Brethren is a subject that has not previously been dealt with as a whole. The monographs on this topic that do exist (of which only one is in Czech, and it is unpublished) cover only a certain period of the history of the Brethren, or just certain aspects of the subject.

One of the aims of this study is to establish whether the evidence about the way the Brethren approached the social dimension of human life represents a body of material that is sufficiently consistent and generally applicable for us to be justified in talking about a social teaching (something that earlier studies have cast doubt upon). A further aim is to demonstrate a continuity (which previous researchers have also questioned) in the social teaching of the Czech Brethren throughout the period of their existence, from the first document they issued setting out a programme (the Concord on the Rychnov Mountains, 1464) right up to the beginning of the Thirty Years' War.

The study examines social teaching, that is, primarily the official, mainly theological, justification for individual social emphases. It takes note of significant divergences, but does not attempt to depict the whole of the social practice of the Brethren. It endeavours to capture the main emphases and principles of the social teaching of the Brethren, and to describe and portray their social theory, its development over time, and its relationship to the way the Brethren saw the world, society and the church. The development of the teaching of the Brethren is divided into four periods: from the beginnings until the change in orientation after 1490, the era of Luke of Prague (up till 1528), the period in which they independently came to terms with the European Reformation (up till the Bohemian Confession in 1575), and the years leading towards the legalisation of the Unity and the brief period of freedom as a result of Rudolf II's Imperial Charter (up till 1618).

The study includes in an appendix the first critical edition of an important document relating to the social teaching of the Brethren: *An exhortation addressed to all the faithful etc.* The text has been prepared on the basis of the three known editions from the second half of the 16th century. One of them was recently discovered *[p. 290]* in the course of the research for this study when examining the previously known copies in the University Library in Wroclaw.

## THE CONTENT OF THE STUDY

### 1. The Church as an Alternative Society (up till 1490)

The social attitudes and principles of the first generation of the Unity were predominantly derived from the thinking of Petr Chelčický, and to a considerable extent it is true to say that the Brethren put his ideas into practice. From the beginning, the Brethren tried to give their community a rural, ascetic character. They considered the world to be intrinsically sinful, and separated themselves from it both internally and (by retiring into seclusion) externally as well. The basic attributes of true Christianity were for them living as a minority and suffering, caused by the hostility of the world to the witness of truth that is the law of Christ. The Brethren were convinced that it was possible to fulfil this law. By following and fulfilling it, it was possible to attain the way of righteousness, which they understood, not as a life of faith in the Reformation sense, but in the traditional medieval sense, as primarily a morally good life. The Brethren's pursuit of "righteousness surpassing that of the Pharisees" (Mat. 5:20) is an understandable reaction to the moral decline of the church, whether it communicated under one kind or under both. Its primary goal in the early period, however, was to become personally righteous as the precondition for being saved from this degenerate world.

From these positions emerged the rejection of power and violence in all forms, and the rejection of all institutions and structures that were linked with worldly domination and violence, from governments and armies, through magistrates and aldermen, to courts and executioners. Trades, which profited from this sinful system, were unacceptable, not to mention those who supported it. Even schools were unacceptable, for they prepared people for this way of life. Christians could feel compassion for this world in its decline into depravity, but genuine solidarity was only possible with those who acknowledged the sinfulness of the world and turned away from it. For this reason, care for the poor, sick, *[p. 291]* abandoned, and all other needy people, which the Unity promoted in its congregations from the beginning, was primarily directed inwardly, to those in need within their own community.

As a protest against a society that called itself Christian, while in fact it was based on unscrupulous oppression, violence and disregard for one's neighbour, the attempts of the first generation of the Unity to establish in practice a different kind of human community is worthy of respect. Nevertheless, as an alternative to the existing society (which is what the Brethren wanted to be), their model proved to be unworkable. When they tried to see whether it was possible in practice to live according to their original strict principles, the evidence began to mount up that this project was impracticable. All kinds of awkward compromises, the evasion of violence and of responsibility for public issues, and the unavoidable contact with the towns, trade, and higher education — all this persuaded the Brethren that their original ideal of simplicity and turning away from the world did not provide the basis for a viable and responsible Christian community. By the 1480s it was already becoming obvious that their relationship to the world, and most of the social ties arising from this relationship, would need to be thought out and assessed again.

### 2. From Radical Rejection to Critical Responsibility (1490–1528)

The entire later development of the Unity of Brethren was determined by the form of teaching and practice of the Brethren that took shape during the first quarter of the 16th century, largely thanks to Brother Luke. A decisive change took place in their relationship to society and its institutions. The Brethren abandoned their uncompromising rejection of all social structures and their attempts to establish a separate, alternative society. They decided to take on a share of the responsibility for the way human society was organised, although they had many reservations and adopted a stance of critical detachment. Their attitude to the instruments of power essential for organising human society continued to be one of caution, if not mistrust.

The first task for those in authority should be to uphold justice and to rectify injustices. But firstly this is far from always being the case, and secondly even in the best of cases authority is *[p. 292]* backed up by violence. The Brethren maintained a strong critical distance from authority and violence, conscious of the fact that these things were not a necessary and inseparable part of Christian life. They believed that coercive power and violence should not be a habitual, common instrument for the administration of human affairs. It was an extreme solution to be used in an emergency, and often had disastrous consequences. If there was no other alternative, the Brethren adopted the approach that was normal in society, but they remained convinced that it was necessary to try to find a better way of resolving society's problems. This applied particularly to service in the army, courts and punishments, and, related to this, oaths, as a special concept in human speech, a kind of "violent" statement.

The Brethren also remained critical of the social divisions of society. They no longer asserted that Christians cannot be subject to social stratification, and they respected the division of society, but they brought to it an emphasis on service and responsibility. While priests were in a position where they had to render a special service, even the clergy should so far as possible earn their living while serving others spiritually and materially. The nobility did not have to renounce their status, but they were called to be responsible for public affairs and to look after those subject to them. They were to use the possessions and talents that had been given them as benefactors, in other words, not just maintaining order, but caring for the needy and seeking ways of promoting the general good. Thus, without attempting to abolish the social structure of society, the Brethren worked towards a change in social conditions "from below" by winning over to their understanding of Christian life those who determined the rules by which society functioned. This was most obvious where they were able to directly influence the local nobility over a lengthy period.

The social dimension of the teaching of the Brethren was linked on the individual level with the requirement of exemplary Christian life, which was to be found almost everywhere. It was not in a radical separation and turning away from society, but in the midst of social life, that Christians should show what a life of faith looked like, and through their example should persuade those around them that it was a good life. Simplifying the matter to some extent, it can perhaps be said that while the position of the first generation of the Brethren was largely determined by obedience, now the principal feature became responsibility.

*[p. 293]* Living a life of critical solidarity and continually re-assessed responsibility in the light of the demands of the Gospel message is an exacting and difficult approach, as the Brethren discovered. The temptation to make it easier by lowering standards, so that Christians adapted to the way others lived, was now far greater than when they turned away from the world, because then the boundaries of the Christian position had been clearly marked out. Now the boundaries were not always so clear in advance, and it was possible to relinquish the position of witness discreetly, in seemingly unimportant matters, and even without anyone noticing.

It was therefore necessary to constantly struggle to improve and renew one's life in accordance with God's commandments, living morally and justly. This called for a renewal of personal morality and a life lived in relationship with others that was outstanding for its uprightness, service and consideration for the needs of one's neighbours. All of this, combined with an intensive community of faith, the performing of devotions at home, attending services of worship in church, and the fellowship of the believers, represented the guidelines of the Brethren for an authentic Christian existence in the world.

The question as to whether a fundamental continuity existed in the social teaching of the Unity through all these changes must be answered in the affirmative. It is true that the "social philosophy" represented by Gregory and later by the Lesser Party, in other words radical separatism and rejection of the world, soon disappeared from the direction taken by the Unity. Nevertheless, the theologically justified rights of all people, based on their essential equality before God, remained a fundamental article of the teaching of the Brethren and an important factor in the shaping of their social practice. The change in direction of the teaching of the Unity from radicalism and obedience towards responsibility and what might be termed "critical realism" is a return to some emphases in the tradition of the church that Chelčický and those of the Brethren who followed his lead had discarded. Among the patristic traditions this meant a return above all to Augustine, while among the currents of the Reformation the influence of Wyclif and Hus clearly asserted itself, not only with Luke of Prague, but also with Tůma Přeloučský. However, this change in its relationship to society did not mean that the Unity now reconciled itself to a place among the established social order. It did not call into question its separation from the mainstream church; on *[p. 294]* the contrary, it continued to work on the theoretical and theological justification for this separation, to which it was led by telling arguments of both a dogmatic and a practical nature. On the theological plane, the Brethren had a very different concept of the church and the sacraments, as can be seen from the work of Brother Luke, while practically their aim was for a church that had a completely independent status in society. Spiritual issues, matters of faith, conscience, and the personal convictions of the individual, were not allowed to be determined or controlled by temporal, coercive authority, but solely by spiritual authority, which used the instruments of discussion and persuasion and in the final analysis had to respect the freedom of each individual.

The radical stance of the first generation, evidently still influenced by the reverberations of the Taborite expectation of the end of time, had not only seen the existing social order in more relative terms but even rejected it, and fixed its attention on the utopia of a perfect human community, made reality by true Christians separated from the world. This now gave way to a more realistic approach, which however continued to follow the same goal, namely the renewal of the church and a Christian life that was not tied to the views and interests of power and politics, a renewal guided by the commandments of God's word, as the Brethren understood it from the Scriptures.

The Brethren thus now set themselves more modest goals that were to be achieved gradually, but on the other hand they expanded their theological horizons to include the whole of contemporary society. They wanted to influence that society through their example, work together with it on everything that they saw as desirable or at least acceptable, and thus take on a share of the responsibility for public affairs. All the changes that took place at this time in the teaching of the Brethren can be considered to be changes of method, the development of a new strategy, while preserving their original starting points and goals. Their relationship to the world, however, which had been one of the defining features of Christian life for the first generation, was now re-assessed and placed among issues of secondary importance, where a certain plurality of attitude was possible. But most of the basic characteristics of the Brethren's teaching remained valid, simply acquiring a new, usually more precise, interpretation. A clear continuity can be found between the teaching of the Unity at its beginning and the form taken by that teaching at the time of Luke.

### 3. Coming to Terms with the Influence of the European Reformation and the Struggle for Recognition by Society (1528–1575)

*[p. 295]* In the turbulent years of the rise of the European Reformation a number of changes can be noted in the teaching of the Brethren, some smaller, some greater, together with new emphases and also a return to the original position in cases where it transpired that the changes had not been thought through properly or were unnecessary. In terms of the relationship to temporal authority the most significant change was a positive evaluation of its importance, although this was accompanied by many conditions and reservations. During the time of Luke of Prague the Brethren had already come to recognise that temporal power does not have to be non-Christian or opposed to God, provided it is exercised in keeping with "surpassing righteousness" according to Christ's teaching. Now a further shift in emphasis took place, with acceptance of the fact that power, properly used, is not only not opposed to God but is a service entrusted to those in power by God, and this also included courts and punishment in the interest of maintaining earthly justice. The Brethren accepted the idea that even violence can be used pedagogically, and punishment inflicted by authority may have a remedial effect. They therefore reconciled themselves to the idea that there are certain situations which it is right to resolve using violence. This was confirmed by their attitude to war and military service, the belief that it is necessary to demonstrate responsibility for the local community and society as a whole in the joint defence of the common good. It is on this point, in the acceptance of the idea of the common good as a positive value that Christians, too, should have a share in asserting and defending, that the Brethren underwent their clearest shift in position from the exclusive understanding of the eschatological demands of the Kingdom of God to the recognition of the relative validity of natural law.

At the same time the Unity kept a close watch on the separation of temporal and spiritual power, defending this principle in their dealings with temporal authorities, with representatives of the nobility, whose involvement and role in the church had increased significantly, and with representatives of the European Reformation, who were to a greater or lesser extent taking on the *[p. 296]* role of the temporal authority in the organisation of the church. The Brethren were aware of the exacting nature of their teaching, but were so convinced of its correctness and vital importance for the witness and ministry of the church that they were unwilling to compromise on it.

The teaching of the Brethren on property and economics remained largely unchanged. However, there was an increasing tension between teaching and practice as the difference between them became ever greater. For the Brethren, work and trade were a way of creating in practice a community of mutual services and assistance, which substantially limited the ways in which private property could be acquired and used. Due to the rapid development in the financial system it was necessary to re-assess the relationship to money, and investing money and saving it at interest were accepted. A significant change took place in the attitude to marriage and the family. The remnants of the mediaeval ascetic ideals in personal life, which Luke had still advocated, were now largely abandoned under the influence of the European Reformation. A clearly positive attitude to marriage and a high regard for family life now emerged.

During this period in the middle of the sixteenth century, the Brethren repeatedly tried to finally achieve a recognised position in society, to acquire the status of a mainstream Christian church. They fumbled their way towards this goal, alternately making use of the influence of the nobility and rejecting it, sometimes trying to act in conjunction with the Czech Lutherans and sometimes insisting on following their own road. Although they were attracted by the vision of a peaceful, legally secure existence for their community, they did not want to sacrifice their own particular features in order to achieve this. Indeed, they continually defended and guarded these features, unaware that they could have no place in the status of a church that was recognised by society. In particular, the Brethren's insistence on the freedom of belief and freedom of conscience of the individual was a stumbling-block in their relations with the temporal authorities, as it made it practically impossible for the authorities to maintain control over the church, and, to say the least, made it complicated for the authorities to use it for political aims.

And the conception of the internal life of the Brethren's congregations was not suited for the situation of a church recognised by society, either. This could be seen particularly well where the *[p. 297]* Unity had been successful in enjoying for a long time a quiet and free life and the support of the local nobility. In such cases complaints proliferated about a decay in morals among both lay members and priests, a relaxation in discipline and order, a failure to observe the basic principles of the Brethren, and adapting to the standards of the rest of society. It appeared that the demanding model of critical solidarity with the world that the Unity had adopted under Luke and which it still tried to follow had virtually been designed for the situation of a tolerated minority whose position was never completely secure and which lived in a state of constant tension and vigilance, because the difference between it and the rest of society was clear for both sides, and even with the best of intentions there were many points of conflict.

A critical attitude to the prevalent way of life in society and a cautious detachment, approaches that had proved their worth and confirmed their legitimacy in situations of conflict and tension, seemingly became unnecessary in times of peace and prosperity, and indeed appeared to restrict the expansion of the Unity. A strict observance of rules of life that sometimes verged on the moralistic disturbed good relations with the rest of society. This development indicated that, paradoxically, acquiring the status of a recognised church might considerably complicate matters for the Unity.

The problem that the first generation of the Unity had wrestled with, whether to radically reject the culture of society and to take a stand against it, or else to attempt to find a way of accepting this culture in a differentiated and critical way and to influence it in a Christian manner, had been definitively resolved in favour of the latter approach during the era of Brother Luke. But subsequent developments demonstrated the extreme difficulty of putting such an approach into practice and above all of maintaining it. External pressure on a community that is characterised by its independence and critical attitude towards society is only to be expected. A community like this is a difficult, disturbing and irritating element in the structure of society.

But a more serious problem is the internal pressure, arising out of the inconvenient and troublesome demands made on the personal life of every Christian. The acceptance of such exacting demands had been supported in the first generation of the Unity by its strong eschatological orientation, concentration on the coming Kingdom of God, and its clear-cut separation from temporal society, and then subsequently by at least clear reservations about *[p. 298]* that society. But once the Brethren accepted the principle that Christians could and should fulfil their mission in the world and in cooperation with others for the common good, the question then logically arose as to whether Christians could not live responsibly in a less demanding way. As soon as the Brethren formed a union with the Lutheran-oriented Utraquists by means of the Bohemian Confession, it was patently obvious that their concept of a good Christian life and of the form of social existence of a Christian community were destined to undergo a severe trial.

### 4. The Church as a Part of Society? (1575–1618)

In the year 1575 the Unity of Brethren entered a period of a kind of semi-legal existence under the protection of the Bohemian Confession, the rather vague acceptance of which by the Emperor was open to contradictory interpretations. It meant that on the domains of amicably inclined and tolerant nobility, the Brethren enjoyed not only peace, but also the privileges of a church that was recognised and protected by the temporal authorities. To a certain extent, the Unity became a part of society in Bohemia and Moravia, in the same way as had already occurred in Poland. Church buildings and parishes were entrusted to its care, so that its numbers increased, but internally it was subject to concerns about its changing character. Not only the union in a joint confession, but also the blurring of the differences between the Brethren and Utraquist communities, led to a feeling of being under threat and the fear of being swallowed up by the larger Lutheran church. This gave rise to repeated displays of distrust of coming too close to the Utraquists and indeed resistance to this.

The trend towards placing greater emphasis in the Unity on the rules of moral life, which had been evident from the middle of the century, now became more marked. This led to an increasingly more carefully defined moralistic approach, but regrettably this was usually not accompanied by a more profound reflection and fundamental theological justification of the issues involved. In relations with temporal authorities the new situation obliged the church to be represented in some matters by its aristocratic members or adherents. This had in practice already been the case for a long time, but it was not until the Confession of the Breth- *[p. 299]* ren in 1607 that this situation became reflected in official teaching with the acceptance of the right and duty of temporal authorities to ensure that good order was maintained in the church, not only in external matters, but also as regards sound teaching. This rather more Calvinist emphasis was a major step away from the traditional conception of the relations between the church and temporal authorities held by the Brethren. Admittedly, it was not generally dominant in the Unity in the period before the Battle of the White Mountain, but it is a clear indication of how the connection between church and society was gradually becoming more and more accepted among the Brethren. This was also reflected in their attitude to the defence of the liberties and rights of society, where the Brethren accepted the teaching of the just war and to a certain extent the right to resistance against an unjust nobility.

The increasing involvement of the Unity in society was also confirmed by the manner of its theological argumentation, which increasingly referred to natural law, and, after 1609, took into account the current norms of the law of the land. It is true that the Brethren did not abandon the principle of the unconditional validity of the witness of Scripture, but they looked for evidence that the society in which they lived was, or at least wanted to be, in conformity with Scriptural norms. Here we can detect the extremely unobtrusive beginnings of the return of the mediaeval ideal of Christendom, a society administered in a Christian way, whose failings and shortcomings are not due to any basic contradiction between the demands of the Gospel and human power structures, but simply to the sins of individuals. From this point of view, human society, in spite of being profoundly corrupted, was still essentially good. Emphases like these were signs of the shift that was taking place in the Brethren's conception of the social role and social responsibility of the church, but they were only individual manifestations and were not integrated into a broader and properly thought out whole. The creation of such a whole through the influence of historical events did not take place, and thus the first and the last coherent form taken by the Brethren's social teaching basically remains the conception of Luke. In spite of the many modifications and discussions it was subject to, the conception of critical responsibility for the world that underlay Luke's approach was never seriously questioned by the Brethren, and right until the end they always returned to it eventually.

## CONCLUSION

*[p. 300]*

The Unity of Brethren was the upholder of the social message of the Gospel in the Czech milieu for over a hundred years. It had its origins in the ideas of the radical wing of the Hussite movement and Taborite theology, and preserved that legacy right through to the start of the 17th century. In the main emphases of its teaching, such as the separation of religion from the structures of power and the freedom of conscience of the individual, it pioneered the path down which society was to go much later on, and on which many communities are still facing a painful struggle today. From the central idea underlying its social teaching, that life is the service of God in one's neighbours, the Brethren drew practical conclusions that led them to the creation of many inspirational examples of Christian social responsibility in various professions and social classes. At the moment when it finally seemed that this heritage was falling silent, drowned out by the lure of social acceptance of the church of the Brethren, the Thirty Years' War put an end to everything.

The development of the social teaching of the Czech Brethren thus came to a sudden and violent close, but perhaps for this very reason its legacy acquired greater sharpness and urgency. In its search for a socially responsible form of Christian life, shaped by the prophetic demands of God's Word, while at the same time being sensitive to the needs of others and their everyday worries, the teaching of the Brethren constitutes a unique chapter not only in the history of Czech Christianity, but also within the context of the European Reformation.
