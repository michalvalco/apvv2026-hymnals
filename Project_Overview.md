## Thesis (working formulation)

The transfer of hymns from Czech Reformation sources (particularly Unity of Brethren kancionáls) into Slovak Lutheran worship through the Cithara Sanctorum involved systematic theological adaptation — a "Lutheranisation" — that reveals both the depth of dogmatic difference between these confessions and the surprising areas of convergence that linguistic proximity and shared anti-Catholic identity made possible. The *lex orandi, lex credendi* lens exposes tensions that normative confessional documents papered over, particularly in Eucharistic theology and ecclesiology.

---

## Scope and Approach (decided 2026-05-10 — Option B: directional reception)

This chapter undertakes a dogmatic comparison of three confessional currents — Augsburg Lutheranism, Old and Neo-Utraquism, and the Unity of Brethren — as these doctrinal positions surface in liturgical and hymnological practice between the consolidation of the Confessio Augustana (1530) and the late phase of Slovak Lutheran orthodoxy in the eighteenth century. The methodological wager of the chapter is that liturgical use and hymnic text constitute a more candid record of confessional theology than the apologetic genres of normative documents alone: what congregations sang and prayed reveals what they actually believed, often more faithfully than what their representatives signed. The methodology is enacted in David R. Holeton's "Evolution of Utraquist Liturgy" (*Studia Liturgica* 25/1, 1995) and the nine BRRP essays that built on it; this chapter extends Holeton's *lex orandi, lex credendi* approach into the Slovak Lutheran reception field that he never lived to complete.

That methodological commitment carries an evidentiary consequence. The *lived theology* record between these three confessional families is not symmetric. It is shaped by a directional flow of textual material — Czech Reformation sources moving into Slovak Lutheran reception, and there undergoing dogmatic re-framing. The Cithara Sanctorum (Tranovský, 1636+) is the most consequential instance of this reception, but not the only one: the Bardejov Catechism (1556) modifies Luther's Small Catechism within a Slovak humanist register, and Leonard Stöckel's annotations on Melanchthon's *Loci Communes* (1561) construct a Wittenberg–Bardejov Lutheran identity that defines itself partly against Czech Reformation alternatives. The doctrinal asymmetry that this directional flow produces is not an artefact of source availability — it is a feature of the historical phenomenon itself.

Accordingly, the chapter pursues comparison **through reception rather than parallel exposition**. The four dogmatic loci — Eucharistic theology, ecclesiology, soteriology and ethics, and scripture–tradition relations — are read primarily through Slovak Lutheran sources adapting Czech material. Where Utraquist or Brethren positions appear, they appear as positions being received, modified, or rejected. The chapter does not attempt parity of dogmatic-profile coverage between the three traditions, and it does not rehearse the full theological identities of Utraquism or the Unity of Brethren independently of this reception. Those independent profiles exist in the secondary literature (notably Holeton on Utraquism; Crews, Atwood, and Halama on the Brethren; Daniel and Csepregi on the Slovak Lutheran context) and are presupposed rather than reconstructed here. The chapter's contribution is to take seriously the proposition that in Slovak Lutheran territory between the late sixteenth and the late eighteenth century, the dogmatic difference between confessions was negotiated more durably in the liturgical and hymnological text than in the confessional document — and that this negotiation has its own genre conventions, its own thresholds of fidelity and divergence, and its own intelligible logic.

The Tier 1 scholar surveys (2026-05-10) confirmed two negative facts that secure this contribution-niche: no Anglophone monograph on Czech-Reformation hymn material received in Slovak Lutheran usage exists (Wilkinson 2003 dissertation is the closest match, at thesis-overview pitch), and no Anglophone Utraquist–Lutheran comparative-Eucharistic monograph exists (Burnett 2011 covers the Hussite→Karlstadt→Zwingli/Bullinger track, not the Hussite→Wittenberg→Slovak track).
